# The Hour of His Judgment Is Come

## Question
What does hoti elthen he hora tes kriseos autou ("for the hour of his judgment is come") mean? What is this judgment — the cross, the second coming, the investigative/pre-advent judgment, or something else? Why does the aorist elthen present it as an accomplished fact? How does krisis (G2920) differ from katakrima (G2631)? Trace krisis through the LXX to Daniel 7's dina. How does Daniel 7:22 establish this judgment is FOR believers? How can judgment be "good news"? Does the pre-advent judgment undermine assurance?

## Summary Answer
The judgment announced in Revelation 14:7 is a divine evaluation process (krisis, G2920), not a condemnation (katakrima, G2631). It is directly connected to Daniel 7's heavenly court scene through a vocabulary chain — Aramaic dina to LXX kriterion/krima to NT krisis — and Daniel 7:22 establishes that this judgment is rendered FOR the saints' benefit, not against them. The prophetic aorist elthen presents the judgment as an accomplished certainty within John's visionary perspective, marking the shift from anticipation ("about to judge") to announcement ("judgment IS come"). Judgment functions as gospel content because it vindicates believers, reverses persecution, and publicly confirms what grace has already done — believers face it with boldness (1 John 4:17), not terror, because Christ intercedes as their advocate (Rom 8:34, 1 John 2:1).

## Key Verses

> **The Crux Text (Revelation 14:7)** Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

> **No Condemnation (Romans 8:1)** There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus, who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit.

> **Judgment FOR the Saints (Daniel 7:22)** Until the Ancient of days came, and judgment was given to the saints of the most High; and the time came that the saints possessed the kingdom.

> **Boldness in Judgment (1 John 4:17)** Herein is our love made perfect, that we may have boldness in the day of judgment: because as he is, so are we in this world.

> **Judgment IS Gospel (Romans 2:16)** In the day when God shall judge the secrets of men by Jesus Christ according to my gospel.

> **Who Condemns? (Romans 8:33-34)** Who shall lay any thing to the charge of God's elect? It is God that justifieth. Who is he that condemneth? It is Christ that died, yea rather, that is risen again, who is even at the right hand of God, who also maketh intercession for us.

> **Delivered by the Book (Daniel 12:1)** And at that time shall Michael stand up, the great prince which standeth for the children of thy people: and there shall be a time of trouble, such as never was since there was a nation even to that same time: and at that time thy people shall be delivered, every one that shall be found written in the book.

> **The Court Scene (Daniel 7:9-10)** I beheld till the thrones were cast down, and the Ancient of days did sit, whose garment was white as snow, and the hair of his head like the pure wool: his throne was like the fiery flame, and his wheels as burning fire. A fiery stream issued and came forth from before him: thousand thousands ministered unto him, and ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him: the judgment was set, and the books were opened.

## Analysis

### The krin- Word Family: Greek Has Precise Vocabulary for Judgment

The first and most important finding is that Greek does not have just one word for "judgment." It has an entire family of words from the krin- root, and each one carries a distinct meaning.

Krisis (G2920) appears 47 times in the NT. It denotes the judicial process — the act of evaluating, separating, and rendering judgment. It is the word used in Revelation 14:7 (kriseos, genitive), John 5:22 (krisin, accusative), John 5:24 (krisin), 1 John 4:17 (kriseos), and Hebrews 9:27 (krisis). When the first angel announces "the hour of his judgment," he uses the process word.

Katakrima (G2631) appears only 3 times in the entire NT — Romans 5:16, 5:18, and 8:1. It denotes the adverse verdict together with its execution. BDAG defines it as "the adjudication of punishment" — not just the sentence, but the sentence plus the penalty. It is the condemnation word.

This distinction is not an academic technicality. It is the key that unlocks the entire study. Romans 8:1 says there is no katakrima for those in Christ Jesus. It does NOT say there is no krisis. Paul had both words available. He chose katakrima. The result: believers are exempt from the condemnatory outcome, but they are not exempt from the evaluative process. Second Corinthians 5:10 confirms this: "We must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ; that every one may receive the things done in his body" — all believers, evaluated.

The wider family reinforces the picture. Krima (G2917, 27 NT occurrences) stresses the judgment decision or result — it is the LXX term for Daniel 7:22's verdict given to the saints. Krino (G2919, 132 occurrences) is the root verb, covering the full range from mental evaluation to divine sentencing. Krites (G2923, 17 occurrences) is the judge — the person who exercises krisis. Kriterion (G2922, 3 occurrences) is the tribunal itself — the LXX term for Daniel 7:10 and 7:26, "the court sat." Anakrino (G350, 18 occurrences) adds the investigative dimension — to examine, scrutinize, investigate — confirming that judicial investigation is built into the krin- word group. On the condemnation side, katakrino (G2632, 22 occurrences) is the verb "to condemn," and katakrisis (G2633, 2 occurrences) is the act of condemning.

Every member of this family serves a distinct function. The angel in Revelation 14:7 chose krisis — the process. Paul in Romans 8:1 negated katakrima — the adverse outcome. Together they tell a coherent story: a judgment process is coming; those in Christ face it without condemnation.

### The Daniel 7 Vocabulary Chain: From Aramaic dina to Greek krisis

Revelation 14:7 does not announce judgment in a vacuum. Its krisis is anchored in the OT's most detailed court scene: Daniel 7:9-10, 22, 26-27.

The Aramaic word for judgment in Daniel 7 is dina (A1780), which appears 5 times in the OT, all in this chapter. In verse 10, the phrase dina yetib means "the court took its seat" — dina is the court/judgment, yetib (A3488) is the Aramaic verb "to sit." In verse 22, dina yehib means "judgment was given." In verse 26, dina yittib means "the court shall sit." The LXX translates these with two krin- family members: kriterion (G2922, "tribunal") in verses 10 and 26, and krima (G2917, "judgment/decision") in verse 22.

The LXX does not use krisis directly in Daniel 7. But it uses kriterion and krima — cognates of krisis from the same krin- root. This is the vocabulary chain: Aramaic dina feeds into Greek kriterion and krima through the LXX, and these connect to krisis in the NT. When the first angel announces the hora tes kriseos ("hour of judgment"), the Greek carries the weight of Daniel 7's entire court scene.

A second channel reinforces this connection. The Hebrew covenant lawsuit term riyb (H7378 as verb, 79 occurrences; H7379 as noun, 59 occurrences) is the formal legal tradition underlying the three-part prophetic pattern identified in study 2 (3am-02). The LXX renders the noun riyb as krisis 29 times. This means krisis in Revelation 14:7 carries not only the Daniel 7 court scene but also the entire OT covenant lawsuit tradition — God bringing His case before the nations, calling witnesses, and vindicating His people.

### Daniel 7:22: The Judgment Is FOR Believers

The most important single verse for answering the study question is Daniel 7:22. The Aramaic reads: dina yehib leqaddishe elyonin — "judgment was given to the saints of the Most High." The parsing confirms: yehib is the peil (passive) perfect of yehab ("give"); the preposition le means "to" or "for." When le governs an indirect object after yehab, it is naturally benefactive — "given in favor of" or "given for the benefit of."

This benefactive reading is confirmed by the parallel in Daniel 7:27, where the kingdom is given le ("to") the people of the saints — clearly "for their benefit." The same construction, the same preposition, the same meaning. The judgment of verse 22 and the kingdom of verse 27 are both given FOR the saints.

The context makes this even clearer. Verse 21 describes the horn making war against the saints and prevailing. Verse 22 reverses this: the Ancient of Days comes, judgment is given FOR the saints, and they possess the kingdom. The judgment is the mechanism that ends the persecution. It is a verdict of vindication — not a second prosecution of the people already being persecuted.

Three judgment references in Daniel 7 form a narrative arc. Verse 10: the court sits and the books are opened — investigation. Verse 22: judgment is given for the saints — favorable verdict. Verses 26-27: the court sits again, dominion is removed from the horn, and the kingdom is given to the saints — execution and transfer. This three-phase structure (investigation, verdict, execution) mirrors the biblical pattern of investigation before execution found in Genesis 3:8-13, Genesis 18:20-21, and Matthew 22:11-14.

The sequence is critical for the pre-advent judgment concept. In Daniel 7, the court sits (vv.9-10) BEFORE the Son of Man approaches the Ancient of Days (vv.13-14) and BEFORE the kingdom is given (v.27). Judgment precedes the kingdom transfer. This is a pre-advent judgment concept derived directly from the text, independent of any date calculation. Ford's critique that the investigative judgment "lacks firm biblical warrant" targets the timing argument (1844 via Daniel 8:14), not the concept itself. The concept — judgment before kingdom transfer — is built into Daniel 7's structure.

### krisis vs. katakrima: Why the Distinction Matters for John 5:24

The most difficult text for the "judgment of believers" reading is John 5:24: "He that heareth my word, and believeth on him that sent me, hath everlasting life, and shall not come into condemnation [krisin]; but is passed from death unto life." The word is krisin — the same word as Revelation 14:7. Does this mean believers avoid all krisis?

The context resolves the apparent contradiction. In John 5:22, ALL krisis is given to the Son. In 5:27, the Son has authority to execute krisis because He is the Son of Man. In 5:29, those who have done evil come forth to "the resurrection of krisis" — condemnatory judgment. The word krisis appears four times in this discourse (vv.22, 24, 27, 29), and its specific sense shifts by context. In verse 24, the krisin being avoided is defined by what follows: "but is passed from death unto life." The metabebeken (perfect tense of metabaino) marks a permanent, completed transition from the death-realm to the life-realm. The krisis the believer avoids is the one that ends in death.

This is not a contradiction with Revelation 14:7. Revelation 14:7 announces the arrival of the evaluation process. John 5:24 promises that the evaluation will not end in death for the believer. The process happens; the adverse outcome does not. Romans 8:1 makes the same point with its more precise vocabulary: no katakrima (condemnation) for those in Christ. John 5:24 says the same thing using krisis in its condemnatory sense.

### Judgment as Gospel Content

One of the study's most striking findings is that judgment is not an addition to the gospel — it is integral gospel content. Three independent NT texts make this explicit.

First, Romans 2:16: "In the day when God shall judge the secrets of men by Jesus Christ according to my gospel." The prepositional phrase kata to euangelion mou ("according to my gospel") modifies the verb krinei ("judges"). Barnes, Meyer, and Cambridge Bible all confirm this: God's judging activity operates according to Paul's gospel. For Paul, judgment is "one of the great and prime articles" of the gospel itself.

Second, Luke 3:18: After recording John the Baptist's warnings about the axe at the root, the winnowing fork, and the unquenchable fire, Luke summarizes with euengelizeto (G2097, imperfect) — "he was gospeling the people." Luke labels John's entire judgment-laden message as gospel. This is not editorial softening; it is theological assessment.

Third, Revelation 14:6-7: The angel carries the euangelion aionion, and the content of that gospel — delivered through the legon participle connecting verse 7 to verse 6 — includes the judgment announcement. Study 4 (3am-04) confirmed this structural embedding: the judgment IS the gospel message.

Acts 17:30-31 provides the logical structure: Paul commands repentance (gospel call) BECAUSE God has appointed a day to judge (judgment as the basis for the gospel call). The pattern is consistent: judgment is the reason for repentance, the content of gospel preaching, and the mechanism of divine vindication.

### Judgment and Assurance: Five Grounds of Confidence

The hardest apologetic question for this study is whether the pre-advent judgment undermines assurance. Ford's critique carried weight precisely because many believers experienced the judgment as a threat rather than good news. The response must be honest: if the judgment creates fear rather than confidence, something has gone wrong — but the problem is in the understanding, not in the doctrine.

The NT provides at least five independent grounds of assurance that extend through judgment, not around it.

**First, God's explicit promises.** First John 5:13 uses oida (G1492) — to know with settled certainty: "That ye may know that ye have eternal life." The same author writes about boldness in judgment (4:17) and settled certainty of life (5:13) in the same letter. These coexist.

**Second, the Spirit's seal.** Ephesians 1:13-14 says believers are sealed (esphragisthete, G4972) with the Holy Spirit, who is the arrabon (G728) — earnest money, a deposit guaranteeing the inheritance. The Spirit is a guarantee that extends through any judgment period to the final redemption.

**Third, Christ's ongoing intercession.** Romans 8:34 uses the present tense entygchanei (G1793) — Christ "is making intercession" right now. Hebrews 7:25 adds that He "ever liveth to make intercession." The intercession is not a past event; it is an active, present, permanent ministry. Christ does not stop interceding during judgment. His intercession IS the mechanism by which believers pass through judgment safely.

**Fourth, the books testify for believers.** Malachi 3:16-17 describes a "book of remembrance" written FOR those who feared the LORD. This book records faithful service, not sins. Daniel 12:1 promises deliverance for everyone "found written in the book." Philippians 4:3 states that names are in the book of life (present tense). Revelation 3:5 gives an emphatic double negative: "I will absolutely NOT blot out his name." The judgment finds names already inscribed and confirms them.

**Fifth, Christ's completed work.** Romans 5:9-10 uses an a fortiori argument: "Much more then, being now justified by his blood, we shall be saved from wrath through him." If Christ's death justified believers while they were enemies, how much more will His life save them now? The completed work of Calvary is the foundation that no judgment can overturn.

Livingston captured the principle well: "The theological change necessary to remove any qualms about assurance is not changing the timing, duration or transparency of any judgement, but a change in basic understanding of salvation or free will." The problem is never the existence of judgment. The problem is misunderstanding grace.

### The Courtroom Pattern: Accusation, Advocacy, Vindication

The biblical judgment is not an abstract process. It has characters and a plot. Zechariah 3:1-5 reveals the pattern: Joshua the high priest stands before the angel of the LORD in filthy garments — real guilt. Ha-satan (H7854, "the adversary") stands at his right hand to accuse. But the LORD rebukes the accuser ("Is not this a brand plucked out of the fire?"), removes the filthy garments, and clothes Joshua in rich robes. The judgment has five movements: (1) real guilt acknowledged, (2) accusation by the adversary, (3) divine advocate intervenes, (4) cleansing, (5) new status conferred.

Romans 8:33-34 replays this with Pauline vocabulary. "Who shall lay charge?" (tis egkalesei, G1458 — formal legal prosecution). "Who condemns?" (tis ho katakrinon, G2632). The answer: God justifies (dikaioon, G1344, present tense — ongoing). Christ died, rose, sits at God's right hand, and makes intercession (entygchanei, G1793, present tense — active right now). The accuser may bring charges, but the defense is overwhelming: death, resurrection, enthronement, and ongoing advocacy.

Revelation 12:10 completes the arc: "the accuser [kategor, G2725] of our brethren is cast down." The prosecutor who stood at Joshua's right hand in Zechariah 3 is permanently disbarred. When the judgment of Revelation 14:7 arrives, the accuser has already been removed. The courtroom has a judge (the Son of Man from Daniel 7), a defense attorney (the parakletos of 1 John 2:1), books that include faithful service (the sepher zikkaron of Malachi 3:16), and no prosecutor.

This is why judgment can be good news. A trial where the judge is righteous (2 Tim 4:8), the defense attorney is Christ, the accuser is disbarred, and the verdict is predetermined for those in Christ (Dan 7:22) — that is not a threat. That is vindication.

### The Prophetic Aorist: Why elthen?

The verb elthen (G2064, V-2AAI-3S) is a second aorist active indicative of erchomai. In normal Greek prose, the aorist narrates past events. But in prophetic and apocalyptic literature, the aorist can present future events as accomplished facts — the "prophetic aorist." The grammar reference identifies this as a standard feature of apocalyptic proclamation.

Revelation uses this device repeatedly. In 14:8, the second angel says "Babylon is fallen, is fallen" — epesen epesen (G4098, same aorist morphology). In 11:15, the kingdoms "became" our Lord's — egeneto (G1096, aorist). In 6:17, "the great day of his wrath is come" — elthen again. Jude 14 quotes the Enochic tradition: "the Lord came [elthen] with ten thousands of his saints." In each case, a future event is narrated as accomplished to stress its certainty.

Study 3 (3am-03) identified the shift from anticipation to announcement. John the Baptist used mellei (Matt 3:7 — "wrath about to come"). Paul used mellei krinein (Acts 17:31 — "about to judge"). The first angel uses elthen — "IS come." The progression from "about to" to "IS" marks the transition point: the judgment is no longer anticipated; it is announced as arrived. The prophetic aorist does not tell us precisely when the judgment began, but it tells us the angel treats its arrival as certain and complete.

### The Vindication Declaration Progression

The judgment announcement in Revelation 14:7 does not stand alone. It begins a progression that runs through the rest of Revelation:

Revelation 14:7 — the announcement: "the hour of his judgment IS come."
Revelation 15:3-4 — character affirmation: "Just and true are thy ways... Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name?" — the same vocabulary as 14:7 (fear, glorify, judgments).
Revelation 16:7 — specific vindication: "True and righteous are thy judgments" — the altar speaks; the martyrs from 6:9-11 are being vindicated. Kriseis (G2920, plural) — the same root word.
Revelation 19:1-2 — completion: "True and righteous are his judgments: for he hath judged [ekrinen, G2919, aorist] the great whore." The judgment announced in 14:7 is completed in 19:2.

This progression moves from announcement to character affirmation to specific vindication to completion. It shows that the judgment of Revelation 14:7 is not a single event but a process that unfolds across the narrative. At each stage, the response is affirmation: God's judgments are true and righteous.

### Implications for the Three Angels' Messages

This study addresses the third element of the first angel's message. Studies 5 and 6 examined "fear God" and "give glory." This study examines the hoti clause that grounds both commands: BECAUSE the hour of judgment has come. The judgment provides the reason for the fear and the glory.

The hoti clause also integrates with the broader series structure. The three imperatives (fear, give glory, worship the Creator) are grounded in the judgment announcement (Rev 14:7d), and all four elements are embedded within the euangelion aionion (Rev 14:6). The sequence discovered across the series is: the gospel is proclaimed (v.6) → its content is three commands + a judgment announcement (v.7) → the commands make sense BECAUSE judgment has arrived.

This means the three angels' messages cannot be separated from the judgment. The judgment is not an appendix or a denominational distinctive — it is the reason clause. Remove the judgment, and the commands lose their grounding. Fear God — why? Because judgment has arrived. Give glory — why? Because the Judge is evaluating. Worship the Creator — why? Because the Creator has the right to judge what He made.

## Word Studies

**krisis (G2920)** — 47 NT occurrences. The judicial process of evaluation, separating, and rendering judgment. The word used in Revelation 14:7. Denotes process, not outcome.

**katakrima (G2631)** — 3 NT occurrences (Rom 5:16, 5:18, 8:1). The adverse verdict together with its execution. Condemnation. What Romans 8:1 negates for believers.

**krima (G2917)** — 27 NT occurrences. The judgment decision or result. The LXX term in Daniel 7:22 (to krima edoken). Bridges process and outcome.

**kriterion (G2922)** — 3 NT occurrences. The tribunal or court. The LXX term in Daniel 7:10 and 7:26 (kriterion ekathisen/kathisei).

**dina (A1780)** — 5 OT occurrences, all in Daniel 7. The Aramaic source for the entire krisis vocabulary chain.

**parrhesia (G3954)** — 31 NT occurrences. Boldness, confidence, freedom of speech before God. The answer to the assurance question: believers have parrhesia in the day of krisis (1 John 4:17).

**parakletos (G3875)** — 5 NT occurrences (all Johannine). Advocate, defense attorney. Christ as the one who represents believers in the heavenly court (1 John 2:1).

**entygchano (G1793)** — 5 NT occurrences. To intercede, make intercession. Present tense in Romans 8:34: Christ IS interceding right now.

**hora (G5610)** — 108 NT occurrences. Hour, appointed time. With the definite article in Revelation 14:7, it marks THE specific appointed hour, paralleling Jesus' "the hour is come" language (John 12:23, 13:1, 17:1).

**elthen / erchomai (G2064)** — 664 NT occurrences for the root verb. Elthen is the second aorist form. In Revelation 14:7, it functions as a prophetic aorist, presenting the judgment's arrival as an accomplished certainty.

## Difficult Passages

### John 5:24 — "Does not come into judgment"
This verse uses krisis — the same word as Revelation 14:7 — and says the believer "shall not come into" it. Taken in isolation, this could mean believers face no judgment at all. The resolution is contextual: the phrase is followed by "but is passed from death unto life," and the perfect tense metabebeken confirms a permanent transition from the death-realm. The krisis avoided is the condemnatory outcome, not the evaluative process. Romans 8:1 makes the same point with more precise vocabulary (katakrima instead of krisis). Still, the tension between "does not come into krisis" (John 5:24) and "the hour of krisis has come" (Rev 14:7) is real, and the resolution requires reading krisis contextually rather than as a flat synonym for condemnation in every occurrence.

### Hebrews 10:26-30 — Fearful expectation of judgment for the apostate
For the willful apostate, judgment IS fearful: "a certain fearful looking for of judgment and fiery indignation." The positive outcomes — boldness, vindication, reward — apply to those who remain in Christ. Those who "trample the Son of God" face a different reality. This means the assurance passages are not unconditional promises to all people regardless of their relationship to God. They address present believers — people who ARE hearing, believing, abiding. The pre-advent judgment does not threaten genuine faith; it threatens presumption. This is a needed corrective: the same judgment that vindicates the faithful exposes the counterfeit.

### The timing question — Does the text specify when?
Revelation 14:7 announces that the hour of judgment HAS arrived (prophetic aorist). Daniel 7's sequence requires judgment before kingdom transfer. These establish the concept of a pre-advent judgment on purely textual grounds. But the specific timing — 1844, derived from Daniel 8:14 via the year-day principle and the 457 BC starting date — involves an additional interpretive chain that this study does not address. Ford's critique is strongest when targeting this chain of reasoning; it is weakest when targeting the concept itself, since Daniel 7's structure plainly places judgment before the kingdom.

### The scope of autou — whose judgment?
The genitive pronoun autou ("of him") in Revelation 14:7 can be read as subjective (God's act of judging) or objective (judgment of God's character). Both have merit. The immediate context favors the subjective reading: the Creator who made heaven and earth judges. But the vindication progression through Revelation (15:3-4, 16:7, 19:1-2) suggests an objective dimension: God's own character is vindicated through the transparency of the judgment. These readings may not be mutually exclusive. The judgment is God's act AND a vindication of God's character before the universe.

## Conclusion

The first angel's judgment announcement is good news because of what it is, who conducts it, and how it ends.

What it is: krisis — an evaluation, not a condemnation. The Greek vocabulary is precise, and the angel chose the process word. Romans 8:1 negates katakrima for those in Christ, but krisis continues. The distinction is between being examined and being condemned. Believers face the first; they are exempt from the second.

Who conducts it: the Son of Man from Daniel 7, who received all judgment from the Father (John 5:22) and who serves as both judge and advocate. The courtroom has a righteous judge (2 Tim 4:8), a defense attorney who is Christ Himself (1 John 2:1), and a disbarred accuser (Rev 12:10). The books include not only deeds but a book of remembrance written FOR those who feared the LORD (Mal 3:16-17). The outcome is predetermined for those in Christ: judgment given FOR the saints (Dan 7:22), deliverance for those found written in the book (Dan 12:1).

How it ends: with vindication, kingdom transfer, and reward. Daniel 7:26-27 shows the dominion removed from the oppressor and given to the saints permanently. Second Timothy 4:8 shows the righteous judge awarding a crown. First Corinthians 3:15 shows that even in the worst case — all works burned — the person is still saved. The judgment refines but does not destroy the believer.

The pre-advent judgment does not undermine assurance. Five independent grounds — God's promises (1 John 5:13), the Spirit's seal (Eph 1:13-14), Christ's intercession (Rom 8:34, Heb 7:25), the books of faithful service (Mal 3:16), and Christ's completed work (Rom 5:9-10) — all extend through judgment, not around it. Assurance comes from understanding grace, not from removing judgment.

For the three angels' messages, this means the judgment announcement is not a threat appended to the gospel but the reason for the gospel commands. Fear God — because the righteous Judge has opened court. Give glory — because the verdict depends on your relationship to Christ, not on your performance. Worship the Creator — because the one who made you has the right to evaluate your response to His grace. And the response to all of this, for the believer, is not terror but parrhesia — boldness in the day of judgment (1 John 4:17), grounded in the finished work and ongoing intercession of the one who holds all krisis in His hands (John 5:22).
