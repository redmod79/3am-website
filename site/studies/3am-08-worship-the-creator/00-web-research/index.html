    Web Research Findings¶

    Study Question¶

    Why does the first angel call for worship of the Creator using the specific formula "him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters"? Is this a direct quotation of Exodus 20:11? Conduct a verse-by-verse verbal comparison of Rev 14:7 and Exo 20:11 through the LXX. Compare with other creation-worship texts (Neh 9:6, Acts 4:24, Acts 14:15, Psa 146:6). In a passage concluding with "the commandments of God" (Rev 14:12), how does the creation formula's strongest link to the one commandment that memorializes creation establish a Sabbath connection? Address the objection that "worship him that made" is generic. How does the creation-to-new-creation bookend (Gen 2:2-3 to Isa 66:22-23) frame the Sabbath as permanent? How does Hebrews 4:9 (sabbatismos remaining) connect?

    Jon Paulien — "The First Angel and the Fourth Commandment" / Blog Series on Revelation 14 (Andrews University)¶

    URL: https://revelation-armageddon.com/tag/sabbath-in-revelation/ Key claims: - Identifies four identical terms in the same order between Rev 14:7 and Exo 20:11: "made," "heaven," "earth," "sea" - Uses three-pronged methodology: (1) verbal parallels (shared terminology), (2) thematic parallels (salvation, judgment, creation in Rev 14:6-7 mapped to the First Table of the Decalogue), (3) structural analysis (Ten Commandments frame Rev 12-14 with "a narrowing of focus" toward the fourth commandment) - Concludes: "The cumulative evidence is so strong that an interpreter could conclude that there is no direct allusion to the Old Testament in Revelation that is more certain than the allusion to the fourth commandment in Rev. 14:7" - States: "the final call of God to the world is in the context of the fourth commandment" - Argues the beasts of Rev 13 systematically counterfeit the First Table of the Ten Commandments (commandments 1-4), making the first angel's response a specific counter to each counterfeit Greek analysis: Notes the shared vocabulary but focuses primarily on thematic/structural methodology rather than word-level Greek parsing Relevance: Central scholarly voice arguing for the Rev 14:7-to-Exo 20:11 allusion. His claim about allusion certainty is the strongest version of this argument in the literature. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Jon Paulien — "Revelation 13-14 and the Ten Commandments" (Blog, 2018)¶

    URL: https://revelation-armageddon.com/2018/05/revelation-13-14-ten-commandments-fourteen-6/ Key claims: - The beasts' calls to worship (Rev 13:4, 8, 12, 15) are counterfeits of the First Table - First Commandment: beast demands worship (13:4, 8) - Second Commandment: land beast sets up an image to be worshipped (13:15) - Third Commandment: beast excels in blasphemy (13:6) - Fourth Commandment: the seal of God "contains the name of the ruler (Yahweh), the territory He rules over, and the basis for God's rule" — contrasted with the mark of the beast (13:16-17) - Calls this a "war of words" where "the unholy trinity seeks through deception and intimidation to shake the loyalty of God's people" Relevance: Establishes the structural argument that Rev 13-14 systematically engages all four worship commandments, making the Sabbath connection part of a larger pattern rather than an isolated proof-text. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Ekkehardt Mueller — "Creation in the Book of Revelation" (JATS 30/1, 2019)¶

    URL: https://digitalcommons.andrews.edu/jats/vol30/iss1/10/ Key claims: - The fourfold creation description in Rev 14:7 ("heaven, earth, sea, springs of water") parallels Exodus 20:11 - Rev 14:7 uses poieo (to make/do) rather than ktizo (to create): "By using poieo instead of ktizo, the connection of Revelation 14:7 to Genesis 1 and 2 is even more clearly established, even if it is through Exodus 20:11" - Concludes: "Those who live on earth in the last days need to acknowledge the Creator by respecting Him and His will and by recovering the true Sabbath that He has instituted at creation" Greek analysis: The distinction between poieo and ktizo is significant. Rev 10:6 uses ktizo for creation; Rev 14:7 deliberately switches to poieo, which is the verb used in Gen 1-2 (LXX) and Exo 20:11 (LXX: epoiesen). This verb choice strengthens the specific allusion to the fourth commandment rather than generic creation theology. Relevance: Provides the key Greek verb analysis distinguishing Rev 14:7's creation formula from other Revelation creation references. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — especially the poieo vs. ktizo distinction in Revelation

    John T. Baldwin — "Creation and Flood Implications of the First Angel's Message in Revelation 14:7" (Ministry Magazine, May 2006)¶

    URL: https://www.ministrymagazine.org/archive/2006/05/creation-and-flood-implications-of-the-first-angels-message-in-revelation-147.html Key claims: - Cites Paulien's allusion thesis as established scholarly consensus - Highlights a critical deviation: while Psa 146:6, Acts 4:24, and Acts 14:15 all conclude with the expected Exodus phrase "and all that is in them," Rev 14:7 substitutes "fountains of waters" (pegas hydaton) instead — this break from pattern signals intentional theological meaning - Cites David Aune (Word Biblical Commentary): "fountains" (pegas) refers to "natural water sources flowing from below ground" - Notes the Greek word pegas appears in both Rev 14:7 and the LXX rendering of Gen 7:11 ("fountains of the deep"), suggesting an implicit Flood reference alongside the Creation reference - The substitution of "fountains of waters" for "all that is in them" makes Rev 14:7 both a Creation AND Flood allusion Greek analysis: pegas hydaton replacing panta ta en autois is the distinctive marker — all other creation-formula texts use the expected Exodus completion phrase Relevance: Critical for showing that Rev 14:7 is NOT simply repeating generic creation language. The deliberate substitution of "fountains of waters" distinguishes it from the parallel texts and adds a layer of meaning. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture — check whether pegas appears in LXX Gen 7:11

    Ranko Stefanovic — Revelation of Jesus Christ: Commentary on the Book of Revelation (Andrews University Press, 2002/2009)¶

    URL: https://revelation-armageddon.com/2019/03/ranko-stefanovic-editorial-changes-lesson-manuscript-rev-14/ Key claims: - "The call to 'worship Him who made heaven and earth, the sea and springs of water' is a verbal allusion to the fourth commandment of the Decalogue (Exod. 20:11)" - "This shows that the call to worship God the Creator is given in the context of the Sabbath observance" - Comparing Exo 20:11 and Deut 5:15 shows the Sabbath functions as memorial of both creation and deliverance - The first angel's message calls people to "return to worshipping the true Creator God" Relevance: Major Revelation commentary supporting the allusion thesis without detailed Greek parsing — relies on thematic and verbal argument. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Andrew T. Lincoln — "Sabbath, Rest and Eschatology in the New Testament" (in From Sabbath to Lord's Day, 1982)¶

    URL: Referenced in https://lifehopeandtruth.com/bible/10-commandments/sabbath/sabbatismos/ Key claims: - Examines sabbatismos usage in extant Greek literature: Plutarch (De Superstitione 3), Justin (Dialogue with Trypho 23:3), Epiphanius (Adversus Haereses 30:2:2), Martyrium Petri et Pauli 1, Apostolic Constitutions 2:36:2 - Concludes: "in each of these places the term denotes the observance or celebration of the Sabbath" - This corresponds to the LXX usage of the cognate verb sabbatizo, which also refers to Sabbath observance - The writer of Hebrews is saying that "since the time of Joshua, an observance of Sabbath rest has been outstanding" Greek analysis: sabbatismos is formed from the verb sabbatizo ("to keep/observe/celebrate the Sabbath"). Every extant use in Greek literature denotes actual Sabbath observance, not generic spiritual rest. This is significant because Lincoln himself argues AGAINST Sabbath-keeping in his broader thesis — yet his own linguistic analysis confirms the word means Sabbath observance. Relevance: Critical for the Heb 4:9 section. Lincoln is an anti-Sabbatarian scholar whose own linguistic findings support the Sabbath meaning of sabbatismos — this makes his data particularly strong as a concession. Status: Lead to verify — confirm Lincoln's actual position and whether he acknowledges the tension

    LXX Greek Verbal Comparison Data¶

    Revelation 14:7 (Greek NT)¶

    καὶ προσκυνήσατε τῷ ποιήσαντι τὸν οὐρανὸν καὶ τὴν γῆν καὶ θάλασσαν καὶ πηγὰς ὑδάτων - Verb: ποιήσαντι (poiesanti) — aorist active participle of poieo, dative masculine singular - Objects: ton ouranon (the heaven) + ten gen (the earth) + thalassan (sea) + pegas hydaton (springs of waters)

    Exodus 20:11 (LXX)¶

    ἐν γὰρ ἓξ ἡμέραις ἐποίησε Κύριος τὸν οὐρανὸν καὶ τὴν γῆν καὶ τὴν θάλασσαν καὶ πάντα τὰ ἐν αὐτοῖς - Verb: ἐποίησε (epoiese) — aorist active indicative of poieo - Objects: ton ouranon (the heaven) + ten gen (the earth) + ten thalassan (the sea) + panta ta en autois (all things in them)

    Verbal Overlap Summary¶

    Element Exo 20:11 LXX Rev 14:7 Match?
    Verb root poieo (epoiese) poieo (poiesanti) YES — same root, different form
    Heaven ton ouranon ton ouranon EXACT
    Earth ten gen ten gen EXACT
    Sea ten thalassan thalassan YES — same noun, Rev drops article
    Fourth element panta ta en autois pegas hydaton DIFFERENT — "all in them" vs. "springs of waters"

    Comparison Texts (from search data)¶

    • Psa 146:6 (LXX 145:6): ton poiesanta ton ouranon kai ten gen ten thalassan — ends with expected "kai panta ta en autois"
    • Acts 4:24: ton poiesanta ton ouranon kai ten gen kai ten thalassan kai panta ta en autois — follows Exo 20:11 exactly
    • Acts 14:15: same formula, concludes with "kai panta ta en autois"
    • Neh 9:6: expands the formula to include "heavens of heavens with all their host" plus earth, seas, and giving life to all — the most expansive version

    Key Finding¶

    All other creation-formula texts (Psa 146:6, Acts 4:24, Acts 14:15) conclude with "and all that is in them" (panta ta en autois), faithfully reproducing Exo 20:11. Rev 14:7 alone substitutes "springs of waters" (pegas hydaton). This deliberate departure from the standard formula while retaining the three core elements (heaven, earth, sea) signals that Rev 14:7 is not merely repeating generic creation theology — it is consciously modifying the Exodus formula for theological purpose.

    Summary of Scholarly Landscape¶

    The scholarly landscape on Rev 14:7 and its creation formula divides broadly along confessional lines, though with significant nuance within each camp.

    The allusion thesis — that Rev 14:7 deliberately echoes Exo 20:11 and therefore embeds a Sabbath reference — is most fully argued by Jon Paulien (Andrews University), supported by Ekkehardt Mueller (Biblical Research Institute), Ranko Stefanovic (Andrews), and John T. Baldwin (Andrews). Paulien's three-pronged methodology (verbal, thematic, structural) is the most rigorous version of this argument. Mueller adds the important observation that Rev 14:7 uses poieo rather than ktizo, aligning it with the LXX creation vocabulary of Gen 1-2 and Exo 20:11. Baldwin contributes the insight that Rev 14:7's substitution of "fountains of waters" for "all that is in them" distinguishes it from all parallel creation-formula texts.

    The generic-creation objection — that "worship him that made" simply invokes God as Creator without Sabbath implications — is implicit in many mainstream commentaries (Aune, Beale, Mounce) that discuss the passage without mentioning the Sabbath connection. The preterist school (Revelation Revolution) treats the creation language as an appeal to divine authority during the Jewish War. Colleen Tinker (former Adventist) argues that "commandments of God" in Rev 14:12 means the teachings of Jesus rather than the Ten Commandments, attempting to sever the passage from Decalogue theology entirely. However, her nomos/entole distinction faces the counter-example of Luke 23:56 where entole clearly refers to the Sabbath commandment.

    On Hebrews 4:9, the sabbatismos debate is remarkably clear linguistically — Andrew Lincoln, himself an anti-Sabbatarian, concedes that every extant use of the word denotes Sabbath observance — but theologically contested. William Lane (WBC) translates "Sabbath celebration" and sees festive worship. The GCI article and mainstream evangelical commentators (Bruce, Morris) argue for spiritual rest as the primary referent. The Adventist mediating position (Page) holds that the Sabbath is "the God-given symbol and sample of the rest of grace."

    On the creation-to-new-creation bookend, even Jon Paulien cautions against over-reading Isa 66:22-23 as a Sabbath proof-text, noting the New Moon and dead-body problems. Anti-Sabbatarian critics (Answering Adventism) exploit the New Moon issue and point to Rev 21:23. However, Paulien concedes that Revelation's use of Isaiah language "allows the possibility of Sabbaths" in the new earth.

    Research Directives for Downstream Agents¶

    1. Paulien's allusion claim — Verify the verbal overlap between Rev 14:7 and Exo 20:11 through direct LXX/Greek NT comparison. Confirm the four shared terms (poieo, ouranos, ge, thalassa) and evaluate whether any other OT text matches as closely.

    2. Mueller's poieo vs. ktizo distinction — Check Rev 10:6 (claimed to use ktizo) against Rev 14:7 (poieo). Verify that Gen 1-2 LXX and Exo 20:11 LXX use poieo rather than ktizo. This verb choice is claimed to be the key distinguishing feature.

    3. Baldwin's "fountains of waters" substitution — Verify that Psa 146:6, Acts 4:24, and Acts 14:15 all use "panta ta en autois" where Rev 14:7 uses "pegas hydaton." Check whether pegas appears in LXX Gen 7:11 (claimed Flood connection).

    4. The entole vs. nomos distinction (Tinker) — Check every Johannine use of entole and nomos. Test whether Luke 23:56 uses entole for the Sabbath commandment. If it does, the distinction collapses.

    5. Lincoln's sabbatismos data — Verify the claimed occurrences in Plutarch, Justin, Epiphanius, and Apostolic Constitutions. Confirm that every extant use denotes Sabbath observance.

    6. Lane's translation — Verify that Lane translates sabbatismos as "Sabbath celebration" in his WBC commentary and what he concludes about its practical implications.

    7. The GCI counter-argument — Check the SDA Bible Commentary quote in context: "If the conclusion...is that Sabbathkeeping remains...there would have been no point in so labored an effort to persuade the Jews to do what they were already doing." Evaluate whether this is a valid argument or a straw man.

    8. Isaiah 66:22-23 New Moon problem — Verify whether Rev 21:23 says there is NO sun/moon or merely that the city does not NEED them for light. This distinction matters for the "no New Moons in the new earth" objection.

    9. Rev 14:12 framing — Verify that Rev 14:12 ("the commandments of God and the faith of Jesus") forms an inclusio with the creation formula of Rev 14:7. If "commandments of God" bookends the three angels' messages alongside a creation formula that echoes the fourth commandment, the structural argument for a Sabbath connection strengthens considerably.

    10. Paulien's structural claim — Verify the specific Rev 13 counterfeits of commandments 1-4 (13:4/8 = first, 13:15 = second, 13:6 = third, seal/mark = fourth). Check whether this mapping holds up under close reading.

    11. Gen 2:2-3 to Isa 66:22-23 bookend — Trace the Sabbath from creation (Gen 2:2-3) through the Exodus command (Exo 20:8-11) to the prophetic vision (Isa 66:22-23). Does this arc establish the Sabbath as a permanent institution or is the evidence ambiguous?

    12. Heb 4:9 in context — Read Heb 3:7-4:11 as a unit. Does the shift from katapausis to sabbatismos in 4:9 signal a deliberate intensification, or is it merely stylistic variation? The downstream agents should pay attention to the argument flow, not just the isolated verse.
