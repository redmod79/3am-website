    Word Studies¶

    Question¶

    What does 'everlasting gospel' (euangelion aionion) mean in Rev 14:6? Is this the same gospel Paul preached, or a different one? What does the anarthrous construction imply? How does euangelisai connect to the OT basar vocabulary chain (Isa 40:9, 52:7, 61:1)? What is the content of a gospel called 'everlasting'? How does the imperial euangelia thesis relate? Does it pass the Galatians 1:6-9 test?

    G2098 — euangelion¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2098-euangelion.md

    Occurrences: 77

    Neuter noun meaning 'good message/gospel.' 77 occurrences in 74 unique verses across 17 NT books. Overwhelmingly Pauline (78% of verses). Translation: 'gospel' 94.6% of the time. Heavy accusative bias (59%). 22+ NT designations show the gospel is a multifaceted message broader than any single summary. The word is absent from Luke, John (Luke prefers the verb euangelizo). Rev 14:6 is the last occurrence of euangelion in the Bible.

    Semantic categories: gospel of the kingdom, gospel of Christ, apostolic trust, standard of conduct, gospel under threat, universal scope, suffering for the gospel

    Key verses: Matt 4:23, Mark 1:1, Rom 1:16, Gal 1:6-7, Rev 14:6

    Relevance: The noun in euangelion aionion — the central term of this study. The 77 occurrences, 22+ designations, and anarthrous/articular distribution are all critical for evaluating the anarthrous construction debate.

    G2097 — euangelizo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2097-euangelizo.md

    Occurrences: 61

    Verb meaning 'to announce good news/evangelize.' 52 unique verses (61 tokens) across 12 books. Dominant voice is middle (88%). LXX bridge from H1319 basar — 18 of 21 OT uses map to euangelizo (PMI 10.0, score 29.44). Used for Messianic proclamation (Luke 4:18, quoting Isa 61:1), apostolic mission (Acts), and angelic announcement (Luke 2:10, Rev 14:6).

    Semantic categories: Messianic proclamation, apostolic mission, itinerant expansion, apostolic compulsion, peace proclaimed, angelic announcement, completed event

    Key verses: Luke 4:18, Rom 10:15, Gal 1:8, Rev 14:6

    Relevance: The verb euangelisai in Rev 14:6 — sharing the same root as the noun euangelion. This cognate construction (having a gospel to gospel) is unique in the NT. The verb creates the direct bridge to the OT basar vocabulary chain.

    G166 — aionios¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G166-aionios.md

    Occurrences: 71

    Adjective meaning 'eternal/everlasting.' 71 occurrences in 69 verses across 19 books. Dominant usage: 'eternal life' (~43 of 69 verses). Also modifies punishment (Mat 25:46), covenant (Heb 13:20), Spirit (Heb 9:14), and gospel (Rev 14:6). Can reach backward before creation (2 Tim 1:9: pro chronon aionion). Hebrew source: H5769 olam (PMI 5.67, score 26.16). KJV: 'eternal' (46.4%) and 'everlasting' (30.4%).

    Semantic categories: eternal life through Christ, eternal judgment/punishment, eternal divine attributes, eternal realities, eternal purpose and pre-temporal reference, eternal kingdom

    Key verses: John 3:16, Matt 25:46, 2 Tim 1:9, Heb 13:20, Rev 14:6

    Relevance: The adjective in euangelion aionion. The semantic range must settle whether the gospel is 'everlasting' (reaching both backward and forward) or merely 'age-long' (a single historical period). 2 Tim 1:9 and Tit 1:2 prove backward temporal reference. Matt 25:46 constrains the range to genuine eternity.

    G165 — aion¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G165-aion.md

    Occurrences: 120

    Masculine noun meaning 'age, era, eternity.' 95 unique verses (120 tokens) across 22 books. Root of aionios (G166). KJV: 'ever' (52.6%), 'world' (30.5%). Used in the doubled formula eis tous aionas ton aionon ('for ever and ever'). Semantic range spans 'this age' (present world-order), 'the age to come' (future age), and 'the ages' (unbounded time).

    Semantic categories: present age, age to come, for ever and ever, before the ages, the worlds

    Key verses: Matt 12:32, Mark 10:30, Heb 1:2, Rev 14:11

    Relevance: Root noun for aionios. Understanding the semantic range of aion (age vs. eternity) is essential for interpreting the adjective aionios in Rev 14:6.

    H1319 — basar¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H1319-basar.md

    Occurrences: 27

    Hebrew verb meaning 'to bear tidings, announce good news.' 27 tokens in 21 unique verses across 8 books. Piel stem in 96% of occurrences. Five Isaiah usages (40:9, 41:27, 52:7, 60:6, 61:1) define the prophetic herald tradition. The Piel participle mevasser functions as a technical term for an authorized divine herald. LXX renders as euangelizo in 18 of 21 occurrences (PMI 10.0, score 29.44).

    Semantic categories: battlefield messenger, birth announcement, prophetic herald of salvation, anointed proclamation to afflicted, liturgical proclamation, proclamation among nations

    Key verses: Isa 40:9, Isa 52:7, Isa 61:1, 2 Sam 18:19-20

    Relevance: The Hebrew root behind the NT gospel vocabulary. The basar-euangelizo bridge (PMI 10.0, score 29.44) is the strongest evidence for gospel continuity across testaments. The angel of Rev 14:6 inherits the Isaianic mevasser tradition.

    G4283 — proeuangelizomai¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4283-proeuangelizomai.md

    Occurrences: 1

    Hapax legomenon (Gal 3:8). Paul's compound: pro (before) + euangelizo (announce good news). 'The scripture gospeled-beforehand unto Abraham.' Paul coined this word to express that the gospel existed in the patriarchal era. The word itself IS the argument for OT gospel presence.

    Semantic categories: gospel proclaimed in advance

    Key verses: Gal 3:8

    Relevance: Linguistic proof that the gospel predated the NT. If Scripture itself 'gospeled-beforehand' to Abraham, then the gospel is genuinely aionion — it extends backward to patriarchal times.

    G2087 — heteros¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2087-heteros.md

    Occurrences: 99

    Adjective meaning 'another of a different kind.' 94 unique verses (99 tokens) across 16 NT books. In Gal 1:6, heteron euangelion marks the false gospel as qualitatively different from the true one — not merely a numerical alternative but a different species entirely.

    Semantic categories: different in kind, another, next in sequence

    Key verses: Gal 1:6, Acts 17:7, 2 Cor 11:4

    Relevance: Critical for the Gal 1:6-9 test. Heteros identifies the false gospel as qualitatively different from Paul's — a different KIND of message, not merely additional content.

    G243 — allos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G243-allos.md

    Occurrences: 160

    Adjective meaning 'another of the same kind.' 141 unique verses (160 tokens) across 13 NT books. In Gal 1:7, ho ouk estin allo clarifies: the false gospel is NOT 'another of the same kind' — it does not deserve the name 'gospel' at all.

    Semantic categories: another of same kind, other, additional

    Key verses: Gal 1:7, John 14:16

    Relevance: Paired with heteros in Gal 1:6-7 to define what Paul condemns. The heteros/allos contrast proves Paul is not condemning gospel breadth but gospel perversion — substituting works-righteousness for grace.

    G331 — anathema¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G331-anáthema.md

    Occurrences: 6

    Noun meaning 'accursed, devoted to destruction.' 6 occurrences across 4 books. In Gal 1:8-9, the double anathema esto constitutes Paul's most severe pronouncement: anyone preaching a different gospel is devoted to divine destruction. The formula extends to angels (Gal 1:8), making it universally applicable. Also used in Rom 9:3 (self-imprecatory), 1 Cor 12:3 (spiritual discernment test), 1 Cor 16:22 (anathema maranatha).

    Semantic categories: apostolic curse formula, self-imprecatory oath, spiritual discernment test

    Key verses: Gal 1:8, Gal 1:9, 1 Cor 12:3, Rom 9:3

    Relevance: The severity of Paul's anathema must be understood precisely. Paul condemns preaching a gospel that is heteron (different in kind) — specifically, substituting circumcision-for-salvation for grace. The anathema does not target broader gospel content but gospel perversion.

    H1309 — besorah¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H1309-bᵉsôwrâh.md

    Occurrences: 7

    Feminine noun meaning 'tidings, reward for good news.' 7 occurrences across 2 books (2 Samuel 6x, 2 Kings 1x). All in narrative contexts — battlefield tidings (2 Sam 18:20,25,27), reward for bringing news (2 Sam 4:10, 18:22), ethical obligation to share good news (2 Ki 7:9). No salvific or prophetic usage — the prophets used the VERB basar, not the noun besorah.

    Semantic categories: the news itself, reward for bringing news, ethical obligation to share news

    Key verses: 2 Sam 18:20, 2 Sam 18:25, 2 Ki 7:9

    Relevance: The OT noun form contrasts with the NT noun euangelion. OT prophets used the VERB basar for salvific proclamation; the NOUN besorah remained in narrative/military contexts. The NT coined euangelion as the theological noun — a semantic development from Hebrew verb to Greek noun.

    G2920 — krisis¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2920-krisis.md

    Occurrences: 47

    Feminine noun meaning 'judgment, judicial verdict, justice.' 47 occurrences across 14 books. Hebrew background: riyb (covenant lawsuit). In Rev 14:7, he hora tes kriseos autou — 'the hour of his judgment' — uses krisis for the divine judgment that grounds the first angel's imperatives.

    Semantic categories: divine judgment, judicial process, justice/righteous judgment

    Key verses: Rev 14:7, John 5:24, John 12:31, Acts 17:31

    Relevance: The judgment vocabulary in Rev 14:7. Krisis as the content of the everlasting gospel — the judgment announcement IS good news because it vindicates God's faithful.

    G5399 — phobeo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5399-phobeo.md

    Occurrences: 110

    Verb meaning 'to fear, reverence.' In Rev 14:7, phobethete (aorist passive imperative, 2nd plural) — 'Fear God!' — is the first imperative of the everlasting gospel. The semantic range spans terror/dread and reverence/awe. In the context of Rev 14:7, the fear is reverential worship of the Creator, contrasting with fear of the beast (Rev 13).

    Semantic categories: reverential fear of God, terror before divine judgment, fear overcome by faith

    Key verses: Rev 14:7, Matt 10:28, Luke 12:5

    Relevance: First imperative of the everlasting gospel. The call to 'fear God' echoes Eccl 12:13 ('Fear God, and keep his commandments') — the same triad of fear + commandments + judgment.

    G4352 — proskyneo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4352-proskyneo.md

    Occurrences: 65

    Verb meaning 'to worship, do reverence to.' In Rev 14:7, proskynhsate (aorist active imperative, 2nd plural) — 'Worship the Creator!' — is the third imperative of the everlasting gospel. Proskyneo appears 24 times in Revelation, establishing worship as the central issue of the book. The contrast: proskyneo the beast (Rev 13:4,8) vs. proskyneo the Creator (Rev 14:7).

    Semantic categories: worship of God/Christ, false worship of beast/idols, worship command

    Key verses: Rev 14:7, Rev 13:4, Rev 13:8, Rev 4:10

    Relevance: Third imperative of the everlasting gospel. The worship issue is the central conflict of Revelation — beast worship vs. Creator worship. The everlasting gospel defines the right object of worship.

    G1391 — doxa¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1391-doxa.md

    Occurrences: 168

    Feminine noun meaning 'glory, honor, splendor.' In Rev 14:7, dote auto doxan — 'give Him glory' — is the second imperative of the everlasting gospel. Glory-giving as a gospel response connects to the LXX bridge kabad→doxazo (Hebrew 'honor/weight' → Greek 'glorify').

    Semantic categories: divine glory, glory given to God, glory of Christ

    Key verses: Rev 14:7, Rom 1:21, Rev 4:11

    Relevance: Second imperative of the everlasting gospel. The call to give glory to God contrasts with the beast's self-glorification (Rev 13:5-6 — 'a mouth speaking great things and blasphemies').

    G5610 — hora¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5610-hora.md

    Occurrences: 108

    Feminine noun meaning 'hour, time, moment.' In Rev 14:7, he hora tes kriseos — 'the hour of his judgment' — uses hora for temporal specificity. Hora (hour) vs. hemera (day) encodes the anticipation-vs-announcement distinction: NT preachers spoke of the 'day' of judgment (Rom 2:16); the first angel speaks of the 'hour' — more specific, more imminent.

    Semantic categories: specific temporal moment, hour of judgment, decisive hour

    Key verses: Rev 14:7, John 5:25, Rev 3:10

    Relevance: The temporal marker of the first angel's message. The shift from hemera to hora signals the arrival of the judgment, not merely its anticipation.

    G4102 — pistis¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4102-pistis.md

    Occurrences: 244

    Feminine noun meaning 'faith, faithfulness, trust.' In Rev 14:12, ten pistin Iesou — 'the faith of Jesus' — may be subjective genitive (Jesus' own faithfulness), objective genitive (faith in Jesus), or a compact genitive encompassing both. The Pauline pistis Christou tradition (Rom 3:22, Gal 2:16) parallels this construction.

    Semantic categories: saving faith, faithfulness, the body of doctrine

    Key verses: Rev 14:12, Rom 3:22, Gal 2:16, Heb 11:1

    Relevance: Coordinates with commandment-keeping in Rev 14:12. If pistis Iesou is subjective genitive, it expresses justification-by-faith theology — 'the faithfulness that Jesus demonstrated.' EGW identifies this as 'justification by faith is the third angel's message in verity.'

    G5083 — tereo¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5083-tereo.md

    Occurrences: 75

    Verb meaning 'to keep, guard, observe.' In Rev 14:12, hoi terountes tas entolas tou Theou — 'the ones keeping the commandments of God' — uses the present active participle to describe the saints' ongoing identity. Tereo emphasizes careful, watchful keeping — not mere acknowledgment but active observance.

    Semantic categories: keeping commandments, guarding/preserving, observing

    Key verses: Rev 14:12, John 14:15, 1 John 5:3

    Relevance: Identifies the saints by their practice. The present participle terountes is descriptive (identifying the saints), not imperative (commanding them). Compare Paul's teresis entolon Theou in 1 Cor 7:19.

    G1785 — entole¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1785-entole.md

    Occurrences: 71

    Feminine noun meaning 'commandment, injunction.' In Rev 14:12, tas entolas tou Theou — 'the commandments of God' — refers to God's authoritative commands. The genitive tou Theou identifies these as God's commandments specifically (not human traditions).

    Semantic categories: divine commandments, moral imperatives, new commandment

    Key verses: Rev 14:12, 1 Cor 7:19, 1 John 2:3-4

    Relevance: Coordinated with pistis Iesou in Rev 14:12. The commandments-of-God + faith-of-Jesus combination is the distinctive description of the saints who respond to the everlasting gospel.

    G4413 — protos¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4413-prōtos.md

    Occurrences: 105

    Contracted superlative adjective meaning 'first, foremost, chief.' 105 occurrences. In 1 Cor 15:3, en protois means 'among the first things' or 'of first importance' — marking priority/primacy, not exclusivity. Paul signals that what follows is foundational, not definitional of the gospel's total content.

    Semantic categories: ordinal position, temporal priority, rank/primacy, first/last reversal, divine title

    Key verses: 1 Cor 15:3, Matt 22:38, Rev 1:17

    Relevance: Critical for the '1 Cor 15:3-4 as exclusive gospel definition' argument. If en protois means 'of first importance' (primacy), then 1 Cor 15:3-4 is a priority statement, not an exclusivity statement. Paul is saying 'these are the MOST important things,' not 'these are the ONLY things.'

    G4059 — peritemno¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4059-peritémnō.md

    Occurrences: 22

    Verb meaning 'to circumcise.' 22 occurrences across 6 books. Heaviest concentration in Galatians (7x) and Acts (7x). In Galatians, peritemno appears exclusively in soteriological polemic — circumcision as a salvation requirement nullifies grace (Gal 5:2-4). In 1 Cor 7:18-19, Paul declares circumcision theologically neutral. In Col 2:11, spiritual circumcision replaces physical.

    Semantic categories: Abrahamic covenant sign, circumcision controversy in Acts, Paul's soteriological polemic, indifference principle, spiritual circumcision

    Key verses: Gal 5:2, Gal 6:12-13, Acts 15:1, 1 Cor 7:18-19

    Relevance: Identifies the specific act Paul condemns in Galatians as the 'different gospel.' The Galatian heresy is circumcision-for-salvation, not the addition of judgment, commandment-keeping, or creation theology to gospel content.

    G2782 — kerygma¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2782-kerygma.md

    Occurrences: 8

    Neuter noun meaning 'proclamation, preaching, the thing preached.' Related to kerysso (to herald/proclaim). In the herald tradition, kerygma emphasizes the authoritative public announcement — the herald declares the king's message. Related to the euangelizo/euangelion word family by function (both describe proclamation) but distinguished by emphasis (kerygma = mode of public announcement; euangelion = the content of good news).

    Semantic categories: authoritative proclamation, apostolic preaching, the thing proclaimed

    Key verses: 1 Cor 1:21, 1 Cor 2:4, 1 Cor 15:14

    Relevance: Related proclamation vocabulary. The herald (keryx) model connects the OT mevasser tradition to NT apostolic proclamation and to the angel of Rev 14:6.

    H5769 — owlam¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H5769-owlam.md

    Occurrences: 432

    Hebrew noun meaning 'everlasting, forever, perpetuity.' The Hebrew conceptual equivalent of aionios. Used 199 times in OT for 'everlasting/forever' contexts. LXX consistently renders olam as aionios (PMI 5.67, score 26.16). The semantic tradition established by olam→aionios in the LXX is the background Rev 14:6 inherits.

    Semantic categories: everlasting duration, ancient/long past, perpetuity of divine covenant

    Key verses: Gen 21:33, Exod 3:15, Dan 12:2

    Relevance: Hebrew background for aionios in Rev 14:6. The olam→aionios LXX bridge establishes that 'everlasting gospel' inherits the Hebrew concept of indefinite duration stretching both backward and forward.

    G5281 — hupomone¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5281-hupomone.md

    Occurrences: 32

    Feminine noun meaning 'patience, endurance, steadfast perseverance.' In Rev 14:12, he hupomone ton hagion — 'the patience of the saints' — introduces the characterization of those who respond to the everlasting gospel. Hupomone implies active, courageous endurance under trial, not passive waiting.

    Semantic categories: steadfast endurance under trial, patient perseverance

    Key verses: Rev 14:12, Rom 5:3-4, James 1:3-4

    Relevance: Introduces the Rev 14:12 characterization. The saints' response to the everlasting gospel is not mere agreement but sustained endurance — keeping commandments and maintaining faith under the pressure of the beast-worship crisis.

    G3466 — mysterion¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3466-mysterion.md

    Occurrences: 27

    Neuter noun meaning 'mystery, hidden purpose.' In Rev 10:7, the 'mystery of God' (to mysterion tou Theou) connects to the everlasting gospel via the shared verb euangelizo. Paul uses mysterion for the gospel's hidden-then-revealed character (Rom 16:25-26; Eph 3:4-9; Col 1:26-27). The mystery kept secret since the world began is now made manifest.

    Semantic categories: revealed mystery of the gospel, mystery of God's purpose, mystery of Christ

    Key verses: Rev 10:7, Rom 16:25-26, Eph 3:4-9

    Relevance: Links Rev 10:7 (mystery of God should be finished, as he euangelise to the prophets) with Rev 14:6 (having euangelion aionion euangelisai). The everlasting gospel is the finishing of the mystery declared to the prophets.

    G3321 — mesouranema¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3321-mesouranema.md

    Occurrences: 3

    Neuter noun meaning 'mid-heaven, zenith.' In Rev 14:6, the angel flies en mesouranhemati — in the middle of heaven. This position ensures maximum visibility and audibility for the gospel proclamation. Appears only 3 times in the NT, all in Revelation (8:13, 14:6, 19:17).

    Semantic categories: zenith position for proclamation

    Key verses: Rev 14:6, Rev 8:13, Rev 19:17

    Relevance: The angel's position in mid-heaven ensures the universal reach of the everlasting gospel proclamation — visible and audible to all who dwell on earth.
