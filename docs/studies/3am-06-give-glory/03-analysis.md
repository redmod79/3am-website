# Verse-by-Verse Analysis

## Question
What does dote auto doxan ("give glory to him") mean as a gospel imperative? How is glory (doxa/kabod) given to God — through confession (Josh 7:19), character acknowledgment (Isa 42:12), conduct (Matt 5:16, 1 Pet 2:12), fruit-bearing (John 15:8), bodily stewardship (1 Cor 6:20, 10:31), beholding-transformation (2 Cor 3:18), or something else?

---

## Focus Area 1: The Kabod/Doxa Semantic Bridge and the Meaning of "Glory"

### Exodus 33:18-19
**Context:** Moses asks God for a direct encounter with His glory (kebodeka, H3519). God responds not with a display of radiance but by promising to make His goodness (tubi, H2898) pass before Moses and to proclaim His name (shem YHWH).
**Direct statement:** Glory equals goodness and name. Moses asked for one thing; God answered with another — except they are the same thing. God's glory IS His character, His goodness, His name. This is the foundational OT passage equating kabod with revealed character rather than visible light.
**Original language:** The Hebrew hareni na eth kebodeka ("show me your glory") uses kabod (H3519), from the root kabad (H3513, "to be heavy"). The root meaning is literal weight — what has substance commands honor. God's response redirects the request: "I will make all my goodness (tubi) pass before you." The shift from kebodeka to tubi is not a deflection but an explanation.
**Cross-references:** Exodus 34:5-7 fulfills this promise. John 1:14 echoes it in the incarnation. Habakkuk 2:14 projects it eschatologically.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This passage is consistent with every other glory text in the study. The equation glory = character anchors the entire investigation and is confirmed rather than contradicted by passages where glory appears as visible radiance (Isa 6:3), since God's visible presence itself expresses His character.

### Exodus 34:5-7
**Context:** The fulfillment of God's promise in Exodus 33:19. God descends in a cloud, stands with Moses, and proclaims His name.
**Direct statement:** The content of God's glory is a list of character attributes: "The LORD, The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth, keeping mercy for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear the guilty." This is what glory looks like when described in words rather than light.
**Original language:** The name-proclamation (qara beshem YHWH) and the character list are the substance of the kabod that Moses requested. Both mercy and justice appear — glory is not one attribute but the full weight of who God is.
**Cross-references:** John 1:14 ("full of grace and truth") directly echoes the chesed ve-emeth of Exodus 34:6. Revelation 15:3-4 ("just and true are thy ways") recalls the same character attributes in an eschatological judgment setting.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This passage resolves any ambiguity about whether kabod is merely visible radiance. It is not. The visible presence accompanies the character proclamation, but the content of the glory is the character itself.

### Isaiah 6:1-3
**Context:** Isaiah's temple vision. The seraphim declare the triple holiness and announce that the whole earth is "full of his glory" (melo kol ha-aretz kebodo).
**Direct statement:** God's glory fills the entire earth — it is not confined to the temple. The seraphim see what the rest of creation is meant to see. The declaration pairs holiness with glory-filling.
**Original language:** kabod (H3519) appears in the climactic statement. The present tense — the earth IS full — declares an existing reality, not a future hope (contrast Hab 2:14, which projects the knowledge of this reality forward).
**Cross-references:** Habakkuk 2:14 adds "the knowledge of" the glory — the earth is full of glory, but it will one day be full of the recognition of that glory. Revelation 18:1 fulfills this when the earth is "lightened with his glory."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Consistent with the Exodus 33-34 equation. If glory = character, then saying the earth is "full of his glory" means creation already testifies to God's character attributes (cf. Psa 19:1, Rom 1:19-20).

### Habakkuk 2:14
**Context:** A prophetic promise set within oracles against Babylonian oppression. The earth will be filled with the "knowledge of the glory of the LORD."
**Direct statement:** The future filling is not of glory itself (Isaiah 6 already declares the earth full of glory) but of the knowledge of that glory. This means glory-giving involves knowing and declaring who God is — not just experiencing a presence but understanding what it means.
**Original language:** ladaath eth kebod YHWH — "to know the glory of YHWH." The addition of daath (knowledge, H1847) shifts from ontological filling (the earth is full) to epistemological filling (the earth will know).
**Cross-references:** Revelation 18:1 ("the earth was lightened with his glory") is the eschatological fulfillment. 2 Corinthians 4:6 ("the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ") uses nearly identical language.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This supports the confession dimension of glory-giving — glory must be known and acknowledged, not merely displayed.

### John 1:14
**Context:** The Prologue to John's Gospel. The Word becomes flesh and "dwelt" (eskenosen, "tabernacled") among humanity.
**Direct statement:** "We beheld his glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father, full of grace and truth." The glory beheld in the incarnate Christ is described with the same terms as Exodus 34:6 — grace and truth (charis kai aletheia = chesed ve-emeth).
**Original language:** etheasametha (V-ADI-1P of theaomai) — "we beheld/contemplated." The verb implies sustained observation, not a glance. The glory seen in Christ is qualified as pleres charitos kai aletheias — "full of grace and truth."
**Cross-references:** Exodus 34:6; 2 Corinthians 4:6; Hebrews 1:3.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Christ is the kabod/doxa bridge incarnate. The OT glory that was proclaimed in character attributes (Exod 34) is now visible in a person. This confirms the character-glory equation and establishes Christ as the one in whom glory is both revealed and given.

### 2 Corinthians 4:6
**Context:** Paul on the unveiled gospel ministry. God has shone into believers' hearts to give the light of knowledge.
**Direct statement:** "God, who commanded the light to shine out of darkness, hath shined in our hearts, to give the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ." Four genitives chain together: light of knowledge of glory of God — all located "in the face of Jesus Christ."
**Original language:** The creation-light parallel (Gen 1:3) means the same God who spoke light into physical darkness speaks revelatory light into spiritual darkness. The prosopon (face) of Christ is where glory becomes visible.
**Cross-references:** Habakkuk 2:14 (knowledge of glory); John 1:14 (glory beheld in Christ); 2 Corinthians 3:18 (beholding glory with unveiled face).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This passage bridges the Exodus equation (glory = character), the incarnation (glory visible in Christ), and the transformation mechanism (glory shines into hearts, enabling recognition).

### 2 Corinthians 4:17
**Context:** Paul contrasts present affliction with future glory.
**Direct statement:** "Our light affliction, which is but for a moment, worketh for us a far more exceeding and eternal weight of glory" (baros doxes).
**Original language:** baros (G922, "weight/heaviness") echoes the Hebrew kabod (from kabad, "to be heavy"). Paul may intentionally preserve the kabod etymology in Greek: glory IS weight, substance, heaviness. The "eternal weight of glory" is the opposite of "light affliction."
**Cross-references:** H3519 kabowd, from H3513 kabad ("to be heavy"). The kabod→doxa bridge preserved kabod's root meaning in passages like this.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Confirms that the OT connection of glory with weight/substance carried into Paul's Greek vocabulary.

### Hebrews 1:3
**Context:** The opening declaration of Christ's superiority. Christ is the apaugasma ("brightness/radiance") of God's glory and the charakter ("exact imprint") of His person.
**Direct statement:** Christ both radiates and reproduces God's glory. He is not an approximation but the exact representation.
**Original language:** apaugasma tes doxes — "radiance of glory." charakter tes hypostaseos — "exact imprint of his substance." The vocabulary combines visible radiance with substantive representation.
**Cross-references:** John 1:14; Colossians 1:15.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Reinforces the connection between visible glory and character substance. Christ does not merely display glory — He IS the exact character-imprint of the Father.

### Revelation 14:6-7
**Context:** The first angel carries the everlasting gospel with three aorist imperatives: fear God (phobethete), give glory (dote auto doxan), worship the Creator (proskynesate).
**Direct statement:** "Give glory to him" is the second imperative, positioned between fear (the internal orientation) and worship (the behavioral expression). The dote auto doxan construction uses didomi (G1325, "to give") + dative pronoun + accusative of doxa (G1391, "glory").
**Original language:** dote (V-2AAM-2P, second aorist active imperative of didomi G1325). The aorist imperative commands a decisive, specific action — not a gradual process but a definitive response. auto (P-DSM, dative pronoun) + doxan (N-ASF of doxa G1391). The hoti (G3754) clause introduces the judgment announcement. The creation language ("him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters") echoes Exodus 20:11 — the Sabbath commandment.
**Cross-references:** Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 (OT template); Revelation 16:9 (verbal echo); Joshua 7:19, John 9:24 (confession idiom); Romans 4:20 (same didomi + doxa construction).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the central text of the study. Every other passage illuminates what "give glory" means. The kabod/doxa semantic bridge tells us what glory is; the various modes (confession, conduct, fruit-bearing, bodily stewardship, beholding-transformation, completed work) tell us how it is given.

## Focus Area 2: The "Give Glory" Confession Idiom (Josh 7:19 → John 9:24 → Rev 14:7)

### Joshua 7:19
**Context:** Achan's sin has been discovered after the defeat at Ai. Joshua commands Achan to confess.
**Direct statement:** "Give glory to the LORD God of Israel, and make confession unto him; and tell me now what thou hast done." Giving glory and making confession are parallel commands — they mean the same thing. This is a judicial setting: a demand for truthful testimony before God.
**Original language:** sim na kabod la-YHWH — "put/place glory to YHWH." The verb is sim (H7760, "put/place"), not nathan ("give"), but the construction functions identically. The parallel command: ten lo todah — "give him confession/thanksgiving." todah (H8426, from yadah H3034) carries the dual meaning of thanksgiving and confession. Both acknowledge that God is right.
**Cross-references:** John 9:24 uses the identical Greek formula. Malachi 2:2 uses the nathan + kabod construction.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This passage establishes the confession dimension of "give glory." When the first angel says "give glory," one layer of meaning is the judicial-oath formula: acknowledge that God is right and you are wrong. This does not exhaust the meaning of "give glory" in Rev 14:7, but it is one significant dimension.

### John 9:24
**Context:** The healing of the man born blind. The Pharisees demand truthful testimony.
**Direct statement:** "Give God the praise" (dos doxan to theo) — the same formula as Joshua 7:19, now in a NT judicial setting. The Pharisees demand that the healed man tell the truth.
**Original language:** dos (V-2AAM-2S of didomi G1325, aorist active imperative) — the same verb as Rev 14:7's dote (but singular). doxan (N-ASF of doxa G1391) — the same noun. to theo (N-DSM) — dative. This is the identical construction as Rev 14:7 (dote auto doxan), used in an investigation demanding honest testimony.
**Cross-references:** Joshua 7:19; Revelation 14:7; Philippians 2:11.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This confirms that the "give glory" formula carries a confessional force across Testaments. The formula survived from the Hebrew judicial tradition into first-century Greek usage.

### Malachi 2:2
**Context:** God's indictment of unfaithful priests. They have failed to honor God's name.
**Direct statement:** "If ye will not hear, and if ye will not lay it to heart, to give glory unto my name, saith the LORD of hosts, I will even send a curse upon you." The consequence of refusing to give glory is cursing.
**Original language:** latheth kabod lishmi — "to give glory to my name." latheth (Qal infinitive construct of nathan H5414) + kabod (H3519) + lishmi ("to my name"). This is the Hebrew construction underlying Rev 14:7's Greek.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 16:9 (refusal to give glory results in plagues — the eschatological form of Malachi's curse). Joshua 7:19 (same confession idiom).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Malachi adds the consequence dimension: failure to give glory brings divine judgment. This directly parallels the glory-judgment connection in Revelation 14:7 and the refusal-plague connection in 16:9.

### Philippians 2:11
**Context:** Christ's exaltation following His humiliation. Every knee bows and every tongue confesses.
**Direct statement:** "Every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father." Confession (exomologesetai, G1843) leads directly to glory (eis doxan theou patros). Confessing Christ's lordship IS giving glory to the Father.
**Original language:** exomologesetai (V-FMI-3S of exomologeo G1843, which appears 11 times in the NT) — the LXX's primary rendering of yadah (H3034, 136 occurrences). The dual meaning of yadah (praise/confess) is preserved: the universal confession is both an admission of Christ's lordship and a doxological act.
**Cross-references:** Joshua 7:19 (confession-glory link); Isaiah 45:23 (every knee shall bow, every tongue confess); Revelation 15:4 (who shall not fear and glorify?).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the eschatological climax of the confession-glory tradition. Every tongue will eventually give glory — the first angel's message offers the opportunity to do so willingly before the judgment makes it compulsory.

## Focus Area 3: Romans 1:18-25 as the Precise Inverse of the First Angel's Message

### Romans 1:18-23
**Context:** Paul's description of humanity's downward spiral. God's wrath is revealed against those who suppress the truth despite having clear evidence from creation.
**Direct statement:** The chain is explicit: (1) knew God through creation (vv.19-20), (2) "glorified him not as God, neither were thankful" (v.21), (3) "became vain in their imaginations, and their foolish heart was darkened" (v.21b), (4) "professing themselves to be wise, they became fools" (v.22), (5) "changed the glory of the uncorruptible God into an image" (v.23). This five-step descent is the precise inverse of the first angel's call: where Rev 14:7 says "fear God → give glory → worship the Creator," Romans 1 says "suppress truth → refuse to glorify → exchange glory → worship the creature."
**Original language:** edoxasan (V-AAI-3P of doxazo G1392) — "they glorified [not]." This is the verbal inversion of Rev 14:7's dote doxan. The constructions differ (verb doxazo vs. didomi + noun doxa) but share the same root. ellaxan (V-AAI-3P of allasso G236) — "they exchanged" the glory. eikoni (N-DSF of eikon G1504) — the glory was exchanged for an "image," the same word used for the beast's image in Revelation 13.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7 (the command this passage inverts); Psalm 106:20 ("changed their glory into the similitude of an ox"); Jeremiah 2:11 ("my people have changed their glory for that which doth not profit"); Revelation 13:14-15 (the beast-image as the eschatological form of the glory-exchange).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the clearest negative parallel to the first angel's message. Romans 1 describes the exact condition that Rev 14:7 addresses. The first angel's command to "give glory" is God's remedy for the glory-deficit described here.

### Romans 1:24-25
**Context:** The consequences of the glory-exchange. God "gave them up" (paredoken) to uncleanness.
**Direct statement:** "Who changed the truth of God into a lie, and worshipped and served the creature more than the Creator." This is the direct antiparallel of Rev 14:7's "worship him that made heaven, and earth."
**Original language:** esebasthesan kai elatreusan te ktisei para ton ktisanta — "worshipped and served the creature rather than the Creator." The ktisis/ktisas vocabulary in Rom 1:25 parallels the poieo vocabulary in Rev 14:7. Both describe the Creator.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7 ("worship him that made"); Revelation 13:4, 8, 12 (beast-worship as the eschatological form of creature-worship).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Confirms that failing to glorify God leads to worshipping something other than God. The trajectory is not neutral — refusing to give glory produces idolatry. This is why the first angel's call to give glory is urgent.

### Romans 3:23
**Context:** Paul's universal verdict: all have sinned and fallen short.
**Direct statement:** "All have sinned, and come short of the glory of God." The verb husterountai (present passive of hustereo G5302) means "are falling short of / are lacking." Every human being has a glory-deficit.
**Original language:** husterountai tes doxes tou theou — "are falling short of the glory of God." The present tense indicates an ongoing condition, not a past event only.
**Cross-references:** 2 Corinthians 3:18 (the resolution: glory received through beholding); Revelation 14:7 (the command to give glory despite the deficit); John 17:22 (Christ transmits glory to believers).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This creates a paradox that 2 Corinthians 3:18 and John 17:22 resolve. The first angel commands giving glory (Rev 14:7), but all humans lack glory (Rom 3:23). The resolution is that glory must be received before it can be given — through beholding Christ (2 Cor 3:18), through Christ's gift (John 17:22), through abiding in the vine (John 15:8).

## Focus Area 4: The Rev 14:7 / Rev 16:9 Verbal Echo and the Seal-Plague Inclusio

### Revelation 14:7 (as analyzed under Focus Area 1)
The core construction: dote (V-2AAM-2P, aorist active imperative of didomi G1325) auto (P-DSM) doxan (N-ASF of doxa G1391). Three lexemes: didomi + autos + doxa. The imperative mood makes this a command — the gospel offered.

### Revelation 16:9
**Context:** The fourth plague. The sun scorches men with great heat. Their response: blasphemy and refusal to repent.
**Direct statement:** "They repented not to give him glory" (ou metenoasan dounai auto doxan). This uses the same three lexemes as Rev 14:7 (didomi + autos + doxa) but shifts from imperative (dote, the command) to infinitive (dounai, the purpose/result they refused). The imperative offered in 14:7 becomes the infinitive refused in 16:9.
**Original language:** metenoasan (V-AAI-3P of metanoeo G3340) — "they did not repent." dounai (V-2AAN, aorist active infinitive of didomi G1325) auto (P-DSM) doxan (N-ASF of doxa G1391). The grammatical shift from imperative to purpose infinitive is significant: the command to give glory (14:7) is now the goal they refuse to reach (16:9).
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7 (the positive command); Revelation 9:20-21 (the precursor refusal under the sixth trumpet); Revelation 11:13 (positive response: edokan doxan); Revelation 15:4 (future universal glorification).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This creates a structural inclusio. The gospel call opens with "give glory" (14:7); the plague narrative records the refusal to "give glory" (16:9). The same lexemes prove the echo is verbal, not merely thematic. The wicked heard the command and rejected it — the plagues are the consequence.

### Revelation 11:13
**Context:** After the two witnesses are resurrected, an earthquake destroys a tenth of the city. The survivors, terrified, respond.
**Direct statement:** "The remnant were affrighted, and gave glory to the God of heaven" (edokan doxan to theo tou ouranou).
**Original language:** edokan (V-AAI-3P of didomi G1325) — the same verb as Rev 14:7's dote and 16:9's dounai. This is one of the rare POSITIVE uses of the didomi + doxa construction in Revelation.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7 (the command); Revelation 16:9 (the refusal). The three Revelation passages form a pattern: commanded (14:7), accepted (11:13), refused (16:9).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Shows that giving glory is a possible response — it is not merely a theoretical command. Some do respond to divine acts with genuine glorification.

### Revelation 9:20-21
**Context:** The sixth trumpet. Despite devastating plagues, the survivors do not repent.
**Direct statement:** "Repented not of the works of their hands, that they should not worship devils, and idols of gold, and silver, and brass, and stone, and of wood." The idols "neither can see, nor hear, nor walk."
**Original language:** oude metenoaesan — "neither did they repent." The refusal to repent under the sixth trumpet anticipates the refusal under the fourth plague (Rev 16:9). The idol description echoes Daniel 5:23 — Belshazzar praised gods that "see not, nor hear, nor know."
**Cross-references:** Revelation 16:9, 11; Daniel 5:23.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the precursor to the Rev 16:9 refusal. The pattern of unrepentance is established before the seven last plagues and continues through them.

### Revelation 15:4
**Context:** The song of Moses and the Lamb, sung by those who overcame the beast.
**Direct statement:** "Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name? for thou only art holy: for all nations shall come and worship before thee; for thy judgments are made manifest."
**Original language:** The fear-glorify-worship cluster from Rev 14:7 recurs: phobethei + doxasei to onoma sou + all nations worship. hai kriseis sou ephanerothesan — "thy judgments are made manifest." The judgment is completed and vindicated.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7 (the same triad during the gospel call); Exodus 15:11 (the original song of Moses after the Red Sea).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Post-judgment, the command becomes celebration. What was imperative in 14:7 ("give glory") becomes a rhetorical question in 15:4 ("who shall not glorify?"). God's judgments, once announced, are now vindicated — and glory is the only possible response.

### Revelation 4:11
**Context:** The heavenly throne-room. The twenty-four elders fall before the throne and cast their crowns.
**Direct statement:** "Thou art worthy, O Lord, to receive glory and honour and power: for thou hast created all things."
**Original language:** labein ten doxan — "to receive glory." The reason: "for thou hast created all things" (hoti su ektisas ta panta). Glory-giving is grounded in creation — the same logic as Rev 14:7's "worship him that made."
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7; Revelation 5:13.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This establishes the heavenly pattern. The throne-room worship in Rev 4 is the model that Rev 14:7 calls earth to follow. Creation grounds the obligation to give glory.

## Focus Area 5: Christ as the Model of Giving Glory (John 17:4)

### John 17:1-5
**Context:** The High Priestly Prayer. Jesus speaks from the vantage of completed work, looking back over His entire earthly mission.
**Direct statement:** "I have glorified thee on the earth: I have finished the work which thou gavest me to do" (v.4). The two clauses are parallel: glorifying = finishing the work. Christ glorified the Father by completing the assigned mission.
**Original language:** edoxasa (V-AAI-1S of doxazo G1392, aorist) — "I have glorified." teleiosas (V-AAP-NSM of teleioo G5048, aorist participle) — "having finished." The aorist participle is circumstantial and epexegetical: it explains HOW the glorification happened. Bengel's claim is grammatically supported — "I have finished" explains "I have glorified." The aorist treats the entire redemptive mission as one completed act. dedokas (V-RAI-2S of didomi G1325, perfect) — the Father's giving is settled and permanent.
**Cross-references:** John 4:34 (same purpose-language); John 12:28; John 13:31-32; John 19:30.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Christ is the model for what "give glory" means. If glorification = completing the assigned work, then believers give glory by completing the work God assigns them. This grounds the imperative of Rev 14:7 not in abstract praise but in faithful living.

### John 4:34
**Context:** Jesus at the well of Samaria. The disciples urge Him to eat.
**Direct statement:** "My meat is to do the will of him that sent me, and to finish his work." Christ defines His sustenance — His deepest motivation — as doing God's will and completing (teleioso, V-AAS-1S of teleioo G5048) the work.
**Original language:** teleioso autou to ergon — "to finish his work." The same teleioo verb appears here and in John 17:4.
**Cross-references:** John 17:4 (the declaration of completion); John 6:38 ("I came down from heaven, not to do mine own will, but the will of him that sent me").
**Relationship to other evidence:** This earlier statement establishes the pattern: Christ's entire life was oriented toward completing the Father's assignment. The glorification declared in John 17:4 was not a sudden act but the culmination of a life lived this way.

### John 12:28
**Context:** Jesus anticipates the cross and asks the Father to glorify His name.
**Direct statement:** "Father, glorify thy name. Then came there a voice from heaven, saying, I have both glorified it, and will glorify it again."
**Original language:** doxason sou to onoma — "glorify your name." edoxasa kai palin doxaso — "I have glorified and will glorify again." Past (edoxasa, aorist) and future (doxaso) glorification bracket the cross. The Father's name = His character (cf. Exod 34:5-7).
**Cross-references:** Exodus 34:5-7 (name-proclamation = character revelation); John 17:4-5.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The Father declares that His name (character) has already been glorified through Christ's life and will be glorified again through the cross and resurrection. This confirms that glorification is an ongoing process with a decisive climax.

### John 13:31-32
**Context:** Judas has just left to betray Jesus. The sequence leading to the cross has begun.
**Direct statement:** "Now is the Son of man glorified, and God is glorified in him." The word doxazo appears five times in two verses — a concentration found nowhere else in Scripture.
**Original language:** nun edoxasthe ho huios tou anthropou — "now the Son of man IS glorified." The betrayal does not defeat glorification — it triggers it. The cross is the supreme act of glory-giving.
**Cross-references:** John 17:1 ("the hour is come; glorify thy Son"); John 12:23 ("the hour is come, that the Son of man should be glorified").
**Relationship to other evidence:** The cross concentrates the glorification theme to its highest intensity. Suffering and apparent defeat become the occasion for the greatest glory — a pattern extended to believers in 1 Peter 4:14-16.

### John 17:22
**Context:** Christ's prayer continues. He reveals the glory chain: Father → Son → believers.
**Direct statement:** "The glory which thou gavest me I have given them; that they may be one, even as we are one."
**Original language:** ten doxan hen dedokas moi dedoka autois — "the glory which you have given me I have given them." Both verbs are perfect tense of didomi (G1325) — the giving is settled and permanent. Glory is not merely ascribed but transmitted. It flows from the Father through Christ to believers.
**Cross-references:** 2 Corinthians 3:18 (the mechanism of glory-transmission); Romans 3:23 (the glory-deficit this resolves); 2 Peter 1:3-4 (partakers of divine nature).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This resolves Bohr's paradox ("how can we give glory when we have none?"). Believers give glory because they have received glory through Christ. The transmission chain — Father to Son to believers — makes glory-giving possible.

### John 21:19
**Context:** Jesus prophesies Peter's martyrdom.
**Direct statement:** "This spake he, signifying by what death he should glorify God."
**Original language:** doxasei ton theon — "he should glorify God" (future active of doxazo G1392). Peter's death IS a mode of glorifying God.
**Cross-references:** 1 Peter 4:14-16 (suffering for Christ glorifies God); Philippians 1:20 ("Christ shall be magnified in my body, whether by life, or by death").
**Relationship to other evidence:** Extends the glorification model beyond completed work to suffering and martyrdom. Nave's lists dying for Christ under GLORIFYING GOD "Accomplished by."

## Focus Area 6: The Beholding-Transformation Mechanism (2 Cor 3:18)

### 2 Corinthians 3:18
**Context:** Paul contrasts the fading glory of Moses' ministry with the surpassing glory of the Spirit's ministry. The old covenant came with glory (Moses' shining face); the new covenant comes with greater glory (permanent transformation).
**Direct statement:** "We all, with open face beholding as in a glass the glory of the Lord, are changed into the same image from glory to glory, even as by the Spirit of the Lord." The mechanism: sustained contemplation of God's glory, mediated by the Spirit, produces progressive character transformation.
**Original language:** katoptrizomenoi (V-PMP-NPM of katoptrizomai G2734, present middle participle) — "beholding as in a mirror." This is a hapax legomenon (1 occurrence only in the NT). The middle voice favors "beholding" over "reflecting" — the subject is actively looking, not passively mirroring. metamorphoumetha (V-PPI-1P of metamorphoo G3339, present passive indicative) — "we are being transformed." Present tense = ongoing. Passive voice = the Spirit is the agent. The same verb used for Christ's transfiguration (Matt 17:2). apo doxes eis doxan — "from glory to glory" — progressive transformation. doxa appears three times in this verse.
**Cross-references:** Exodus 34:29-35 (Moses' shining face — the OT background); Romans 8:29 (conformed to Christ's image); 1 John 3:1-3 (eschatological completion).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the mechanism that resolves the glory-deficit problem. All have sinned and fall short of God's glory (Rom 3:23), but through beholding Christ's glory, believers are progressively transformed into that same image. Glory is received before it is given. This makes the first angel's command (Rev 14:7) possible — not through human effort but through Spirit-empowered contemplation.

### 2 Corinthians 3:9-10
**Context:** Paul's comparison of the old and new covenant ministries.
**Direct statement:** "If the ministration of condemnation be glory, much more doth the ministration of righteousness exceed in glory." The new covenant's glory surpasses the old.
**Original language:** The a fortiori argument: if condemnation came with glory, how much more does righteousness. The progression means the glory available for beholding under the new covenant is greater than what Moses experienced.
**Cross-references:** 2 Corinthians 3:18 (the result of beholding this greater glory).
**Relationship to other evidence:** The surpassing glory of the gospel means the transformation available through beholding is greater than anything under the old covenant. The unveiled face (3:18) contrasts with Moses' veil (3:13) — believers have direct access.

### Romans 8:29
**Context:** The golden chain: foreknew, predestinated, called, justified, glorified.
**Direct statement:** "Predestinated to be conformed to the image of his Son." The divine purpose is conformity to Christ's image (symmorphous tes eikonos).
**Original language:** symmorphos (G4832) = "same form/nature." eikon (G1504) = image. The goal is not surface resemblance but substantive conformity to Christ's character.
**Cross-references:** 2 Corinthians 3:18 (the progressive process); 1 John 3:2 (the completion).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the predestined goal of the beholding-transformation process. What 2 Cor 3:18 describes as ongoing, Rom 8:29 describes as God's eternal purpose.

### Colossians 3:10
**Context:** Believers have "put on the new man."
**Direct statement:** "Which is renewed in knowledge after the image of him that created him."
**Original language:** kat' eikona tou ktisantos — "after the image of the one who created." eikon (G1504) + ktizo (G2936, "to create"). The renewal is directional: toward the Creator's image. The creation vocabulary connects to Rev 14:7 ("worship him that made").
**Cross-references:** 2 Corinthians 3:18; Ephesians 4:24; Genesis 1:26-27.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Image-renewal is described as a return to the original creation pattern. Giving glory to the Creator (Rev 14:7) includes being renewed in His image (Col 3:10).

### 1 John 3:1-3
**Context:** The eschatological completion of transformation.
**Direct statement:** "We shall be like him; for we shall see him as he is" (v.2). The beholding-transformation mechanism reaches its endpoint: seeing Christ produces full likeness. The hope of this transformation produces present purification (v.3).
**Original language:** homoioi auto esometha hoti opsometha auton kathos estin — "we shall be like him because we shall see him as he is." The hoti is causal: seeing produces likeness. This is the 2 Cor 3:18 mechanism taken to completion.
**Cross-references:** 2 Corinthians 3:18 (the process); Romans 8:29 (the predestined goal).
**Relationship to other evidence:** The eschatological endpoint of all glory-giving through transformation. What begins in beholding (2 Cor 3:18) and was purposed in eternity (Rom 8:29) reaches completion at Christ's appearing (1 John 3:2).

### Ephesians 4:24
**Context:** Put off the old man, put on the new.
**Direct statement:** "The new man, which after God is created in righteousness and true holiness."
**Original language:** ton kata theon ktisthenta en dikaiosyne kai hosioteti — "created according to God in righteousness and holiness." The new creation is kata theon — according to God's pattern.
**Cross-references:** Colossians 3:10; 2 Corinthians 3:18.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Parallels Colossians 3:10 — image-restoration language using creation vocabulary.

### James 1:23-24
**Context:** The mirror metaphor for hearing vs. doing the word.
**Direct statement:** "A man beholding his natural face in a glass: for he beholdeth himself, and goeth his way, and straightway forgetteth."
**Original language:** esoptron (G2072, "mirror") is the standard word; Paul uses katoptrizomai (G2734, from katoptron) in 2 Cor 3:18. James's mirror reveals the self; Paul's mirror reveals the Lord's glory.
**Cross-references:** 2 Corinthians 3:18.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The contrast matters: a self-focused mirror produces no change (James); a Christ-focused mirror produces transformation (Paul). The beholding-transformation mechanism works only when the object is God's glory, not the self.

## Focus Area 7: Conduct-Based Glorification (Matt 5:16, 1 Pet 2:12)

### Matthew 5:16
**Context:** The Sermon on the Mount. Believers are salt and light.
**Direct statement:** "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven." The mechanism: visible conduct produces glorification in the observers, not just in the believer.
**Original language:** doxasosin (V-AAS-3P of doxazo G1392, aorist active subjunctive) — a purpose clause: "so that they may glorify." The subjunctive with hopos (G3704) indicates intended result. ta kala erga (kalos G2570 + ergon G2041) — "the beautiful/good works." Kalos (G2570, 102 occurrences in NT) means aesthetically compelling, noble — not just morally correct (agathos). The works attract by their beauty.
**Cross-references:** 1 Peter 2:12 (nearly identical language); Galatians 1:24 ("they glorified God in me").
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is a distinct mode of glory-giving — communal rather than individual. The believer's conduct does not just glorify God directly; it creates conditions for others to glorify God. This connects conduct to the missionary dimension of Rev 14:6-7's universal gospel.

### 1 Peter 2:12
**Context:** Peter echoes Jesus' teaching. Christians live among Gentiles who speak against them.
**Direct statement:** "Having your conversation honest among the Gentiles: that, whereas they speak against you as evildoers, they may by your good works, which they shall behold, glorify God in the day of visitation."
**Original language:** epopteuontes (V-PAP-NPM of epopteuo G2029) — "beholding/observing." The verb suggests sustained observation over time, not a passing glance. doxasosin — same form as Matt 5:16. kalon ergon — same vocabulary. The addition of "in the day of visitation" (en hemera episkopes) introduces a judgment dimension.
**Cross-references:** Matthew 5:16; Revelation 14:7 (judgment follows glory-giving).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Peter adds the eschatological note: the observers will glorify God "in the day of visitation" — on the day of divine reckoning. This parallels Rev 14:7's glory-judgment sequence. Good works today produce glorification at the judgment.

### Galatians 1:24
**Context:** Paul recounts his conversion and its effect on the Judean churches.
**Direct statement:** "They glorified God in me."
**Original language:** edoxazon en emoi ton theon — "they glorified God in me." The preposition en ("in") suggests Paul became the arena where God's glory was visible. His transformed life — persecutor turned preacher — was the "good work" observers beheld.
**Cross-references:** Matthew 5:16; 1 Peter 2:12; 2 Corinthians 3:18 (Paul's transformation is an example of the beholding mechanism at work).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Paul's conversion is a concrete instance of conduct-based glorification. Others saw the transformation and glorified God. The en emoi ("in me") suggests that the believer becomes the location where God's glory is displayed.

### 2 Corinthians 9:13
**Context:** Generous giving by the Corinthians.
**Direct statement:** "They glorify God for your professed subjection unto the gospel of Christ, and for your liberal distribution."
**Original language:** doxazontes ton theon — "glorifying God." Obedience to the gospel and practical generosity become occasions for others to glorify God.
**Cross-references:** Matthew 5:16; 1 Peter 2:12.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Extends conduct-based glorification to specific acts: gospel obedience and financial generosity. These are "beautiful works" (kala erga) that observers translate into glorification.

### 2 Thessalonians 1:12
**Context:** Paul's prayer for the Thessalonians.
**Direct statement:** "That the name of our Lord Jesus Christ may be glorified in you, and ye in him."
**Original language:** endoxasthe (V-APS-3S of endoxazo G1740, aorist passive) — an intensified form of doxazo. Christ is glorified IN believers; believers are glorified IN Christ. Mutual glorification through union.
**Cross-references:** Galatians 1:24; John 17:22.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Adds the reciprocal dimension: glorification runs in both directions when believers are united with Christ.

## Focus Area 8: Bodily Stewardship as Glorification (1 Cor 6:19-20, 10:31, Rom 12:1)

### 1 Corinthians 6:19-20
**Context:** Paul argues against sexual immorality by establishing that the body is God's temple.
**Direct statement:** "Know ye not that your body is the temple of the Holy Ghost which is in you, which ye have of God, and ye are not your own? For ye are bought with a price: therefore glorify God in your body."
**Original language:** doxasate (V-AAM-2P of doxazo G1392, aorist active imperative) — commands immediate, decisive bodily glorification. The aorist imperative calls for a specific, definitive response, not gradual adjustment. egorasthete (V-API-2P of agorazo G59, aorist passive) — "you were bought" (divine passive: God is the implied purchaser). The purchase-price argument grounds the obligation: the body was ransomed and now belongs to its purchaser. somati (N-DSN of soma G4983) — dative of sphere, "in your body." The body is the arena of glorification. naos (G3485, v.19) — the inner sanctuary where God's presence dwells, not hieron (the outer precinct). Calling the body naos means it is the holy of holies — the most sacred space.
**Cross-references:** 1 Corinthians 10:31 (universalizes the scope); Romans 12:1 (extends to "living sacrifice"); Leviticus 10:3 (those who approach God must glorify Him).
**Relationship to other evidence:** The immediate context is sexual purity (porneia, v.18), but the principle extends further. The purchase-price argument does not limit itself to one type of bodily behavior. The body belongs to God — all bodily conduct is either glorification or defilement of the temple.

### 1 Corinthians 10:31
**Context:** Paul's discussion of eating meat offered to idols.
**Direct statement:** "Whether therefore ye eat, or drink, or whatsoever ye do, do all to the glory of God."
**Original language:** eis doxan theou — "unto the glory of God." The preposition eis indicates the goal/purpose. The "whatsoever ye do" (pan ho ti) universalizes the principle: eating, drinking, everything. There is no area of life excluded from the obligation to glorify God.
**Cross-references:** 1 Corinthians 6:20 (the bodily foundation); Romans 14:7-8 ("none of us liveth to himself").
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse confirms that bodily glorification (1 Cor 6:20) extends beyond sexual purity to all stewardship — diet, drink, every activity. It supports reading the first angel's "give glory" as encompassing all of life, not just worship services.

### Romans 12:1
**Context:** The hinge between Romans' theology (chs.1-11) and ethics (chs.12-16).
**Direct statement:** "Present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable service."
**Original language:** parastesai ta somata humon thysian zosan — "present your bodies a living sacrifice." The latreian (worship/service) vocabulary connects to Rev 14:7's worship command. logiken latreian — "reasonable/logical service." The logos root (G3056) connects rationality to worship — this is not irrational devotion but a logically necessary response to God's mercies. V.2 adds: "be ye transformed (metamorphousthe, present passive imperative of metamorphoo G3339) by the renewing of your mind" — the same metamorphoo verb as 2 Cor 3:18.
**Cross-references:** 1 Corinthians 6:20; 2 Corinthians 3:18 (transformation through the same verb).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Bridges bodily stewardship and the beholding-transformation mechanism. The body is presented as a sacrifice (worship dimension); the mind is renewed through transformation (the same metamorphoo process as 2 Cor 3:18). Both are forms of giving glory.

## Focus Area 9: Fruit-Bearing as Glorification (John 15:8, Phil 1:11)

### John 15:1-8
**Context:** The vine and branches discourse at the Last Supper.
**Direct statement:** "Herein is my Father glorified, that ye bear much fruit" (v.8). Fruit-bearing IS glorification. But fruit is not self-generated: "As the branch cannot bear fruit of itself, except it abide in the vine; no more can ye, except ye abide in me" (v.4). "Without me ye can do nothing" (v.5).
**Original language:** edoxasthe (V-API-3S of doxazo G1392, aorist passive) — "my Father is glorified." The aorist passive is a divine passive: the Father receives glory through the fruit-bearing of disciples. karpon polun (karpos G2590 + polus G4183) — "much fruit." The quantity matters — not token fruit but abundant fruit. menete (present imperative in v.4) — continuous abiding. The prerequisite for fruit-bearing is sustained connection to Christ.
**Cross-references:** Philippians 1:11 (fruits of righteousness through Christ); Galatians 5:22-23 (the fruit of the Spirit); Matthew 5:16 (good works/fruit causing glorification).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Fruit-bearing as glorification confirms that glory-giving is Christ-empowered, not self-generated. The branch produces fruit not through effort but through connection. This aligns with the beholding-transformation mechanism (2 Cor 3:18) — both describe processes where the human role is receptive (abiding/beholding) rather than productive. This resolves Bohr's paradox at the practical level: believers give glory through fruit that Christ generates in them.

### Philippians 1:11
**Context:** Paul's prayer for the Philippians.
**Direct statement:** "Being filled with the fruits of righteousness, which are by Jesus Christ, unto the glory and praise of God."
**Original language:** karpon dikaiosynes ton dia Iesou Christou eis doxan kai epainon theou — "fruits of righteousness through Jesus Christ unto the glory and praise of God." The dia + genitive construction makes Christ the agent/channel. eis doxan = "unto glory" — the purpose of fruit-bearing is God's glorification.
**Cross-references:** John 15:8; Galatians 5:22-23.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The dia Iesou Christou ("through Jesus Christ") confirms what John 15:5 states — fruit is Christ-generated. The eis doxan ("unto the glory") confirms John 15:8 — fruit-bearing's purpose is God's glorification.

## Focus Area 10: The Glory-Judgment Connection

### Revelation 14:7 (glory-judgment clause)
**Direct statement:** The hoti (G3754) clause connects "give glory" to "the hour of his judgment is come." The connection is at least causal: give glory BECAUSE judgment is here. The impending judgment motivates the call to glorify God.
**Original language:** hoti (G3754) can function as causal ("because"), content-declarative ("namely that"), or result. The most natural reading in context is causal: the reason to give glory is that judgment has arrived. elthen (V-2AAI-3S of erchomai G2064) — "has come." The aorist treats the judgment as already having arrived, not as future.
**Cross-references:** Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 (OT template); Acts 17:30-31 (same logic).
**Relationship to other evidence:** The glory-judgment link is consistent across both Testaments. God's judgment vindicates His character (glory), and the announcement of judgment motivates the creature to acknowledge that character.

### Ecclesiastes 12:13-14
**Context:** The conclusion of Ecclesiastes — "the whole duty of man."
**Direct statement:** "Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man. For God shall bring every work into judgment."
**Original language:** eth ha-Elohim yera — "fear God." mishpat (H4941, the Hebrew equivalent of krisis G2920). The structure: fear God → commandments → judgment. The first angel restates this universally and adds "give glory" as the relational dimension between fear and worship.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7 (the same structure expanded); Psalm 50:3-6.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the OT template. The first angel did not invent this structure — he universalized what Ecclesiastes had already established as "the whole duty of man."

### Acts 17:30-31
**Context:** Paul at Athens, addressing the Areopagus.
**Direct statement:** "God now commandeth all men every where to repent: Because he hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world."
**Original language:** nun parangellei tois anthropois pantas pantachou metanoein — "now commands all men everywhere to repent." dioti estesen hemeran — "because he has appointed a day." The same logic as Rev 14:7: universal command + because judgment is coming.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7; Ecclesiastes 12:13-14.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Paul uses identical logic to the first angel: God's right as Creator (v.24, "God that made the world") grounds His command to repent, and judgment is the motivation. The Creator-right-to-judge argument is the theological link between glory-giving and judgment.

### Psalm 50:3-6
**Context:** A theophanic judgment scene. God comes to judge His covenant people.
**Direct statement:** "Our God shall come, and shall not keep silence... He shall call to the heavens from above, and to the earth, that he may judge his people."
**Original language:** lishpot ammo — "to judge his people." The heavens declare God's righteousness (tsidqo) — His character is vindicated through judgment.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7; Psalm 50:23 ("whoso offereth praise glorifieth me").
**Relationship to other evidence:** The OT judgment-glory connection prefigures Rev 14:7. In Psalm 50, judgment and glorification belong together: God judges (v.4), the heavens declare His righteousness (v.6), and praise glorifies Him (v.23).

## Focus Area 11: Negative Examples — Consequences of Refusing to Glorify God

### Daniel 5:23
**Context:** Belshazzar's feast. Daniel delivers the indictment: Belshazzar praised gods of silver, gold, brass, iron, wood, and stone, but "the God in whose hand thy breath is, and whose are all thy ways, hast thou not glorified."
**Direct statement:** Failure to glorify the God who sustains your breath brings immediate judgment. Belshazzar gave glory to dead idols instead of the living God.
**Original language:** la hadarta — "you have not glorified." The God who holds Belshazzar's breath is the Creator God — directly paralleling Rev 14:7's "worship him that made." The idol description ("which see not, nor hear, nor know") echoes Rev 9:20.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7; Revelation 9:20-21; Acts 12:23.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Belshazzar is a case study of the Rom 1:21-23 pattern: knew God (through Daniel's earlier witness) → refused to glorify → worshipped images → judgment. His story is the Rom 1 cycle compressed into one night.

### Acts 12:23
**Context:** Herod Agrippa I accepts divine acclamation ("the voice of a god, and not of a man") without redirecting glory to God.
**Direct statement:** "Immediately the angel of the Lord smote him, because he gave not God the glory: and he was eaten of worms, and gave up the ghost."
**Original language:** ouk edoken ten doxan to theo — "he did not give the glory to God." edoken (V-AAI-3S of didomi G1325) — the same verb as Rev 14:7's dote. This is the most direct NT parallel to the dote doxan construction: failure to didomi + doxa + dative = immediate divine judgment.
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7; Daniel 5:23.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Herod's death illustrates the severity of glory-theft. He did not oppose God or blaspheme — he simply failed to redirect glory to its rightful owner. The judgment was immediate.

### Leviticus 10:3
**Context:** Nadab and Abihu offer "strange fire" and are consumed.
**Direct statement:** "I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the people I will be glorified."
**Original language:** ekkabbed (Niphal imperfect 1s of kabad H3513) — "I will be glorified." The parallel between "sanctified" (eqqadesh) and "glorified" (ekkabbed) equates holiness and glory in worship. Approaching God requires giving Him glory — unauthorized worship is failure to glorify.
**Cross-references:** 1 Corinthians 6:19 (body as naos — approaching God's presence); Revelation 14:7 (worship the Creator).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Nadab and Abihu illustrate that worship which does not glorify God on His terms is not merely useless but dangerous. The first angel's call to "worship him that made" (Rev 14:7) specifies the object and manner of true worship.

### Numbers 20:12
**Context:** Moses strikes the rock instead of speaking to it.
**Direct statement:** "Because ye believed me not, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel."
**Original language:** lo he'emantem bi lehaqdiysheniy — "you did not believe me, to sanctify me." Unbelief = failure to sanctify/glorify. The charge frames Moses' disobedience as a failure of faith that prevented God from being glorified before the people.
**Cross-references:** Romans 4:20 (Abraham's faith AS glory-giving — the positive counterpart); Hebrews 11:6 ("without faith it is impossible to please him").
**Relationship to other evidence:** Moses' failure confirms the inverse of Abraham's faith-glory connection: if believing = glorifying (Rom 4:20), then disbelieving = failing to glorify (Num 20:12). The two passages form a positive-negative pair.

### Malachi 2:2 (covered under Focus Area 2)
Curse for not giving glory — directly parallels Rev 16:9 refusal.

### Revelation 16:9 (covered under Focus Area 4)
The eschatological refusal to give glory — the plagues answer the rejected gospel call.

## Focus Area 12: Abraham's Faith as Glory-Giving (Rom 4:20) and the Faith-Glory Connection

### Romans 4:20
**Context:** Abraham as the model of justifying faith. Against all physical evidence, he trusted God's promise.
**Direct statement:** "He staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief; but was strong in faith, giving glory to God."
**Original language:** dous (V-2AAP-NSM of didomi G1325, aorist active participle) doxan (N-ASF of doxa G1391) to theo (N-DSM) — "giving glory to God." This is the SAME construction as Rev 14:7 (dote auto doxan) but with an aorist participle instead of imperative. The participle is circumstantial: it describes HOW Abraham was strengthened — by trusting the promise. Faith = glory-giving. enedynamothe (V-API-3S of endynamoo G1743, aorist passive) — "was strengthened" (divine passive: God did the strengthening). te pistei (N-DSF of pistis G4102, instrumental dative) — "by faith."
**Cross-references:** Revelation 14:7 (same construction); Numbers 20:12 (unbelief as failure to glorify); Hebrews 11:6.
**Cross-references with the series:** Study 3am-05 established that "fear God" (the first imperative) includes trusting God's word. This passage shows that "give glory" (the second imperative) is accomplished through that same trust.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is one of the most significant findings of the study. The same didomi + doxa + dative construction used in Rev 14:7 appears in Rom 4:20 to describe Abraham's faith. Faith is not merely the precondition for glorifying God — faith IS glorifying God. Trusting God's word when evidence contradicts it acknowledges God's character (His faithfulness, His power) and gives Him the weight/substance He is due.

## Supporting Verses (Grouped Analysis)

### Psalmic Glory-Giving Commands (1 Chr 16:28-29; Psa 22:23; Psa 86:9, 12; Psa 115:1; Psa 99:9)
These passages form a cluster of OT commands to give glory. They use the habuw/kabad/nathan + kabod + la-YHWH construction that underlies Rev 14:7's dote doxan. 1 Chronicles 16:28-29 commands: "Give unto the LORD glory and strength... worship the LORD in the beauty of holiness." Psalm 22:23 pairs fear with glorify — anticipating the Rev 14:7 triad. Psalm 86:9 envisions universal glorification ("all nations shall come and worship... and shall glorify thy name"). Psalm 86:12 expresses personal resolve ("I will glorify thy name for evermore"). Psalm 115:1 redirects glory from humans to God's name, grounded in mercy and truth (chesed and emeth — the Exod 34:6 attributes). Psalm 99:9 grounds glorification in holiness, connecting to Isa 6:3. Together these establish that giving glory to God is a persistent OT obligation, not an invention of Revelation 14.

### Praise as Glorification (Psa 50:23)
"Whoso offereth praise glorifieth me." The equation is direct: todah (from yadah H3034) = kabad (H3513). Praise IS glorification. This connects the praise/confession vocabulary (yadah) to the glory/honor vocabulary (kabad), confirming their overlap.

### Glorifying God for Judgments (Isa 25:3)
"The strong people glorify thee, the city of the terrible nations shall fear thee." Even powerful nations glorify God when they witness His judgments. This connects glorification directly to the judgment motif — paralleling Rev 14:7.

### Isaiah Eschatological Glory (Isa 42:12; Isa 60:1-2; Isa 60:21)
Isaiah 42:12 commands: "Let them give glory unto the LORD, and declare his praise in the islands." The universality ("in the islands") parallels Rev 14:6. Isaiah 60:1-2 portrays the glory of the LORD rising on His people while darkness covers the earth. Isaiah 60:21 reveals the purpose of all divine blessings: "that I may be glorified" (lehithpa'er). All blessings aim at glorification.

### Glory as Divine Attribute (Rom 6:4; Rom 8:18; Rom 9:23; 2 Pet 1:3-4)
Romans 6:4: Christ raised "by the glory of the Father" — doxa here means omnipotence, a divine attribute. Romans 8:18: future glory outweighs present suffering. Romans 9:23: believers are "vessels of mercy, prepared unto glory." 2 Peter 1:3-4: God has called believers "to glory and virtue" and offered participation in "divine nature." These passages show that glory is not only something given to God but something God gives to believers — glory flows both ways, and what believers receive they are then positioned to give.

### Doxological Passages (Rom 11:36; Eph 1:12; Rev 5:13)
Romans 11:36: "To whom be glory for ever." Ephesians 1:12: "That we should be to the praise of his glory." Revelation 5:13: every creature ascribes "glory and power" to God and the Lamb. These doxological formulas represent glory-giving in its most concentrated verbal form — ascribing glory through worship and proclamation.

### United and Corporate Glorification (Rom 15:6; 1 Pet 4:11)
Romans 15:6: "with one mind and one mouth glorify God." 1 Peter 4:11: "that God in all things may be glorified through Jesus Christ." Corporate glory-giving requires both doctrinal unity and christological mediation.

### The Glory Transmission Chain (Heb 1:3; Heb 2:10; Col 1:15; 1 Th 2:12)
Hebrews 1:3: Christ is the "brightness of God's glory." Hebrews 2:10: God is "bringing many sons unto glory." Colossians 1:15: Christ is "the image of the invisible God." 1 Thessalonians 2:12: believers are "called unto his kingdom and glory." The chain: God's glory radiates through Christ (Heb 1:3) → Christ is the visible image (Col 1:15) → many sons are brought to glory (Heb 2:10) → believers are called into that glory (1 Th 2:12).

### Joy, Suffering, and Glory (1 Pet 1:8; 1 Pet 4:14-16)
1 Peter 1:8: "joy unspeakable and full of glory" (dedoxasmene, perfect passive of doxazo — the joy itself is glorified). 1 Peter 4:14-16: the "spirit of glory" rests on those reproached for Christ's name. Suffering as a Christian is an occasion to "glorify God on this behalf." Glory-giving is not limited to pleasant circumstances.

### Observational Glorification (Luke 2:14; Luke 17:15-18; Acts 4:21)
Luke 2:14: angels glorify God at Christ's birth — the heavenly model. Luke 17:15-18: only one of ten healed lepers returns "to give glory to God" (dounai doxan to theo — the same construction as Rev 14:7 and 16:9). Gratitude is a mode of glory-giving; ingratitude is the refusal. Acts 4:21: the people glorified God for the healing miracle. These show that glory-giving is a spontaneous response to God's works — not merely commanded but naturally produced when God's acts are recognized.

### The Beast Anti-Glory and Eschatological Fulfillment (Rev 14:11; Rev 21:11, 23; Rev 18:1)
Revelation 14:11: no rest for beast-worshippers — the ceaseless unrest of those who give glory to the beast instead of God. Revelation 21:11, 23: the New Jerusalem has "the glory of God" and needs no sun because "the glory of God did lighten it." Revelation 18:1: the earth "lightened with his glory" — the fourth angel's message. This is the eschatological fulfillment of Habakkuk 2:14 and Isaiah 6:3. In the new earth, the command to give glory is no longer needed because glory fills creation visibly and permanently.

### Creation's Declaration (Psa 19:1)
"The heavens declare the glory of God." Creation itself gives glory to God by displaying His attributes — the non-verbal testimony that Romans 1:19-20 says leaves humanity "without excuse."

## Patterns Identified

1. **Glory = Character, not merely radiance.** The Exodus 33-34 equation (Moses asks for glory, God proclaims character) is the interpretive key. Every subsequent glory passage is consistent with this equation.

2. **The didomi + doxa construction tracks through both Testaments.** Hebrew: nathan/sim + kabod + le-YHWH (Josh 7:19, Mal 2:2, 1 Chr 16:28). Greek: didomi + doxa + dative (Rev 14:7, 16:9, 11:13, Rom 4:20, Acts 12:23, John 9:24, Luke 17:18). The consistency of this construction shows it is a recognized biblical idiom, not an ad hoc phrase.

3. **Glory-giving is multidimensional.** The study identifies at least seven distinct modes: (a) confession/truth-telling (Josh 7:19, John 9:24), (b) character acknowledgment (Isa 42:12, Exod 34:5-7), (c) visible conduct (Matt 5:16, 1 Pet 2:12), (d) fruit-bearing (John 15:8, Phil 1:11), (e) bodily stewardship (1 Cor 6:20, 10:31), (f) beholding-transformation (2 Cor 3:18), (g) completed work (John 17:4). All seven are modes of one reality, not competing definitions.

4. **Glory must be received before it can be given.** Romans 3:23 establishes the deficit; John 17:22 and 2 Cor 3:18 establish the supply chain. The paradox is real but resolved: believers give glory they have received through Christ.

5. **The Rev 14:7/16:9 inclusio frames the entire seal-plague narrative.** The same three lexemes (didomi + autos + doxa) appear in the command (14:7) and the refusal (16:9), with only the mood changed (imperative → infinitive). This is a precise verbal echo, not merely a thematic parallel.

6. **Faith IS glory-giving.** Romans 4:20 uses the dote doxan construction to describe Abraham's faith. Numbers 20:12 confirms the inverse: unbelief = failure to glorify. This means the faith-glory connection is structural, not incidental.

7. **The Creator-right-to-judge logic links glory and judgment.** Revelation 14:7, Ecclesiastes 12:13-14, and Acts 17:30-31 all use the same structure: God commands response → because judgment is coming → grounded in His identity as Creator.

## Word Study Integration

The kabod→doxa bridge (H3519→G1391) is the linguistic foundation of the entire study. Kabod (214 occurrences) carries the root meaning of weight/heaviness from kabad (H3513, 141 occurrences). The LXX rendered kabod as doxa (168 occurrences) in 170 co-occurrences, infusing the Greek word with meanings it did not carry in classical usage. Classical doxa meant "opinion/reputation"; biblical doxa means weight, substance, character, radiance.

The verb doxazo (G1392, 71 occurrences) appears in concentrated form in John's Gospel (16 of 54 unique verses, or 30%), especially in chapters 12-17. The edoxasa of John 17:4 and the edoxasan of Romans 1:21 use the same verb in opposite directions: Christ glorified (edoxasa), humanity refused to glorify (ouk edoxasan).

The didomi (G1325, 430 occurrences) + doxa construction proves more significant than expected. It appears in Rev 14:7 (dote, imperative), Rev 16:9 (dounai, infinitive), Rev 11:13 (edokan, indicative), Rom 4:20 (dous, participle), Acts 12:23 (ouk edoken, negative indicative), John 9:24 (dos, imperative), and Luke 17:18 (dounai, infinitive). The Hebrew equivalent nathan/sim + kabod appears in Josh 7:19 (sim), Mal 2:2 (latheth), 1 Chr 16:28-29 (habuw), and Psa 115:1 (ten). This construction is a recognized cross-testament idiom meaning "give God the full weight/honor/recognition He deserves."

The hapax legomenon katoptrizomai (G2734, 1 occurrence) in 2 Cor 3:18 carries the specific sense of "beholding as in a mirror." The middle voice favors the active "beholding" rather than the passive "reflecting." The present tense indicates ongoing action. Its pairing with metamorphoo (G3339, 4 occurrences total — Matt 17:2, Mark 9:2, Rom 12:2, 2 Cor 3:18) places the believer's transformation in the same category as Christ's transfiguration: real, radical change of essential form (morphe), not merely outward appearance (schema).

Metanoeo (G3340, 36 occurrences) in Rev 16:9 ("they repented not to give him glory") shows that repentance and glory-giving are connected. The refusal to repent IS the refusal to glorify. This connects to yadah (H3034, 136 occurrences) where confession (Hithpael) and praise (Hiphil) are two sides of the same act.

## Cross-Testament Connections

1. **Exodus 33-34 → John 1:14 → 2 Cor 4:6:** Moses' request to see God's glory, fulfilled in the character proclamation (Exod 34:5-7), is re-enacted in the incarnation ("we beheld his glory, full of grace and truth") and further extended into every believer's experience ("the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ").

2. **Joshua 7:19 → John 9:24 → Revelation 14:7:** The confession idiom (sim/ten kabod → dos doxan → dote doxan) carries from the conquest period through the first century into the apocalyptic proclamation. The judicial-oath formula survived intact across language changes.

3. **Malachi 2:2 → Revelation 16:9:** The curse for refusing to give glory (Mal 2:2) finds its eschatological counterpart in the plagues for refusing to give glory (Rev 16:9). Both use the nathan/didomi + kabod/doxa construction.

4. **Psalm 106:20, Jeremiah 2:11 → Romans 1:23 → Revelation 13:14-15:** The OT pattern of exchanging glory for images (golden calf, Jeremiah's indictment) is systematized by Paul (Rom 1:23) and projected eschatologically in Revelation (the beast-image). The first angel's call to give glory to God opposes this trajectory directly.

5. **Exodus 34:29-35 → 2 Corinthians 3:18:** Moses' shining face after encountering God's glory is the OT type; the believer's progressive transformation through beholding is the NT antitype. But the antitype surpasses the type: Moses' glory faded; the believer's glory increases ("from glory to glory").

6. **Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 → Revelation 14:7 → Acts 17:30-31:** The OT template (fear God → commandments → judgment) is universalized by the first angel and paralleled by Paul at Athens. The structure persists across dispensations.

## Difficult or Complicating Passages

### Romans 3:23 — The Glory-Deficit Paradox
If all have sinned and fall short of God's glory, how can anyone obey the command to "give glory"? The resolution from 2 Cor 3:18 (glory received through beholding) and John 17:22 (glory transmitted from Christ) is theologically coherent, but it means glory-giving is not a human accomplishment — it is a grace-enabled response. This complicates any reading that treats "give glory" as a self-generated moral achievement.

### The Hoti Clause in Rev 14:7 — Causal or Declarative?
If hoti is causal ("give glory BECAUSE judgment has come"), then judgment motivates glory-giving. If content-declarative ("namely that the hour of judgment has come"), then the judgment announcement IS part of the gospel content. The two readings produce different theological emphases but are not mutually exclusive. The causal reading fits the grammar more naturally, but the declarative reading fits the context (the angel is proclaiming what the gospel says).

### Katoptrizomai — Beholding or Reflecting?
If "beholding" (favored by Meyer and most modern scholars), the believer is the active subject gazing at Christ's glory. If "reflecting" (Chrysostom), the believer is a passive surface mirroring glory to others. The middle voice and the contextual parallel with Moses (who beheld God's glory and then reflected it) suggest that both are happening: beholding produces reflecting. The debate does not undermine the core point — sustained engagement with divine glory produces transformation — but it affects whether the emphasis falls on reception or transmission.

### The Scope of 1 Cor 6:20 — Sexual Purity Only, or All Bodily Stewardship?
The immediate context is sexual purity (porneia, v.18). But the purchase-price argument (v.20a) and the universal scope of 1 Cor 10:31 ("whatsoever ye do") suggest broader application. The tension is between respecting the immediate context and recognizing the principle's reach. The most honest reading acknowledges that Paul's specific argument addresses sexual purity but his principle extends to all bodily conduct, as he himself demonstrates two chapters later.

### John 15:8 — Aorist Passive as Gnomic or Proleptic?
edoxasthe (aorist passive) could be gnomic (a timeless truth: "the Father IS glorified whenever fruit is borne") or proleptic (anticipating the result: "the Father WILL BE glorified when you bear fruit"). The gnomic reading makes fruit-bearing a standing principle; the proleptic reading makes it a future promise. The theological difference is minimal — either way, fruit-bearing glorifies the Father.

## Preliminary Synthesis

The weight of evidence points in a clear direction: "give glory to him" (dote auto doxan, Rev 14:7) is not a single act but a comprehensive reorientation of life toward acknowledging who God is. The kabod/doxa semantic bridge establishes that glory means the full weight and substance of God's character. The confession idiom (Josh 7:19 → John 9:24) adds the dimension of truth-telling — acknowledging God's rightness. Christ's model (John 17:4) shows that glorification equals completing the assigned work. The beholding-transformation mechanism (2 Cor 3:18) explains how human beings who lack glory (Rom 3:23) can give glory — by receiving it through sustained contemplation of Christ. The conduct passages (Matt 5:16, 1 Pet 2:12) add the communal dimension — believers' lives become arenas where others see and glorify God. The bodily stewardship passages (1 Cor 6:20, 10:31) extend glory-giving into all physical life. The fruit-bearing passages (John 15:8, Phil 1:11) make clear that the glory given is Christ-generated, not self-produced. Abraham's faith (Rom 4:20) identifies believing God as a primary mode of glorifying Him.

The Romans 1:18-25 inverse framework confirms all of this negatively. The failure described there — knew God, refused to glorify, exchanged glory for images, worshipped the creature — is the exact condition the first angel addresses. The Rev 14:7/16:9 verbal echo creates an inclusio around the seal-plague narrative: the gospel call to give glory (14:7) is answered by the refusal to give glory (16:9), using the same three lexemes in both passages.

The answer to the study question is therefore: "give glory to him" means all of the above — confession, character acknowledgment, conduct, fruit-bearing, bodily stewardship, beholding-transformation, and completed work — because glory-giving is not a single action but the comprehensive, Spirit-empowered response of the whole person to the full character of God. The judgment announcement follows because the Creator who reveals His character has the right to evaluate how His creatures respond to that revelation.
