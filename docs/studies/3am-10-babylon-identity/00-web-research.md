# Web Research Findings

## Study Question
What is Babylon in Revelation 14:8? Is it Rome, a past empire, a future rebuilt city, or a religious system? Why does the harlotry vocabulary (porneia, porne) require a prior covenant relationship with God? What does the zanah/porneia word chain from OT prophets (Ezek 16, 23, Hosea 2) reveal? What does the mother-daughter structure (Rev 17:5) reveal? How does the balal etymology (Gen 11:9) establish mixture/confusion? Address preterist (Rome) and futurist (rebuilt city) readings. Show why the harlot-bride bracket structure (Rev 17:1 / 21:9) requires a symbolic religious-system reading.

## Harlot Vocabulary and Covenant Requirement

### Zanah in Ezekiel 16 and 23
**Sources:** https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezekiel-16/; https://www.preceptaustin.org/ezekiel_1615-34; https://www.bibleoutlines.com/ezekiel-231-49-two-sisters-of-harlotry-and-their-horrible-judgment/
**Key findings:**
- The verb zanah (root znh) and derivatives occur twenty-one times in Ezekiel 16 alone describing Jerusalem's unfaithfulness
- Although na'aph (adultery) is more fitting for covenant infidelity, zanah (harlotry) offers "a more forceful rhetorical tool"
- Ezekiel 16 presents God as a devoted husband married to an adulterous wife — the metaphor presupposes covenant marriage (Exod 19-24, Sinai covenant)
- Ezekiel 23 extends the metaphor with Oholah (Samaria) and Oholibah (Jerusalem) as two sisters of harlotry
- "Behind the allegory is the common metaphorical language of ancient international treaties and of Israelite prophets that likened unfaithfulness to the Lord to adultery and prostitution"
- Cultural context: imagery specific to marriage practice in ancient Israel — a God who as husband "entered into a covenant and lavished benefits only to see them used to promote an idolatrous lifestyle"

### Zanah in Hosea
**Sources:** https://medium.com/@horatio10as/when-prophets-become-the-message-dafa698d0afc; https://www.blueletterbible.org/lexicon/g4202/kjv/tr/0-1/
**Key findings:**
- Hebrew construction in Hosea 1:2: ki-zanoh tizneh ha'aretz — doubling of the verb zanah emphasizing the land's infidelity
- Hosea interpreted Israel's political involvement with Assyria negatively as "deserting him in favor of lovers"
- Israel committed both porneia (as God's betrothed virgin) and adultery (as God's bride) by worshiping idols
- The harlotry metaphor requires a prior relationship — one cannot be unfaithful to someone to whom one was never committed

### Porneia in NT/Revelation
**Sources:** https://biblehub.com/commentaries/revelation/14-8.htm; https://apologeticspress.org/fornication-porneia-defined-in-new-testament-greek-lexicons/
**Key findings:**
- Porneia defined as "harlotry (including adultery and incest); figuratively, idolatry"
- Meyer's commentary on Rev 14:8: "The porneia is the idolatry practised with great Babylon, the all-ruling secular power"
- Gill: "by her 'fornication' is meant the idolatry of the church of Rome; so the idolatry of Israel and Judah is often expressed in the Old Testament by fornication"
- The term consistently denotes idolatry in apocalyptic context

## Balal Etymology and Babylon's Name

### Dyck, Andrew — "Babel or Babylon" (Jewish Bible Quarterly)
**URL:** https://jbqnew.jewishbible.org/assets/Uploads/454/jbq_454_dyckbabel.pdf
**Key claims:**
- Hebrew balal encompasses both "confusion" and "mixture" — the mixing of languages creates confusion
- Biblical writers transformed a Mesopotamian toponym into a theological statement about human pride and divine judgment
- Sophisticated use of etymological wordplay for religious purposes rather than historical etymology

### Abarim Publications — Etymology of Babel
**URL:** https://www.abarim-publications.com/Meaning/Babel.html
**Key claims:**
- Name derives from Sumerian Bab-ilim ("Gate of the Deity"); Hebrew Babel and Greek Babylon reference this
- Genesis 11:9 wordplay: "YHWH balalled the speech" — the verb balal describes mixing something with oil until saturation
- The verb balal means "to fill something with oil until it can absorb no more and begins to reject excess"
- Related nouns: belil (rich animal feed mixture), shabbelul (snail, appearing saturated)
- The Akkadian "Gate of God" meaning contrasts with the Hebrew "confusion/mixture" meaning — the text employs deliberate theological irony

### Wikipedia — Tower of Babel
**URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tower_of_Babel
**Key claims:**
- Scholars understand balal as "a satirical word play," taking advantage of phonetic similarity to Akkadian Babel/Babylon
- Babylonians understood their city name as bab-ili ("Gate of God"); the Hebrew author reinterprets this as balal ("confusion/mixture")
- Augustine took the Genesis wordplay as serious etymology: "Babylon means Confusion"
- The theological point: what humans call "the gate of God" (self-exaltation), God calls "confusion" (judgment)

| Element | Rev 17:1-3 (Harlot) | Rev 21:9-10 (Bride) |

| Angel | "one of the seven angels who had the seven bowls" | "one of the seven angels who had the seven bowls" |

**Scholarly observations:**
- Bauckham: the four "in the Spirit" markers (1:10; 4:2; 17:3; 21:10) create the book's major structural divisions
- The identical angel and formula create deliberate contrast — the same messenger delivers opposite revelations
- The wilderness vs. mountain contrast echoes OT prophetic geography (wilderness = judgment; mountain = God's presence)
- Structural implication: if the bride is a symbolic community (the church), then the harlot must also be a symbolic community (apostate religion), not a literal geographic city

## Summary of Scholarly Landscape

The identity of Babylon in Revelation is one of the most debated questions in Revelation studies, with five major positions occupying the scholarly landscape. The **preterist/Rome identification** commands the majority of academic commentators (Aune, Charles, Swete, Caird, Mounce, Beasley-Murray), grounded primarily in the seven-hills imagery, Rome's first-century dominance, and extrabiblical parallels in Jewish apocalyptic literature. The **preterist/Jerusalem identification** (Ford, Chilton, Gentry, Leithart) draws strength from the harlot metaphor's covenant requirement and the explicit identification of "the great city" with the crucifixion site (Rev 11:8). The **idealist/symbolic view** (Beale, Hendriksen) reads Babylon as a trans-historical principle of satanic deception. The **dispensational view** (Walvoord, Ryrie) distinguishes religious Babylon (Rev 17) from commercial Babylon (Rev 18). The **futurist/literal view** (Woods, Thomas) insists on a rebuilt Babylon on the Euphrates.

The scholarly consensus is fragmented, but several observations cut across positions. First, nearly all scholars acknowledge the deliberate structural parallel between Rev 17:1-3 and 21:9-10 (Bauckham's bracket structure), which positions the harlot and bride as antithetical figures. Second, the OT harlot vocabulary (zanah) is overwhelmingly used for entities in covenant relationship with God — primarily Israel/Jerusalem (Ezek 16, 23; Hosea 1-3; Isa 1:21; Jer 2:20), with rare exceptions for treaty-partner cities (Tyre, Nineveh). Third, Beale's observation that the harlot's garments match the LXX description of the high priest's attire suggests a religious/priestly parody rather than a purely political entity. Fourth, the mother-daughter structure (Rev 17:5) is widely recognized as indicating the harlot is an archetype generating multiple historical manifestations, not reducible to any single city.

The key unresolved tension is between the historical-critical approach (which anchors Babylon firmly in first-century Rome based on external evidence) and the theological-literary approach (which reads Babylon as a covenant-violating religious system based on intertextual OT allusions). The balal etymology adds a thematic layer: what claims to be "the gate of God" (bab-ili) is actually "confusion/mixture" (balal) — a theological irony that fits a corrupt religious system better than a secular political empire.
