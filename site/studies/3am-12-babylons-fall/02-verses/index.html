    Verses¶

    Question¶

    Babylon's fall: doubled epesen, proleptic aorist, Isa 21:9 template, Rev 14:8 / Rev 17-18 relationship, Jer 50-51 verbal parallels, 'in one hour' refrain, millstone imagery, progressive fulfillment.

    The three angels' messages — first and second angel announcements¶

    And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters. And there followed another angel, saying, Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication.

    Context: The three angels fly in sequence: first angel proclaims the everlasting gospel with judgment-hour announcement, second angel announces Babylon's fall. The sequential placement — gospel first, then fall — may imply causation (Babylon fell because she rejected the gospel).

    Notes: The doubled epesen epesen (V-2AAI-3S, second aorist active indicative 3rd singular of pipto G4098) is the prophetic/proleptic aorist — grammatically past tense but describing a future event to emphasize certainty. Mirrors Isa 21:9 LXX exactly. The hoti clause ('because') is causal: Babylon fell BECAUSE she made nations drink. pepotiken (V-RAI-3S, perfect active indicative of potizo G4222) indicates completed action with ongoing results — the intoxication was already accomplished when the fall is announced. The genitive chain ek tou oinou tou thumou tes porneias autes compresses Babylon's corrupting influence and God's responsive wrath into a single phrase.

    The watchman announces Babylon's fall — OT template for the doubled formula¶

    The burden of the desert of the sea. As whirlwinds in the south pass through; so it cometh from the desert, from a terrible land. A grievous vision is declared unto me; the treacherous dealer dealeth treacherously, and the spoiler spoileth. Go up, O Elam: besiege, O Media; all the sighing thereof have I made to cease. Therefore are my loins filled with pain: pangs have taken hold upon me, as the pangs of a woman that travaileth: I was bowed down at the hearing of it; I was dismayed at the seeing of it. My heart panted, fearfulness affrighted me: the night of my pleasure hath he turned into fear unto me. Prepare the table, watch in the watchtower, eat, drink: arise, ye princes, and anoint the shield. For thus hath the Lord said unto me, Go, set a watchman, let him declare what he seeth. And he saw a chariot with a couple of horsemen, a chariot of asses, and a chariot of camels; and he hearkened diligently with much heed: And he cried, A lion: My lord, I stand continually upon the watchtower in the daytime, and I am set in my ward whole nights: And, behold, here cometh a chariot of men, with a couple of horsemen. And he answered and said, Babylon is fallen, is fallen; and all the graven images of her gods he hath broken unto the ground. O my threshing, and the corn of my floor: that which I have heard of the LORD of hosts, the God of Israel, have I declared unto you.

    Context: Isaiah's burden of the desert of the sea. The watchman is stationed on the watchtower and announces what he sees. The mention of Elam and Media (v.2) ties the oracle to the historical fall of Babylon by Cyrus (Isa 45:1-4). The doubled 'is fallen, is fallen' (naphlah naphlah, Qal Perfect 3fs of naphal H5307) is the direct source for Rev 14:8/18:2.

    Notes: The Hebrew prophetic perfect (qatal) treats the future fall as accomplished fact — the same device that becomes the prophetic aorist in Greek. naphlah naphlah babel — the doubling is epizeuxis (immediate repetition for emphasis). The watchman vocabulary cluster: tsaphah (H6822, look out — v.6), shamar (H8104, watch), amad (H5975, stand — v.8), nagad (H5046, declare — v.6). Compare with Hab 2:1 where the prophet stands on his watch to see what God will say.

    Expanded proclamation of Babylon's fall — earth-illuminating angel¶

    And after these things I saw another angel come down from heaven, having great power; and the earth was lightened with his glory. And he cried mightily with a strong voice, saying, Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird. For all nations have drunk of the wine of the wrath of her fornication, and the kings of the earth have committed fornication with her, and the merchants of the earth are waxed rich through the abundance of her delicacies.

    Context: Rev 18:1 echoes Ezek 43:2 where God's returning glory causes the earth to shine (ephotisthe he ge ek tes doxes autou). This angel has authority beyond the second angel of 14:8. Rev 18:2 ADDS to 14:8: Babylon has become (egeneto, V-2ADI-3S) the dwelling of demons (katoiketerion daimonion), haunt of unclean spirits, cage of unclean birds — drawing on Isa 13:21-22 and Isa 34:13-14. Rev 18:3 expands the affected parties: nations, kings, merchants.

    Notes: The same doubled epesen epesen appears (V-2AAI-3S x2), confirming verbatim repetition from 14:8. The desolation imagery added in 18:2 (demons, unclean spirits, unclean birds) draws from Isa 13:21-22 (wild beasts, owls, satyrs), Isa 34:13-14 (dragons, owls, screech owl, satyr), and Jer 50:39 (wild beasts of the desert). The expansion from 14:8 to 18:2 suggests progressive deepening — Babylon's condition worsens from announced fall to demon-dwelling desolation. The emporoi (G1713, merchants) of 18:3 connect to Isa 23:8 LXX where Tyre's traders are called merchants as 'rulers of the earth.'

    Come out of her my people — separation call and double recompense¶

    And I heard another voice from heaven, saying, Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues. For her sins have reached unto heaven, and God hath remembered her iniquities. Reward her even as she rewarded you, and double unto her double according to her works: in the cup which she hath filled fill to her double. How much she hath glorified herself, and lived deliciously, so much torment and sorrow give her: for she saith in her heart, I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow. Therefore shall her plagues come in one day, death, and mourning, and famine; and she shall be utterly burned with fire: for strong is the Lord God who judgeth her.

    Context: The 'come out' call (Exelthate, aorist active imperative of exerchomai G1831) draws directly from Jer 51:6,45 and Isa 48:20. The possessive 'my people' (ho laos mou) means God claims these people as His own even while in Babylon. The queen hubris soliloquy (v.7) is a direct quotation of Isa 47:7-8. The 'in one day' (mia hemera, v.8) parallels Isa 47:9 'in one day' (beyom echad).

    Notes: Rev 18:6 diplosate ta dipla ('double unto her double') echoes Isa 40:2 and Jer 16:18 but INVERTS the sense: Isa 40:2 announces comfort after suffering double for sins; Rev 18:6 commands double judgment. Rev 18:7 Kathemai basilissa (V-PNI-1S + N-NSF, 'I sit as queen') and chera ouk eimi ('am no widow') with ou me ido (emphatic double negation + aorist subjunctive, 'shall never see mourning') — this is a verbatim echo of Isa 47:7-8. Also compare Zeph 2:15 where Nineveh says 'I am, and there is none beside me.' The burned with fire (katakauthēsetai en pyri, v.8) echoes Jer 51:58 and Rev 17:16.

    Kings' lament — first 'in one hour' refrain¶

    And the kings of the earth, who have committed fornication and lived deliciously with her, shall bewail her, and lament for her, when they shall see the smoke of her burning, Standing afar off for the fear of her torment, saying, Alas, alas, that great city Babylon, that mighty city! for in one hour is thy judgment come.

    Context: First of three mourning groups. Kings stand 'afar off' (apo makrothen, G575+G3113) — spatial motif of fear-at-a-distance. mia hora (G1520+G5610, 'one hour') + elthen (V-2AAI-3S, aorist of erchomai) + krisis (G2920, judgment). The kings' focus is on JUDGMENT.

    Notes: The apo makrothen motif appears in all three laments (vv.10, 15, 17b). The ouai ouai ('alas, alas') doubled interjection parallels the doubled epesen — twofold emphasis on the calamity. First 'in one hour' refrain focuses on judgment (krisis).

    Merchants' lament — second 'in one hour,' 28-item cargo list¶

    And the merchants of the earth shall weep and mourn over her; for no man buyeth their merchandise any more: The merchandise of gold, and silver, and precious stones, and of pearls, and fine linen, and purple, and silk, and scarlet, and all thyine wood, and all manner vessels of ivory, and all manner vessels of most precious wood, and of brass, and iron, and marble, And cinnamon, and odours, and ointments, and frankincense, and wine, and oil, and fine flour, and wheat, and beasts, and sheep, and horses, and chariots, and slaves, and souls of men. And the fruits that thy soul lusted after are departed from thee, and all things which were dainty and goodly are departed from thee, and thou shalt find them no more at all. The merchants of these things, which were made rich by her, shall stand afar off for the fear of her torment, weeping and wailing, And saying, Alas, alas, that great city, that was clothed in fine linen, and purple, and scarlet, and decked with gold, and precious stones, and pearls! For in one hour so great riches is come to nought.

    Context: Second mourning group. Merchants' lament with the 28-item cargo list (Rev 18:12-13) modeled on Ezek 27:12-22 (Tyre's trade inventory). Bauckham argues 4x7 structure. The list descends from most precious (gold) to most damnable (somaton kai psuchas anthropon — 'bodies and souls of men'). Second 'in one hour' (mia hora) refrain + eremothē (V-API-3S, aorist passive of eremoō G2049, 'made desolate'). Focus is on RICHES.

    Notes: Counting the cargo items: (1) gold, (2) silver, (3) precious stones, (4) pearls, (5) fine linen, (6) purple, (7) silk, (8) scarlet — GROUP 1: luxury raw materials. (9) thyine wood, (10) ivory vessels, (11) precious wood vessels, (12) brass, (13) iron, (14) marble — GROUP 2: construction/craft materials. (15) cinnamon, (16) odours, (17) ointments, (18) frankincense, (19) wine, (20) oil, (21) fine flour — GROUP 3: consumables. (22) wheat, (23) beasts, (24) sheep, (25) horses, (26) chariots, (27) slaves (somaton), (28) souls of men (psuchas anthropon) — GROUP 4: living commodities. The 28-item/4x7 structure is confirmed. The somaton kai psuchas anthropon is the climactic indictment: trafficking in human beings.

    Shipmasters' lament — third 'in one hour,' Tyre echoes¶

    And every shipmaster, and all the company in ships, and sailors, and as many as trade by sea, stood afar off, And cried when they saw the smoke of her burning, saying, What city is like unto this great city! And they cast dust on their heads, and cried, weeping and wailing, saying, Alas, alas, that great city, wherein were made rich all that had ships in the sea by reason of her costliness! for in one hour is she made desolate.

    Context: Third mourning group. Shipmasters echo the Tyre lament of Ezek 27:29-32 where mariners come down from ships, stand on land, cast dust on heads, and lament 'What city is like Tyrus?' (Ezek 27:32). Third 'in one hour' refrain + eremothē (same form as 18:17). Focus is on DESOLATION.

    Notes: The three mourning groups progress: kings focus on judgment (krisis), merchants on riches (ploutos), shipmasters on desolation (eremothē). All three stand apo makrothen (afar off). All three say ouai ouai (alas, alas). The self-interested nature of the mourning is notable — no spiritual repentance, only material loss.

    Heaven rejoices — vindication of God's people¶

    Rejoice over her, thou heaven, and ye holy apostles and prophets; for God hath avenged you on her.

    Context: Dramatic reversal from lamentation to vindication. This completes the altar vindication arc from Rev 6:10 ('How long?') through this verse to 18:24. Echoes Jer 51:48 where heaven and earth sing for Babylon's judgment.

    Notes: The verb ekrinen (G2919, 'hath avenged/judged') in the aorist shows the judgment is viewed as accomplished. This connects directly to the martyrs' cry in Rev 6:10 and to the altar vindication in 18:24.

    Millstone cast into sea — sevenfold 'no more at all' and blood of prophets¶

    And a mighty angel took up a stone like a great millstone, and cast it into the sea, saying, Thus with violence shall that great city Babylon be thrown down, and shall be found no more at all. And the voice of harpers, and musicians, and of pipers, and trumpeters, shall be heard no more at all in thee; and no craftsman, of whatsoever craft he be, shall be found any more in thee; and the sound of a millstone shall be heard no more at all in thee; And the light of a candle shall shine no more at all in thee; and the voice of the bridegroom and of the bride shall be heard no more at all in thee: for thy merchants were the great men of the earth; for by thy sorceries were all nations deceived. And in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth.

    Context: The millstone imagery draws on Jer 51:63-64 where Seraiah binds a stone (eben H68) to a scroll and casts it into the Euphrates. Rev shifts from stone to millstone (mylinos G3458), from Euphrates to sea, from prophetic symbolic action to angelic execution. The sevenfold 'no more at all' (ou me + aorist subjunctive) uses the strongest Greek negation. The silenced musicians echo Ezek 26:13 (Tyre); the silenced bridegroom/bride echoes Jer 7:34 and 25:10 (Jerusalem warnings).

    Notes: Seven items silenced: (1) Babylon herself found no more (v.21), (2) musicians (v.22a), (3) craftsmen (v.22b), (4) sound of millstone (v.22c), (5) light of lamp (v.23a), (6) voice of bridegroom and bride (v.23b), (7) pharmakeia deceiving nations (v.23c). Rev 18:23 pharmakeia (G5331, N-DSF) + eplanesthesan (V-API-3P, aorist passive of planao G4105) — 'by thy sorceries were all nations deceived' — parallels Nahum 3:4 LXX where Nineveh is called 'mistress of sorceries who sells nations through her whoredoms and families through her witchcrafts.' Rev 18:24 connects to Matt 23:34-35 — blood of prophets from Abel to Zechariah found in Babylon, expanding Babylon's guilt beyond one generation.

    The harlot portrait — golden cup, drunk with blood of saints¶

    And there came one of the seven angels which had the seven vials, and talked with me, saying unto me, Come hither; I will shew unto thee the judgment of the great whore that sitteth upon many waters: With whom the kings of the earth have committed fornication, and the inhabitants of the earth have been made drunk with the wine of her fornication. So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness: and I saw a woman sit upon a scarlet coloured beast, full of names of blasphemy, having seven heads and ten horns. And the woman was arrayed in purple and scarlet colour, and decked with gold and precious stones and pearls, having a golden cup in her hand full of abominations and filthiness of her fornication: And upon her forehead was a name written, MYSTERY, BABYLON THE GREAT, THE MOTHER OF HARLOTS AND ABOMINATIONS OF THE EARTH. And I saw the woman drunken with the blood of the saints, and with the blood of the martyrs of Jesus: and when I saw her, I wondered with great admiration.

    Context: The harlot portrait establishes Babylon's identity before the fall narrative. Sits on many waters (17:1) = Jer 51:13. Golden cup (17:4) = Jer 51:7. Drunk with blood (17:6) = Jer 51:49. The mother-daughter structure (METER TON PORNON) shows Babylon generates offspring.

    Notes: The golden cup (poterion chrysoun) with bdelygmata (abominations, G946) and akatharta (unclean things, G169) links to the categories of false teaching identified in 3am-11. The harlot-bride bracket structure (Rev 17:1-3 paralleling Rev 21:9-10 with identical angel-introduction formula) requires symbolic interpretation.

    Waters = peoples; ten horns destroy the harlot¶

    And he saith unto me, The waters which thou sawest, where the whore sitteth, are peoples, and multitudes, and nations, and tongues. And the ten horns which thou sawest upon the beast, these shall hate the whore, and shall make her desolate and naked, and shall eat her flesh, and burn her with fire. For God hath put in their hearts to fulfil his will, and to agree, and give their kingdom unto the beast, until the words of God shall be fulfilled. And the woman which thou sawest is that great city, which reigneth over the kings of the earth.

    Context: The interpretive key for 'many waters' = peoples/nations, from Jer 51:13. The ten horns turn against the harlot — Babylon is destroyed by her own allies. Burned with fire (17:16) = Jer 51:58.

    Notes: The self-destruction mechanism — Babylon's own political allies turn on her — is a key feature of the fall. This internal dissolution parallels how historical Babylon fell (through the defection of subject peoples).

    Hallelujah response — smoke rises forever, vindication¶

    And after these things I heard a great voice of much people in heaven, saying, Alleluia; Salvation, and glory, and honour, and power, unto the Lord our God: For true and righteous are his judgments: for he hath judged the great whore, which did corrupt the earth with her fornication, and hath avenged the blood of his servants at her hand. And again they said, Alleluia. And her smoke rose up for ever and ever.

    Context: The heavenly response to Babylon's fall. The fourfold Hallelujah (19:1,3,4,6) — only occurrence of 'Alleluia' in the NT. The blood-of-servants vindication completes the arc from Rev 6:10.

    Notes: The smoke rising 'for ever and ever' (eis tous aionas ton aionon) echoes Isa 34:10 (Edom's judgment: 'the smoke thereof shall go up for ever'). This permanence language confirms the finality of the fall.

    Queen hubris and sudden judgment — 'in one day' with sorceries¶

    And thou saidst, I shall be a lady for ever: so that thou didst not lay these things to thy heart, neither didst remember the latter end of it. Therefore hear now this, thou that art given to pleasures, that dwellest carelessly, that sayest in thine heart, I am, and none else beside me; I shall not sit as a widow, neither shall I know the loss of children: But these two things shall come to thee in a moment in one day, the loss of children, and widowhood: they shall come upon thee in their perfection for the multitude of thy sorceries, and for the great abundance of thine enchantments.

    Context: Direct verbal source for Rev 18:7's queen soliloquy. The sorceries (kesheph, H3785) connect to Rev 18:23's pharmakeia (G5331).

    Notes: Verbal correspondence: Isa 47:7 'I shall be a lady for ever' → Rev 18:7 'I sit a queen'; Isa 47:8 'I shall not sit as a widow' → Rev 18:7 'am no widow'; Isa 47:8 'neither shall I know loss of children' → Rev 18:7 'shall see no sorrow'. The sorceries (kesheph) in Isa 47:9,12 connect to Nahum 3:4 (same word) and to Rev 18:23 (pharmakeia). Also Isa 47:11 'desolation shall come upon thee suddenly' (pitom) — suddenness vocabulary.

    Sudden destruction and ineffective sorcery¶

    Context: The suddenness of destruction (pitom, H6597) in v.11 parallels Jer 51:8 ('Babylon is suddenly fallen'). The sorceries are ineffective — they cannot prevent the judgment. Background for Rev 18:23's pharmakeia and the permanence of the fall.

    Notes: The irony: Babylon's very sorceries that deceived nations cannot save her from God's judgment. This reversal pattern — the deceiver deceived, the intoxicator judged — runs throughout the fall narrative.

    Come out of Babylon — golden cup — suddenly fallen¶

    Notes: Jer 51:7: kowce zahab (cup of gold, H3563+H2091) → poterion chrysoun (golden cup) in Rev 17:4. meshakkeret (Piel participle of shakar H7937, 'making drunk') → pepotiken (perfect of potizo G4222, 'has made to drink'). The nations drink of her wine (miiyeynah shathu goyim) → Rev 14:8 'made all nations drink.' Jer 51:8: pitom (H6597, 'suddenly') naphlah ('is fallen') → Rev 14:8 epesen epesen. Jer 51:9: 'her judgment reacheth unto heaven' → Rev 18:5 'her sins have reached unto heaven.'

    Babylon on many waters — source for Rev 17:1¶

    O thou that dwellest upon many waters, abundant in treasures, thine end is come, and the measure of thy covetousness.

    Context: Direct source for Rev 17:1 ('the great whore that sitteth upon many waters'). The 'many waters' (mayim rabbim, H4325+H7227) refers historically to the Euphrates canal system; Rev 17:15 interprets symbolically as 'peoples, and multitudes, and nations, and tongues.'

    Notes: The covetousness/treasure vocabulary connects to the economic critique in Rev 18.

    Come out my people — heaven rejoices — blood of slain¶

    My people, go ye out of the midst of her, and deliver ye every man his soul from the fierce anger of the LORD. And lest your heart faint, and ye fear for the rumour that shall be heard in the land; a rumour shall both come one year, and after that in another year shall come a rumour, and violence in the land, ruler against ruler. As Babylon hath caused the slain of Israel to fall, so at Babylon shall fall the slain of all the earth.

    Context: Jer 51:45 'My people, go ye out' → Rev 18:4 'Come out of her, my people.' Jer 51:48 heaven/earth sing → Rev 18:20 'Rejoice over her, thou heaven.' Jer 51:49 blood of Israel's slain → Rev 18:24 blood of prophets and saints.

    Notes: The possessive 'my people' (ammi) in both Jer 51:45 and Rev 18:4 is theologically significant: God claims these people as His own EVEN WHILE they remain in Babylon. The separation call implies there are genuine believers inside Babylon's system.

    Stone cast into Euphrates — OT source for millstone imagery¶

    And it shall be, when thou hast made an end of reading this book, that thou shalt bind a stone to it, and cast it into the midst of Euphrates: And thou shalt say, Thus shall Babylon sink, and shall not rise from the evil that I will bring upon her: and they shall be weary. Thus far are the words of Jeremiah.

    Context: Seraiah's prophetic symbolic action: binding a stone (eben, H68) to a scroll (sepher, H5612) and casting it into the Euphrates (perath, H6578). Rev 18:21 adapts this: angel lifts a stone 'like a great millstone' (lithos hos mylinon megan) and casts it into the sea (thalassa). The shift from stone→millstone and river→sea is theologically loaded.

    Notes: Key differences from Jer 51:63-64 to Rev 18:21: (1) Stone (eben) → millstone (mylinos) — the millstone evokes Jesus' warning in Matt 18:6/Mark 9:42 about causing little ones to stumble; (2) Euphrates → sea — the sea in Revelation often represents nations/chaos; (3) Human prophet Seraiah → angelic agent; (4) Binding to scroll → no scroll (the prophetic word has already been spoken). Jer 51:64: tishqa (Qal imperfect of shaqa H8257, 'shall sink') + lo taqum (shall not rise, qum H6965) — permanent destruction. Compare Rev 18:21 ou me eurethē ('shall be found no more at all').

    Recompense according to deeds — parallels Rev 18:6¶

    Call together the archers against Babylon: all ye that bend the bow, camp against it round about; let none thereof escape: recompense her according to her work; according to all that she hath done, do unto her: for she hath been proud against the LORD, against the Holy One of Israel.

    Context: Direct verbal parallel to Rev 18:6 ('Reward her even as she rewarded you, and double unto her double according to her works'). The recompense-according-to-deeds principle.

    Notes: Jer 50:29 gamal (H1580, recompense) → Rev 18:6 apodidomi (G591, reward/pay back). The same lex talionis principle operates in both passages.

    Permanent desolation — never inhabited, like Sodom¶

    Therefore the wild beasts of the desert with the wild beasts of the islands shall dwell there, and the owls shall dwell therein: and it shall be no more inhabited for ever; neither shall it be dwelt in from generation to generation. As God overthrew Sodom and Gomorrah and the neighbour cities thereof, saith the LORD; so shall no man abide there, neither shall any son of man dwell therein.

    Context: Wild beasts and owls inhabiting the desolated Babylon → Rev 18:2 (habitation of devils, unclean spirits, unclean birds). The Sodom comparison adds moral-judgment weight. 'No more inhabited for ever' parallels the sevenfold 'no more at all' of Rev 18:21-23.

    Notes: This passage also connects to Isa 13:19-22 (Babylon as Sodom, wild beasts and owls dwelling in ruins). The desolation imagery forms a consistent tradition across Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Revelation.

    Cup-of-wrath origin — God gives the cup to nations¶

    For thus saith the LORD God of Israel unto me; Take the wine cup of this fury at my hand, and cause all the nations, to whom I send thee, to drink it. And they shall drink, and be moved, and be mad, because of the sword that I will send among them. Then took I the cup at the LORD'S hand, and made all the nations to drink, unto whom the LORD had sent me:

    Context: The origin point of the cup-of-wrath trajectory: God personally gives the cup of fury (kowce yayin hachema, cup of wine of fury) to Jeremiah to make nations drink. This becomes the template for both Jer 51:7 (Babylon as cup-bearer) and Rev 14:8/16:19 (Babylon distributes then receives the cup).

    Notes: The full cup-of-wrath trajectory: Psa 75:8 (cup in God's hand) → Isa 51:17 (cup of trembling) → Jer 25:15-17 (God gives cup to nations) → Jer 51:7 (Babylon IS a golden cup) → Rev 14:8 (Babylon makes nations drink) → Rev 14:10 (beast worshippers drink God's wrath) → Rev 16:19 (Babylon receives cup of fierceness of wrath) → Rev 18:6 (double recompense in the cup) → Rev 19:15 (winepress of fierceness and wrath of Almighty God). The intensification from thumos alone (14:8) to thumos + orge + pantokrator (19:15) is the longest genitive chain in Revelation.

    Cup of wrath in God's hand — earliest trajectory reference¶

    For in the hand of the LORD there is a cup, and the wine is red; it is full of mixture; and he poureth out of the same: but the dregs thereof, all the wicked of the earth shall wring them out, and drink them.

    Context: The earliest explicit cup-of-wrath passage. The cup is IN GOD'S HAND — He controls both its distribution and its contents. The 'wine is red' (yayin chamar) and 'full of mixture' (mesek) establish the intoxication/judgment imagery that Jeremiah and Revelation develop.

    Notes: The dregs (shemarim) that the wicked must drain parallels Rev 14:10 'poured out without mixture' (akraton, unmixed) — an intensification from mixed wine to undiluted wrath.

    Babylon remembered — cup of fierceness of wrath, terminal genitive swap¶

    And the great city was divided into three parts, and the cities of the nations fell: and great Babylon came in remembrance before God, to give unto her the cup of the wine of the fierceness of his wrath.

    Context: The polarity flip from 3am-11: Rev 14:8 ends with 'her fornication' (tes porneias autes); Rev 16:19 substitutes 'his wrath' (tes orges autou). Same genitive structure, terminal genitive swapped. The cup that intoxicates IS the cup that judges.

    Notes: The genitive chain: tou poteriou tou oinou tou thumou tes orges autou — 'the cup of the wine of the fierceness of his wrath.' This is the polarity flip endpoint: the same cup shifts from Babylon's fornication to God's wrath.

    Nineveh as harlot with sorceries — parallels Rev 18:23¶

    Notes: Matt 18:6 mylos onikos — a large millstone turned by a donkey. Mark 9:42 uses the same image. The sea (thalassa) destination is shared between Jesus' warning and Rev 18:21's millstone cast into the sea.

    Context: Edom's desolation imagery — smoke going up forever (v.10, echoed in Rev 19:3), unclean birds, dragons, satyrs. The 'habitation of dragons' (naveh tannim) parallels Rev 18:2 'habitation of devils.'

    Notes: Isa 34:10 'the smoke thereof shall go up for ever' → Rev 19:3 'her smoke rose up for ever and ever.'     Nineveh desolation — hubris paralleling Rev 18:7¶

    And he will stretch out his hand against the north, and destroy Assyria; and will make Nineveh a desolation, and dry like a wilderness.     Context: Nineveh's hubris soliloquy: 'I am, and there is none beside me' — the same self-exalting formula found in Isa 47:8 (Babylon) and Rev 18:7 (eschatological Babylon). The desolation with beasts parallels Rev 18:2.

    Notes: The hubris formula 'I am, and there is none beside me' appears across three judged civilizations: Nineveh (Zeph 2:15), Babylon (Isa 47:8), and Revelation's Babylon (Rev 18:7). This is the self-deification speech formula analyzed in the grammar reference entry.

    Woe to him who makes neighbors drink — cup imagery¶

    Notes: The reversal pattern (intoxicator becomes intoxicated by God's wrath) is the same dynamic operating in Rev 14:8 → 16:19 — Babylon makes nations drink, then God makes Babylon drink.

    Beast worshippers drink God's wrath — cup shifts from Babylon to God¶

    The same shall drink of the wine of the wrath of God, which is poured out without mixture into the cup of his indignation; and he shall be tormented with fire and brimstone in the presence of the holy angels, and in the presence of the Lamb:

    Context: The third angel's warning. The cup shifts from Babylon's wine (14:8) to God's wrath (14:10). The genitive chain: ek tou oinou tou thumou tou theou tou kekerasmenou akratou en to poterio tes orges autou — wine of the wrath of God, poured unmixed into the cup of his indignation.

    Notes: The transition from 14:8 to 14:10: same 'wine of wrath' vocabulary (oinou tou thumou), but the source shifts from Babylon (autes, 'her') to God (tou theou, 'of God'). The akraton ('without mixture/unmixed') intensifies — God's wrath is undiluted.

    In one day — death, mourning, famine, fire¶

    Therefore shall her plagues come in one day, death, and mourning, and famine; and she shall be utterly burned with fire: for strong is the Lord God who judgeth her.

    Context: The 'in one day' (mia hemera, G1520+G2250) construction parallels Isa 47:9 'in one day' (beyom echad). This is distinct from the 'in one hour' (mia hora) refrain in vv.10,17,19. The escalation from 'day' to 'hour' emphasizes increasing suddenness.

    Notes: The progression: Isa 47:9 'in a moment, in one day' → Rev 18:8 'in one day' → Rev 18:10,17,19 'in one hour.' Each step compresses the timeframe further, heightening the shock of judgment.

    Cup of trembling — Jerusalem drinks first¶

    Awake, awake, stand up, O Jerusalem, which hast drunk at the hand of the LORD the cup of his fury; thou hast drunken the dregs of the cup of trembling, and wrung them out.

    Context: Jerusalem drinks the cup of wrath first, then the cup passes to her tormentors. This is a midpoint in the cup-of-wrath trajectory between Psa 75:8 and Jer 25:15.

    Notes: The cup trajectory: the same cup that judged Jerusalem is now turned toward Babylon — a reversal pattern.

    Golden cup in the LORD's hand — Babylon as cup-bearer¶

    Babylon hath been a golden cup in the LORD'S hand, that made all the earth drunken: the nations have drunken of her wine; therefore the nations are mad.

    Context: The critical link: Babylon is not merely holding a cup — she IS a golden cup in God's hand. She is an instrument of both intoxication and judgment. This dual function explains the polarity flip between Rev 14:8 and 16:19.

    Notes: Hebrew parsed: kowce zahab (H3563+H2091, cup of gold) babel (H894) beyad YHWH (H3027+H3068, in hand of YHWH) meshakkeret (H7937 Piel Ptcp fs, making drunk) kol ha-aretz (whole earth). miyeynah (H3196+3fs, of her wine) shathu (H8354 Qal Perf 3p, have drunk) goyim (H1471 nations). yitholelu (H1984 Hithpael Impf 3mp, are mad/infatuated) goyim.

    Context: Tyre's merchants as 'princes' (sarim) and 'honourable of the earth' — the LXX renders this with emporoi (G1713) as rulers of the earth, which Rev 18:23 directly echoes ('thy merchants were the great men of the earth').     Notes: Isa 23:8 LXX emporoi → Rev 18:23 emporoi. The merchants-as-rulers vocabulary directly connects Tyre to Revelation's Babylon. The fornication language applied to Tyre's commercial dealings shows that the metaphor of economic corruption as fornication predates Revelation.

    Double recompense — inversion of Isa 40:2¶

    Reward her even as she rewarded you, and double unto her double according to her works: in the cup which she hath filled fill to her double.

    Context: The diplosate ta dipla ('double unto her double') construction echoes Isa 40:2 ('she hath received of the LORD's hand double for all her sins') and Jer 16:18 ('recompense their iniquity and their sin double'). But Revelation INVERTS the sense: Isa 40:2 announces comfort after punishment; Rev 18:6 commands punishment.

    Notes: The inversion from comfort-after-double-punishment (Isa 40:2) to double-punishment-as-recompense (Rev 18:6) is theologically significant: what Jerusalem received as suffering, Babylon deserves as judgment.

    Full cargo list — 28 items culminating in human trafficking¶

    The merchandise of gold, and silver, and precious stones, and of pearls, and fine linen, and purple, and silk, and scarlet, and all thyine wood, and all manner vessels of ivory, and all manner vessels of most precious wood, and of brass, and iron, and marble, And cinnamon, and odours, and ointments, and frankincense, and wine, and oil, and fine flour, and wheat, and beasts, and sheep, and horses, and chariots, and slaves, and souls of men.

    Context: Bauckham's 4x7 structure verified: Group 1 (luxury raw materials, 8 items: gold through scarlet), Group 2 (construction/craft, 6 items: thyine wood through marble), Group 3 (consumables, 7 items: cinnamon through wheat), Group 4 (living commodities, 7 items: beasts through souls of men). The list climaxes in somaton kai psuchas anthropon — physical bodies AND human souls.

    Notes: The 28-item count is confirmed. The somaton (G4983, bodies) kai psuchas (G5590, souls) anthropon (G444, of men) distinguishes physical slavery from spiritual captivity. This is the anti-Jubilee indictment — trafficking in human beings is the ultimate sin of the commercial system.

    Blood of prophets found in her — altar vindication arc completion¶

    Context: Completes the altar vindication arc: martyrs' cry (Rev 6:10) → vindication announced (Rev 18:20) → blood found (Rev 18:24). Connects to Matt 23:34-35 where Jesus says 'upon you may come all the righteous blood shed upon the earth, from the blood of righteous Abel unto the blood of Zacharias.'

    Notes: The phrase 'all that were slain upon the earth' (panton ton esphagmenon epi tes ges) expands Babylon's guilt beyond one generation — she is responsible for the accumulated bloodguilt of all persecuting systems throughout history. Haskell (SSP 312.2) connects this to Matt 23:34-35.

    Silenced bride in Jerusalem — OT source for Rev 18:23¶

    Then will I cause to cease from the cities of Judah, and from the streets of Jerusalem, the voice of mirth, and the voice of gladness, the voice of the bridegroom, and the voice of the bride: for the land shall be desolate.

    Context: Jeremiah's warning to JERUSALEM — the silencing of bridegroom and bride as a sign of judgment. Rev 18:23 applies this same imagery to Babylon. Significantly, Babylon's final state is worse than what Jeremiah warned for Jerusalem.

    Notes: Also Jer 25:10 includes 'the sound of the millstones, and the light of the candle' — precisely the additional items in Rev 18:22-23. The combination of bridegroom/bride + millstone + lamp is unique to Jer 25:10 and Rev 18:22-23, confirming direct literary dependence.

    Sound of millstones and light of candle — closest parallel to Rev 18:22-23¶

    Moreover I will take from them the voice of mirth, and the voice of gladness, the voice of the bridegroom, and the voice of the bride, the sound of the millstones, and the light of the candle.

    Context: The most specific verbal parallel to Rev 18:22-23. Five items silenced: (1) voice of mirth, (2) voice of gladness, (3) voice of bridegroom, (4) voice of bride, (5) sound of millstones, (6) light of candle. Rev 18:22-23 adds musicians, craftsmen, and expands to sevenfold structure.

    Notes: This Jeremiah passage was a warning to JERUSALEM about coming Babylonian judgment. Revelation applies it to Babylon herself — the instrument of God's judgment on Jerusalem becomes the object of God's final judgment. The reversal is complete.

    Sixth bowl — Euphrates dried up, typological echo of Cyrus's conquest¶

    And the sixth angel poured out his vial upon the great river Euphrates; and the water thereof was dried up, that the way of the kings of the east might be prepared.

    Context: Typological echo of Cyrus's conquest: the historical Cyrus diverted the Euphrates to enter Babylon (cf. Isa 44:27-28; 45:1-4). The 'kings of the east' in Revelation correspond typologically to Cyrus. LaRondelle's fifth typological element: dried Euphrates as God's judgment on Babylon's protective 'wall.'

    Notes: LaRondelle's typological framework: (1) Babylon as enemy/oppressor — Rev 17-18; (2) Euphrates as protective wall — Rev 16:12; (3) dried Euphrates = God's judgment — Rev 16:12; (4) Cyrus/kings from east — Rev 16:12; (5) faithful remnant — Rev 18:4 'my people, come out.'

    Winepress of fierceness and wrath of Almighty God — longest genitive chain¶

    And out of his mouth goeth a sharp sword, that with it he should smite the nations: and he shall rule them with a rod of iron: and he treadeth the winepress of the fierceness and wrath of Almighty God.

    Context: The climax of the cup-of-wrath trajectory. The genitive chain: ton lenon tou oinou tou thumou tes orges tou theou tou pantokratoros — 'the winepress of the wine of the fierceness of the wrath of God the Almighty.' The longest genitive chain in Revelation, intensifying from thumos alone (14:8) through thumos + orge (16:19) to thumos + orge + pantokrator (19:15).

    Notes: The progression of wrath intensification across Revelation: Rev 14:8 thumos porneias (wrath of fornication) → Rev 14:10 thumos theou (wrath of God) → Rev 16:19 thumos orges (fierceness of wrath) → Rev 19:15 thumos orges theou pantokratoros (fierceness of wrath of God Almighty). Each stage adds weight.

    Crux text — the second angel's doubled epesen and genitive chain (standalone)¶

    And there followed another angel, saying, Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication.

    Context: The second angel's message in isolation. The doubled epesen epesen (V-2AAI-3S x2) is the prophetic aorist declaring Babylon's fall as accomplished certainty. The hoti clause is causal. pepotiken (V-RAI-3S, perfect of potizo G4222) shows completed action with ongoing results.

    Notes: Standalone entry for the crux verse. The genitive chain ek tou oinou tou thumou tes porneias autes runs four genitives deep. The terminal genitive (autes, 'her') flips to autou ('his') in Rev 16:19.

    Come out of her my people — separation call with sins reaching heaven¶

    And I heard another voice from heaven, saying, Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues. For her sins have reached unto heaven, and God hath remembered her iniquities.

    Context: The separation call draws directly from Jer 51:6,45 and Isa 48:20. The possessive 'my people' (ho laos mou) means God claims these people as His own even while they remain in Babylon. Sins reaching heaven echoes Jer 51:9.

    Notes: Exelthate (V-2AAM-2P, aorist active imperative of exerchomai G1831) commands a decisive, complete act of separation. The aorist imperative demands immediate, whole action — not gradual withdrawal.

    OT template — watchman announces naphlah naphlah babel (standalone)¶

    And, behold, here cometh a chariot of men, [with] a couple of horsemen. And he answered and said, Babylon is fallen, is fallen; and all the graven images of her gods he hath broken unto the ground.

    Context: The OT source verse for the doubled fall formula. naphlah naphlah babel — Qal Perfect 3fs of naphal (H5307) doubled. The prophetic perfect treats future fall as accomplished. Direct verbal source for Rev 14:8 and 18:2 via LXX.

    Notes: Standalone entry for this critical source verse. The watchman context (Isa 21:6-8) establishes the pattern of a prophetic sentinel who sees and announces what is coming.

    Go forth from Babylon — come-out call in Isaiah¶

    Go ye forth of Babylon, flee ye from the Chaldeans, with a voice of singing declare ye, tell this, utter it [even] to the end of the earth; say ye, The LORD hath redeemed his servant Jacob.

    Context: Isaiah's come-out call, part of the chain from Jer 50:8, 51:6, 51:45 to Rev 18:4. The joyful tone ('voice of singing') contrasts with the urgency of Jeremiah's calls and the judgment context of Revelation's call.

    Notes: Supporting verse in the come-out chain. The LXX yatsa-to-exerchomai bridge (489 co-occurrences) confirms lexical dependence across the tradition.

    Set up watchmen against Babylon — watchman motif¶

    Set up the standard upon the walls of Babylon, make the watch strong, set up the watchmen, prepare the ambushes: for the LORD hath both devised and done that which he spake against the inhabitants of Babylon.

    Context: The watchman motif applied to Babylon's own walls. Connects to Isa 21:6-8 where God commands Isaiah to set a watchman. Here the watchmen are set against Babylon, preparing for her fall.

    Notes: Supporting verse for the watchman motif. The vocabulary cluster: mishmereth (watch), shomer (watchman), standard — same semantic field as Isa 21:6-8.

    Babylon shall become heaps — desolation vocabulary¶

    And Babylon shall become heaps, a dwellingplace for dragons, an astonishment, and an hissing, without an inhabitant.

    Context: Desolation vocabulary: heaps (galim), dragons (tannim), astonishment (shammah), hissing (shreqah), without inhabitant. This feeds into Rev 18:2's desolation imagery.

    Notes: Supporting verse. The dwellingplace-for-dragons language parallels Rev 18:2 habitation of demons. The tannim (H8565/H8577) are wild creatures inhabiting ruins.

    Perpetual sleep and walls burned with fire — permanence of fall¶

    And I will make drunk her princes, and her wise [men], her captains, and her rulers, and her mighty men: and they shall sleep a perpetual sleep, and not wake, saith the King, whose name [is] the LORD of hosts. Thus saith the LORD of hosts; The broad walls of Babylon shall be utterly broken, and her high gates shall be burned with fire; and the people shall labour in vain, and the folk in the fire, and they shall be weary.

    Context: The permanence of Babylon's fall: perpetual sleep (never to wake), walls utterly broken, gates burned. Jer 51:58 'burned with fire' parallels Rev 17:16 and 18:8.

    Notes: Supporting verse. The 'perpetual sleep' (shenat olam) language denotes irrecoverable destruction — they shall not wake. The burning parallels Rev 17:16 (ten horns burn the harlot) and Rev 18:8 (utterly burned with fire).

    Taunt song against king of Babylon — descent to Sheol¶

    That thou shalt take up this proverb against the king of Babylon, and say, How hath the oppressor ceased! the golden city ceased! The LORD hath broken the staff of the wicked, and the sceptre of the rulers. He who smote the people in wrath with a continual stroke, he that ruled the nations in anger, is persecuted, and none hindereth. The whole earth is at rest, and is quiet: they break forth into singing. Yea, the fir trees rejoice at thee, and the cedars of Lebanon, saying, Since thou art laid down, no feller is come up against us. Hell from beneath is moved for thee to meet thee at thy coming: it stirreth up the dead for thee, even all the chief ones of the earth; it hath raised up from their thrones all the kings of the nations. All they shall speak and say unto thee, Art thou also become weak as we? art thou become like unto us? Thy pomp is brought down to the grave, and the noise of thy viols: the worm is spread under thee, and the worms cover thee.

    Context: The mashal (taunt song) against Babylon's king. The whole earth rejoicing at his fall (v.7) prefigures the heavenly rejoicing at Babylon's fall in Rev 18:20 and 19:1-3. The descent to Sheol is the ultimate reversal of hubris.

    Notes: Supporting verse. The self-deification pattern analyzed in the grammar reference entry on Ezek 28/Isa 14/Rev 18 speech formulas. The mashal genre connects to Hab 2:5-6 (Prinsloo's inner-biblical allusion network).

    Go out of the land of the Chaldeans — first Jeremiah come-out call¶

    Remove out of the midst of Babylon, and go forth out of the land of the Chaldeans, and be as the he goats before the flocks.

    Context: The first of Jeremiah's come-out calls in the Babylon oracle (also 51:6, 51:45). The chain: Jer 50:8 → 51:6 → 51:45 → Rev 18:4.

    Notes: Supporting verse. 'Be as the he goats before the flocks' — lead the way out, don't wait to be told twice.

    Nations mourn at news of Babylon's capture — weeping parallel¶

    At the noise of the taking of Babylon the earth is moved, and the cry is heard among the nations.

    Context: Nations mourning at Babylon's fall parallels the three mourning groups in Rev 18:9-19 (kings, merchants, shipmasters). The earth-shaking nature of the event.

    Notes: Supporting verse. The 'cry heard among the nations' corresponds to the ouai ouai ('alas, alas') refrain in Rev 18:10, 16, 19.

    First angel provides context — fall is consequence of rejecting the everlasting gospel¶

    And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

    Context: The first angel's message provides the framework for the second angel's announcement. The sequential placement — gospel (vv.6-7) then fall (v.8) — suggests the fall is a consequence of rejecting the everlasting gospel. The prophetic aorist elthen ('is come') in v.7 uses the same grammatical device as epesen in v.8.

    Notes: Supporting verse. Both Bohr and EGW claim the fall is a direct consequence of rejecting the first angel's message. The grammar supports this: hoti in v.8 is causal ('because').

    Millstone around neck — parallel to Matt 18:6¶

    And whosoever shall offend one of [these] little ones that believe in me, it is better for him that a millstone were hanged about his neck, and he were cast into the sea.

    Context: Parallel to Matt 18:6. Same mylos onikos (donkey millstone) + thalassa (sea) vocabulary as Rev 18:21. Jesus' warning about causing stumbling connects to Babylon's function of making nations stumble through her sorceries and false teachings.

    Notes: Supporting verse. The millstone-into-sea imagery in both Jesus' warning and Revelation's judgment may be intentionally linked — Babylon causes the ultimate stumbling.

    Cyrus prophecy — God's instrument for Babylon's fall¶

    Thus saith the LORD to his anointed, to Cyrus, whose right hand I have holden, to subdue nations before him; and I will loose the loins of kings, to open before him the two leaved gates; and the gates shall not be shut; I will go before thee, and make the crooked places straight: I will break in pieces the gates of brass, and cut in sunder the bars of iron: And I will give thee the treasures of darkness, and hidden riches of secret places, that thou mayest know that I, the LORD, which call [thee] by thy name, [am] the God of Israel. For Jacob my servant's sake, and Israel mine elect, I have even called thee by thy name: I have surnamed thee, though thou hast not known me.

    Context: God names Cyrus as His 'anointed' (mashiach) — the instrument of Babylon's fall. The opened gates (v.1) connect to Dan 5:30-31 where Babylon falls 'that very night.' LaRondelle's typological framework: Cyrus as type of the kings from the east (Rev 16:12).

    Notes: Supporting verse. The typological connection: Cyrus → kings of the east (Rev 16:12); dried Euphrates → sixth bowl; deliverance of Israel → 'come out of her, my people' (Rev 18:4).

    Nebuchadnezzar besieges Tyre — historical link in prophetic network¶

    For thus saith the Lord GOD; Behold, I will bring upon Tyrus Nebuchadrezzar king of Babylon, a king of kings, from the north, with horses, and with chariots, and with horsemen, and companies, and much people.

    Context: Historical connection between Babylon and Tyre — Nebuchadnezzar besieges Tyre. This establishes the literary-historical basis for the composite Babylon-Tyre imagery in Rev 17-18.

    Notes: Supporting verse. Babylon destroyed Tyre historically; Revelation's Babylon inherits Tyre's commercial sins. The oppressor absorbs the sins of the oppressed.

    Medes stirred against Babylon — historical agent of fall¶

    Behold, I will stir up the Medes against them, which shall not regard silver; and [as for] gold, they shall not delight in it.

    Context: God stirs up the Medes as His instrument against Babylon — connecting to Isa 21:2 (Elam and Media) and Dan 5:28 (kingdom given to Medes and Persians). Part of the historical-fall background for the typological reading.

    Notes: Supporting verse. The Medes' indifference to silver and gold contrasts with Babylon's commercial obsession — a theme Rev 18's cargo list develops.

    Babylon's queen hubris soliloquy (standalone)¶

    How much she hath glorified herself, and lived deliciously, so much torment and sorrow give her: for she saith in her heart, I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow.

    Context: Babylon's self-assessment echoes Isa 47:7-8 verbatim and Zeph 2:15's Nineveh hubris. The Kathemai basilissa (I sit as queen) + chera ouk eimi (am no widow) + penthos ou me ido (shall never see sorrow) formula combines present indicative, negated present, and emphatic double negation + aorist subjunctive.

    Notes: Standalone entry for the hubris soliloquy. The ou me ido construction is the strongest Greek negation — Babylon categorically denies even the possibility of sorrow. The irony: her very certainty guarantees her judgment (v.8 'Therefore shall her plagues come in one day').

    Every one astonished at Babylon's plagues — desolation witness motif¶

    Because of the wrath of the LORD it shall not be inhabited, but it shall be wholly desolate: every one that goeth by Babylon shall be astonished, and hiss at all her plagues.

    Context: The desolation-witness motif: passersby astonished at Babylon's plagues. Connects to the three mourning groups in Rev 18 who stand 'afar off' and witness the destruction with horror.

    Notes: Supporting verse. The 'wrath of the LORD' (ebrath YHWH) as cause of desolation connects to the cup-of-wrath trajectory. The 'hiss at all her plagues' parallels the ouai ouai refrain.
