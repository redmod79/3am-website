    The Everlasting Gospel: What Euangelion Aionion Means in Revelation 14:6¶

    Question¶

    What does "everlasting gospel" (euangelion aionion) mean in Rev 14:6? Is this the same gospel Paul preached, or a different one? What does the anarthrous construction imply? How does euangelisai connect to the OT basar vocabulary chain (Isa 40:9, 52:7, 61:1)? What is the content of a gospel called "everlasting"? How does the imperial euangelia thesis relate? Does it pass the Galatians 1:6-9 test?

    Summary Answer¶

    The euangelion aionion of Revelation 14:6 is the same gospel Paul preached — the one gospel of God's saving action that spans from pre-creation purpose (2 Tim 1:9; Eph 1:4) through patriarchal promise (Gal 3:8) through apostolic proclamation (Rom 1:16) to eschatological announcement (Rev 14:6-7). The anarthrous construction is qualitative, emphasizing the gospel's eternal and all-encompassing character, while still referring to the well-known gospel. Its content — fear God, give glory, judgment hour, worship Creator — appears in Paul's own preaching at Athens (Acts 17:22-31) and matches the OT triad of Ecclesiastes 12:13-14. It passes the Galatians 1:6-9 test because Paul condemns the substitution of works-righteousness for grace, not the inclusion of judgment, creation, or commandment-keeping in gospel content.

    Key Verses¶

    The Everlasting Gospel Proclaimed (Revelation 14:6-7) And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

    Grace Given Before Eternal Times (2 Timothy 1:9) Who hath saved us, and called us with an holy calling, not according to our works, but according to his own purpose and grace, which was given us in Christ Jesus before the world began.

    Paul's Anathema on a Different-in-Kind Gospel (Galatians 1:6-7) I marvel that ye are so soon removed from him that called you into the grace of Christ unto another gospel: Which is not another; but there be some that trouble you, and would pervert the gospel of Christ.

    Judgment According to Paul's Gospel (Romans 2:16) In the day when God shall judge the secrets of men by Jesus Christ according to my gospel.

    Paul Quoting Isaiah's Herald (Romans 10:15) As it is written, How beautiful are the feet of them that preach the gospel of peace, and bring glad tidings of good things!

    The OT Fourfold Herald (Isaiah 52:7) How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace; that bringeth good tidings of good, that publisheth salvation; that saith unto Zion, Thy God reigneth!

    Circumcision Nothing, Commandments Everything (1 Corinthians 7:19) Circumcision is nothing, and uncircumcision is nothing, but the keeping of the commandments of God.

    The Same Gospel Preached to Both Testaments (Hebrews 4:2) For unto us was the gospel preached, as well as unto them: but the word preached did not profit them, not being mixed with faith in them that heard it.

    The Saints Described by Gospel Results (Revelation 14:12) Here is the patience of the saints: here are they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus.

    The Whole Duty of Man (Ecclesiastes 12:13-14) Let us hear the conclusion of the whole matter: Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man. For God shall bring every work into judgment, with every secret thing, whether it be good, or whether it be evil.

    Analysis¶

    1. What Euangelion Aionion Means — The Phrase Itself¶

    Revelation 14:6 is the only verse in Scripture where the phrase "everlasting gospel" appears. It is also the only NT verse where the noun euangelion (G2098) and the verb euangelisai (G2097) co-occur. The cognate construction — "having an everlasting gospel to gospel" — creates a figure of speech (cognate accusative) that intensifies meaning. The gospel-ness of the proclamation is impossible to miss.

    The adjective aionios (G166) appears 71 times in the NT, most often modifying "life" (approximately 43 of 69 verses). Its temporal range is not limited to the future: 2 Timothy 1:9 uses pro chronon aionion ("before times eternal"), explicitly placing God's purpose and grace before creation. Titus 1:2 uses the identical phrase for the promise of eternal life. If aionios can describe pre-creation reality, it does the same in Rev 14:6: the gospel is "everlasting" in both directions — backward into pre-creation purpose and forward into endless effect.

    DeSilva proposed translating euangelion aionion as "an age-long accession announcement," paralleling Vespasian's imperial accession sequence. But this translation fails two tests. First, 2 Tim 1:9 places grace pro chronon aionion — "before eternal times." If aionios means merely "age-long" (pertaining to a single historical period), then grace was given before a single age — which is incoherent. Second, Matt 25:46 applies aionios to both punishment (kolasin aionion) and life (zoen aionion) in the same sentence. If "age-long" means temporary for punishment, it means temporary for life. No interpreter accepts temporary eternal life. Aionios must carry genuine eternal force.

    The Hebrew background strengthens this reading. Aionios is the standard LXX rendering of the Hebrew olam (H5769), which carries the sense of indefinite duration stretching in both temporal directions. The olam-aionios LXX bridge (documented in the TR-owlam-aion translation relationship study) establishes a semantic tradition that Revelation inherits. When John writes euangelion aionion, he uses a word with deep roots in Hebrew eternity-language filtered through the Greek Bible.

    Furthermore, Rev 14:6 is the last occurrence of euangelion in the Bible. The word "gospel" never appears again after this verse. Its final placement in the eschatological proclamation of the first angel makes this the Bible's closing statement about the gospel — it is everlasting.

    2. The Anarthrous Construction — Alford vs. Vincent¶

    Both euangelion and aionion lack the article in Rev 14:6. This has generated two interpretive positions. Alford argues that euangelion had become "so technical" a term that "no argument can be derived" from the missing article — it still means "the gospel." Vincent takes the opposite view, rendering "an eternal Gospel" to emphasize its qualitative and universal character.

    The evidence supports a position that draws from both while accepting neither entirely. Anarthrous euangelion appears elsewhere in the NT — Rom 1:1 (euangelion Theou, "gospel of God"), Gal 1:6 (heteron euangelion, "a different gospel"), Phil 1:27 (tou euangeliou, genitive). In every case, the referent is THE gospel — the well-known message of God's saving action. The anarthrous form does not introduce a new or different gospel. Alford is right that euangelion is technical.

    But Vincent correctly identifies the qualitative force. The anarthrous construction in Greek can emphasize the quality or character of the noun rather than merely identifying it. "Everlasting gospel" does not just label a message; it describes its character. The adjective aionion highlights what KIND of gospel this is — one that stretches across eternity, not one confined to a particular audience, age, or cultural moment. The qualitative reading enriches without changing the referent. It IS the gospel, and it IS everlasting in character.

    The double-anarthrous pattern (both noun and adjective lacking the article) also appears in Heb 13:20: diatheke aionios ("everlasting covenant"). The everlasting covenant and the everlasting gospel share the same grammatical construction, the same adjective, and the same theological reality. The covenant establishes the gospel; the gospel proclaims the covenant.

    3. The Basar-Euangelizo-Euangelion Vocabulary Chain¶

    The most powerful evidence for gospel continuity across the testaments is the vocabulary chain running from Hebrew basar through LXX euangelizo to NT euangelion.

    Hebrew basar (H1319) appears 27 times in 21 verses, with the Piel stem in 96% of occurrences. Five Isaiah passages (40:9, 41:27, 52:7, 60:6, 61:1) define the prophetic herald tradition. In Isa 40:9, the mevasseret (feminine Piel participle) proclaims "Behold your God!" In Isa 52:7, the mevasser (masculine Piel participle) delivers a fourfold announcement: shalom (peace), tov (good), yeshuah (salvation), and "Thy God reigneth!" In Isa 61:1, the Spirit-anointed one is commissioned levassar (Piel infinitive) — to herald good news to the afflicted. The root mashach (to anoint), from which mashiyach (Messiah/Christ) derives, is directly linked to the herald function.

    The LXX translates basar with euangelizo in 18 of 21 occurrences — a near-exclusive equivalence documented with a PMI score of 29.44. This is not a loose correspondence; it is a deliberate and consistent translation choice that created a vocabulary bridge between Hebrew prophecy and Greek gospel.

    The NT confirms this bridge by direct quotation. Paul quotes Isa 52:7 in Rom 10:15, using euangelizomenon agatha ("preaching good things") for Isaiah's mevasser tov. Jesus quotes Isa 61:1 in Luke 4:18, using euangelisasthai for Isaiah's levassar, and declares "This day is this scripture fulfilled in your ears." Peter echoes Isa 52:7 in Acts 10:36, "preaching peace by Jesus Christ" (euangelizomenos eirenen). Paul coins the hapax legomenon proeuangelizomai (G4283) in Gal 3:8 — "gospeled-beforehand" — a word he created specifically to express that Scripture preached the gospel to Abraham before the NT era. The word itself is the argument: if the Scriptures "gospeled-beforehand," then the gospel existed before the apostles.

    The angel of Rev 14:6 stands at the end of this chain. He carries euangelion (the noun) and uses euangelisai (the verb) — both from the same root as Isaiah's basar, both transmitted through the LXX. The chain is: basar (Hebrew verb) → euangelizo (Greek verb via LXX) → euangelion (Greek noun in NT). The angel proclaims the same message that Isaiah's mevasser announced, Jesus fulfilled, and Paul preached. The vocabulary alone makes the case for continuity.

    The OT noun besorah (H1309, 7 occurrences) provides an interesting contrast. All 7 occurrences are in narrative contexts (2 Samuel, 2 Kings) — battlefield tidings, not salvific proclamation. The prophets used the VERB basar for theological content; they did not use the noun besorah. The NT created euangelion as the theological noun — a semantic development from Hebrew verb to Greek noun. When the angel of Rev 14:6 carries euangelion, he carries the noun that the OT never needed because the verb did the work.

    4. The Content of the Everlasting Gospel — What the Angel Actually Says¶

    The legon participle in Rev 14:7 (present active participle of lego, G3004) introduces direct discourse. What follows legon IS the content of the euangelisai from v.6. The first angel's message contains three aorist imperatives — decisive commands demanding immediate response:

    1. Phobethete ton Theon — "Fear God" (aorist passive imperative of phobeo, G5399). This echoes the OT covenant formula. Eccl 12:13: "Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man." The Hebrew yare (H3372) behind phobeo carries the dual sense of reverence and awe. In the context of the beast-worship crisis (Rev 13), "Fear God" means: give reverence to the true God, not the counterfeit.

    2. Dote auto doxan — "Give Him glory" (aorist active imperative of didomi). This connects to the OT covenant-lawsuit tradition. Josh 7:19: "Give glory to the LORD God of Israel." In Revelation's context, glory-giving to God contrasts with the beast's self-glorification (Rev 13:5-6 — "a mouth speaking great things and blasphemies"). Paul calls the gospel "the glorious gospel of Christ" (2 Cor 4:4), using the same doxa (G1391) vocabulary.

    3. Proskynhsate to poiesanti — "Worship the One who made" (aorist active imperative of proskyneo, G4352). The Creator is identified by a fourfold catalog: heaven, earth, sea, fountains of waters. This echoes the creation language of Exod 20:11: "the LORD made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that in them is." The parallel is not exact — Rev 14:7 has "fountains of waters" where Exod 20:11 has "all that in them is" — but the structural overlap is unmistakable. The Creator-worship imperative of the everlasting gospel echoes the Sabbath commandment's creation language.

    These three imperatives are grounded by a causal hoti clause: "for the hour of his judgment is come" (elthen he hora tes kriseos autou). The aorist elthen ("is come") marks the judgment as arrived, not merely anticipated. This is the shift from anticipation to announcement that the 3am-03 study identified: earlier NT preachers spoke of a judgment day to come (Acts 17:31); the first angel speaks of the judgment hour that has arrived.

    The element-by-element correspondence with Paul at Athens (Acts 17:22-31) is exact: (1) Creator God who made the world and all things (Acts 17:24 = Rev 14:7); (2) Universal scope — all nations, all men everywhere (Acts 17:26,30 = Rev 14:6); (3) Judgment — appointed a day to judge the world (Acts 17:31 = Rev 14:7); (4) Repentance — commandeth all men everywhere to repent (Acts 17:30 = Rev 14:7's "Fear God"). If Paul's Athens sermon IS the gospel preached to Gentiles, then Rev 14:7's content IS the gospel.

    5. The Galatians 1:6-9 Test — What Paul Actually Condemns¶

    Paul uses two different Greek words for "another" in Gal 1:6-7. Heteros (G2087) in v.6 means "another of a different kind" — the Galatians are turning to a gospel that is qualitatively different. Allos (G243) in v.7 means "another of the same kind" — and Paul says the false gospel is NOT allo, it does not even deserve the name "gospel." The implication: the false teaching is so radically different from the true gospel that it falls outside the category entirely.

    What is this false gospel? Galatians identifies it with precision. Gal 2:3-5: false brethren demanded circumcision. Gal 5:2-3: "If ye be circumcised, Christ shall profit you nothing." Gal 6:12-13: the troublemakers want to "glory in your flesh." Acts 15:1: "Except ye be circumcised after the manner of Moses, ye cannot be saved." The heresy is making circumcision a salvation requirement — the substitution of human merit for divine grace.

    Paul's anathema (G331, "let him be devoted to destruction") targets this specific perversion: adding a works-righteousness condition to salvation. The double anathema (vv.8-9) extends to anyone, including "an angel from heaven" — universalizing the principle. But universalizing the principle does not universalize the content. Paul condemns gospels that are heteros — different in KIND — from his own. The question for Rev 14 is therefore: does the first angel preach a gospel that replaces grace with works-righteousness?

    The answer is clearly no. The first angel does not mention circumcision or any works-righteousness condition. His message — fear God, give glory, judgment hour, worship Creator — matches Paul's own preaching at Athens element by element (Acts 17:22-31). Paul himself includes judgment in "my gospel" (Rom 2:16). Paul himself affirms commandment-keeping while nullifying circumcision (1 Cor 7:19). Paul himself declares that faith establishes the law (Rom 3:31). If Paul's own teachings include judgment, creation, and commandments without violating Gal 1:6-9, then Rev 14:7's content cannot violate it either.

    The Answering Adventism objection — that the everlasting gospel must be identical to 1 Cor 15:3-4 and any "addition" corrupts the gospel — fails on Paul's own terms. En protois in 1 Cor 15:3 (dative plural of protos, G4413) means "among the first things" or "of first importance." It is a priority statement, not an exclusivity statement. Paul gives a DIFFERENT gospel summary in Rom 1:1-4 (Davidic descent, declared Son of God by resurrection). If 1 Cor 15:3-4 were the complete and exclusive definition of the gospel, Paul himself would be preaching a "different gospel" in Romans — which fails his own test. The gospel is broader than any single summary, and every NT formulation highlights different facets of the same message.

    6. The Imperial Euangelia Parallel — Counter-Proclamation, Not Replacement¶

    The imperial euangelia thesis recognizes that euangelion was a politically charged word in the first-century Roman world. The Priene Calendar Inscription (9 BC) called Augustus's birthday "the beginning of the good tidings (euangelion) for the world." Augustus was called "god manifest" and "savior." When Mark opens his gospel with "The beginning of the gospel (arche tou euangeliou) of Jesus Christ, the Son of God" (Mark 1:1), the echo of the Priene inscription is deliberate. The true euangelion belongs to Jesus, not to Caesar.

    Revelation sustains this counter-imperial pattern throughout. Rev 1:5 calls Jesus "the ruler of the kings of the earth" (ho archon ton basileon tes ges) — directly subverting Roman claims to universal sovereignty. Rev 13:4-8 describes the beast demanding universal worship using the same fourfold formula ("all kindreds, tongues, nations") and the same worship verb (proskyneo) that appear in Rev 14:6-7. Rev 17:14 and 19:16 apply the imperial title "King of kings and Lord of lords" to the Lamb. The entire book of Revelation uses imperial vocabulary — and subverts it.

    DeSilva's contribution is recognizing this context. His error is making it the primary meaning. When he translates euangelion aionion as "an age-long accession announcement," he replaces the theological content with the political frame. But the political frame does not replace the theological content — it reinforces it. The angel's proclamation is BOTH a theological gospel (the biblical message of God's saving action) AND a political counter-proclamation (a declaration that the beast's claims are false and God's kingdom is the true authority). The imperial parallel strengthens, rather than weakens, the identification of euangelion aionion with the biblical gospel. John co-opts the emperor's vocabulary to declare that the true good news belongs to God, not to any earthly power or beastly counterfeit.

    7. Paul's Gospel as Broader Than Any Single Summary¶

    The claim that the gospel equals 1 Cor 15:3-4 and nothing else collapses under the weight of Paul's own diversity. The NT contains at least 22 distinct designations for the gospel (documented in the G2098 word study): gospel of the kingdom (Matt 4:23), gospel of God (Rom 1:1), gospel of Christ (Rom 1:16), gospel of peace (Eph 6:15), gospel of salvation (Eph 1:13), glorious gospel (2 Cor 4:4), mystery of Christ (Eph 3:4), word of truth (2 Cor 6:7), word of life (Phil 2:16), and many more. Each designation reveals a different dimension. If the gospel were a single-point formula, it would not need 22 names.

    Paul's own preaching varies by audience. To Corinth (1 Cor 15:3-4): death, burial, resurrection. To Rome (Rom 1:1-4): Davidic descent, declared Son of God. To Athens (Acts 17:22-31): Creator God, universal scope, judgment, repentance. To the Galatians (Gal 3:8): justification by faith, gospeled-beforehand to Abraham. These are different summaries of the same gospel, not different gospels. If 1 Cor 15:3-4 were the ONLY gospel content, Paul violated his own Gal 1:6-9 test every time he preached something different.

    Jesus himself preached "the gospel of the kingdom" (Mark 1:14-15) before his death, burial, and resurrection had occurred. His gospel content was: the time is fulfilled, the kingdom of God is at hand, repent, and believe. If the gospel is solely the events of death-burial-resurrection, Jesus never preached the gospel during his entire earthly ministry. This is an absurd conclusion that no interpreter can sustain.

    8. Revelation 14:12 as Gospel Result, Not Addition¶

    The saints in Rev 14:12 are described as hoi terountes tas entolas tou Theou kai ten pistin Iesou — "the ones keeping the commandments of God and the faith of Jesus." The articular present active participle terountes (G5083) is substantival, functioning as a descriptor, not an imperative. It identifies who the saints are by what they do. It does not command them.

    The coordination of commandments-of-God and faith-of-Jesus under a single participle means these are not separate identities but a unified description. Pistis Iesou may be subjective genitive (the faithfulness that Jesus demonstrated), objective genitive (faith directed toward Jesus), or a compact genitive encompassing both. The subjective reading connects to the Pauline pistis Christou tradition (Rom 3:22, Gal 2:16) — justification by the faithfulness of Christ.

    Paul's own formulation in Gal 5:6 — pistis di agapes energoumene ("faith working through love") — combines faith with active obedience. Paul's declaration in 1 Cor 7:19 — "Circumcision is nothing, and uncircumcision is nothing, but the keeping of the commandments of God" (alla teresis entolon Theou) — uses the same root (teresis/tereo) as Rev 14:12's terountes. Paul explicitly nullifies circumcision while AFFIRMING commandment-keeping. If Paul can do this without violating his own Gal 1:6-9 anathema, then Rev 14:12 does not violate it either.

    Rev 14:12 is not an addition to the gospel. It is a description of what the gospel produces. The everlasting gospel proclaimed by the first angel creates a people who keep God's commandments and hold the faith of Jesus. This is the gospel's fruit, not its corruption.

    Word Studies¶

    Euangelion (G2098) — 77 NT occurrences in 74 verses across 17 books. The noun "gospel." Rev 14:6 is its last biblical occurrence. The 22+ NT designations show it is a multifaceted message broader than any single summary.

    Euangelizo (G2097) — 61 NT tokens across 52 verses in 12 books. The verb "to preach the gospel." LXX bridge from H1319 basar with PMI score 29.44. Rev 14:6 is the only NT verse where the noun and verb appear together.

    Aionios (G166) — 71 NT occurrences. "Eternal, everlasting." Modifies life (~43 of 69 verses), punishment (Matt 25:46), covenant (Heb 13:20), gospel (Rev 14:6). Reaches backward before creation (2 Tim 1:9; Tit 1:2). DeSilva's "age-long" fails the 2 Tim 1:9 and Matt 25:46 tests.

    Basar (H1319) — 27 tokens, Piel stem in 96%. Five Isaiah passages (40:9, 41:27, 52:7, 60:6, 61:1) define the prophetic herald tradition. LXX renders as euangelizo in 18 of 21 occurrences.

    Heteros (G2087) — 99 NT occurrences. "Another of a different kind." In Gal 1:6, marks the false gospel as qualitatively different from Paul's. Allos (G243) — 160 NT occurrences. "Another of the same kind." In Gal 1:7, clarifies: the false gospel is NOT "another of the same kind."

    Proeuangelizomai (G4283) — Hapax legomenon (Gal 3:8 only). Paul's compound meaning "gospeled-beforehand." Linguistic proof of OT gospel presence.

    Difficult Passages¶

    The Anarthrous Construction and the "Natural Theism" Reading¶

    The Cambridge Bible proposes that Rev 14:7's content — fear God, worship Creator, judgment — is "pure, natural theism," broader than (and different from) Pauline soteriology. The problem: the language of Rev 14:7 is not generic but covenantal. "Fear God" echoes Eccl 12:13 (covenant formula). "Give glory" echoes Josh 7:19 (covenant lawsuit). "Worship the Creator" echoes the Sabbath commandment (Exod 20:11). "The hour of his judgment" uses krisis (G2920), which carries the OT riyb (covenant lawsuit) background. These are not generic religious concepts; they are drawn from Israel's covenant vocabulary.

    Gal 1:8 — "Even If an Angel from Heaven"¶

    Paul says even an angel preaching a different gospel should be accursed. Does this target the angel of Rev 14:6? Only if the angel preaches a gospel heteros — different in kind. The content of Rev 14:7 (Creator, judgment, worship) appears in Paul's own preaching (Acts 17:22-31). Paul includes judgment in "my gospel" (Rom 2:16). The angel does not preach a heteron euangelion; he preaches the same gospel in its eschatological phase. The temporal frame shifts from "God has appointed a day" (Acts 17:31, future) to "the hour of his judgment is come" (Rev 14:7, arrived), but the content is identical.

    DeSilva's Imperial Euangelia Thesis¶

    DeSilva recognizes a real dimension of the text — John's counter-imperial vocabulary. But he makes the political frame the primary meaning, reducing aionios to "age-long" and euangelion to "accession announcement." His own caveat — "many are clear to distinguish this 'gospel' from 'the Gospel'" — acknowledges the difficulty. The imperial parallel illuminates the text's rhetorical force but does not determine its theological content. The angel's message is politically subversive AND theologically definitive — both, not one or the other.

    Paul's Silence on Judgment in 1 Cor 15:3-4¶

    If Paul's primary summary omits judgment, does this mean judgment is not part of the gospel? No. En protois (G4413, dative plural) marks priority, not exclusivity. Paul includes judgment explicitly in Rom 2:16 ("according to my gospel") and implicitly at Athens (Acts 17:31). His silence on judgment in one summary does not exclude it from the gospel, just as his silence on Davidic descent in 1 Cor 15 does not exclude it from the gospel he presents in Rom 1:3-4.

    Conclusion¶

    The everlasting gospel of Revelation 14:6 is the same gospel that runs from the protoevangelium (Gen 3:15) through the patriarchs (Gal 3:8), through the OT prophetic herald tradition (Isa 40:9, 52:7, 61:1), through Jesus' earthly ministry (Luke 4:18-21; Mark 1:14-15), through apostolic proclamation (Rom 1:16; Acts 17:22-31), to the final angelic announcement before the harvest of the earth.

    Six lines of evidence converge:

    First, the vocabulary chain from Hebrew basar through LXX euangelizo to NT euangelion/euangelisai is confirmed by apostolic quotation (Rom 10:15 quoting Isa 52:7), dominical application (Luke 4:18 quoting Isa 61:1), and Pauline coinage (proeuangelizomai, Gal 3:8). The angel of Rev 14:6 stands at the end of this chain, using both noun and verb from the same root — a construction unique in the NT.

    Second, the anarthrous construction (euangelion aionion) is qualitative, emphasizing the eternal character of the gospel while still referring to THE well-known gospel. Anarthrous euangelion appears elsewhere (Rom 1:1, Gal 1:6) and always refers to the same gospel. The qualitative reading enriches without changing the referent.

    Third, aionios reaches backward before creation (2 Tim 1:9; Tit 1:2) and forward to eternity (Matt 25:46). The gospel is "everlasting" because it was purposed before creation (Eph 1:4; 1 Pet 1:18-20; Rev 13:8) and will never be superseded. DeSilva's "age-long" translation fails the 2 Tim 1:9 test (pre-creation backward reach) and the Matt 25:46 test (same adjective for punishment and life).

    Fourth, the content of Rev 14:7 passes the Galatians 1:6-9 test because Paul condemns the substitution of works-righteousness for grace (specifically circumcision-for-salvation), not the inclusion of judgment, creation, or commandments in gospel content. Paul himself includes all three: judgment in "my gospel" (Rom 2:16), creation in his preaching (Acts 17:24), and commandment-keeping as what matters while circumcision does not (1 Cor 7:19). The first angel's message matches Paul's Athens sermon element for element.

    Fifth, the imperial euangelia parallel strengthens the identification. John uses counter-imperial vocabulary throughout Revelation (1:5; 13:4-8; 17:14; 19:16). Euangelion in Rev 14:6 is both a theological proclamation and a political counter-claim: the true good news belongs to God, not to the beast or any earthly power. The political frame reinforces the theological content rather than replacing it.

    Sixth, Rev 14:12's description of the saints — commandments of God + faith of Jesus — is gospel result, not gospel addition. The articular present participle terountes describes who the saints are. It parallels Paul's "faith working through love" (Gal 5:6) and "keeping of the commandments of God" (1 Cor 7:19). The gospel produces a people who keep God's commandments and hold the faith of Jesus. This is the fruit of the everlasting gospel, not its corruption.

    The everlasting gospel is one message, proclaimed through all ages, by patriarchs and prophets and apostles and angels. It calls for fear of God, glory to God, worship of the Creator, and faithfulness through judgment. It produces a people who keep God's commandments and hold the faith of Jesus. It is the same gospel Paul preached (Rom 2:16; Acts 17:22-31), the same gospel Isaiah announced (Isa 52:7), the same gospel Jesus proclaimed at Nazareth (Luke 4:18-21), and the same gospel that was "gospeled-beforehand" to Abraham (Gal 3:8). It bears the adjective aionion — everlasting — because it stretches from before creation to beyond the harvest of the earth.
