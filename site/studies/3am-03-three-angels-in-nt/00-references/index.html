    Reference Gathering: The Three Angels' Pattern in the New Testament¶

    Question¶

    Does the New Testament repeat the same three-part pattern (gospel call → expose false system → warn of judgment) before Revelation 14? Examine: John the Baptist, Jesus' ministry, Stephen's speech, Paul at Athens, Paul to Rome.

    Study Plan Context¶

    This is Study 3 of a 22-study series on the Three Angels' Messages (3AM series). It completes the foundation arc (Arc 0) by showing the 3AM pattern runs through both testaments.

    Series position: Follows 3am-00 (methodology), 3am-01 (literary context of Rev 12-14), and 3am-02 (OT pattern — Noah, Sodom, Egypt, Carmel, Josiah, Jeremiah).

    Completed prior 3AM studies to integrate: - 3am-00-series-methodology — mapped Rev 14:6-12 to the 22-study arc - 3am-01-literary-context-rev-14 — Rev 12-14 structure, competing-messages framework - 3am-02-three-angels-in-ot — proved the three-part pattern in six OT episodes

    Other relevant prior studies: - gsp-10-three-angels-gospel — proved the 3AM are the gospel, not an addition to it - gsp-07-judgment-and-gospel — proved judgment is integral to the gospel (Rom 2:16, Acts 17:30-31, Rev 14:6-7)

    Prior Studies¶

    From Study Plan (Integrate list)¶

    3am-02-three-angels-in-ot: - Question: "Is the three-part pattern of the three angels' messages repeated throughout the Old Testament?" - Key finding 1: Six OT episodes (Noah, Sodom, Egypt, Carmel, Josiah, Jeremiah) all contain the three elements: gospel/worship call, exposure of false system, judgment warning - Key finding 2: The covenant lawsuit (riyb, H7378) tradition provides the formal legal structure — summons/recall of obligations → indictment of violations → announcement of covenant curse - Key finding 3: The riyb-to-krisis lexical bridge (H7378 → G2920 through the LXX) means Rev 14:7's "hour of judgment" carries the weight of the entire OT covenant lawsuit tradition - Key finding 4: The "come out" vocabulary chain from yatsa (H3318) through Jer 51:45 to exerchomai (G1831) in Rev 18:4 provides direct textual dependence — not vague thematic similarity - Key finding 5: The chemah (H2534) to thumos (G2372) bridge connects Huldah's wrath prophecy (2 Ki 22:17) to the third angel's warning (Rev 14:10) - Key finding 6: The pattern escalates — from judgment on the world at large (Noah) through pagan cities/empires (Sodom, Egypt) to internal crises within Israel (Carmel, Josiah, Jeremiah). By Jeremiah, the "Babylon" is the system corrupting God's own people - Key finding 7: Josiah's case shows individual salvation within irrevocable corporate judgment — paralleling Rev 14:12's "patience of the saints" amid Babylon's fall - Open question: The gospel-call element in Noah depends on NT interpretation (2 Pet 2:5, Heb 11:7); Genesis alone does not explicitly describe preaching. This is the weakest standalone case - Comparison matrix provided covering all six episodes

    gsp-10-three-angels-gospel: - Question: "Are the three angels' messages the gospel, or an addition to it?" - Key finding 1: The text itself calls the message euangelion aionion (Rev 14:6), and the legon construction of v.7 identifies the content as that gospel — the judgment announcement IS the gospel, not an addendum - Key finding 2: Paul at Athens (Acts 17:22-31) contains every element of the first angel's message: Creator worship (v.24), universal scope (vv.26,30), repentance call (v.30), appointed judgment day (v.31), resurrection as proof (v.31). "No one disputes that Acts 17 is gospel preaching. The element-by-element correspondence with Rev 14:6-7 is exact." - Key finding 3: Three independent NT lines place judgment within the gospel: Rev 14:6-7 (labels it gospel), Rom 2:16 ("according to my gospel"), Acts 17:30-31 (climax of gospel preaching) - Key finding 4: Eccl 12:13-14 provides an exact OT structural match — fear God, keep commandments, judgment — connected through verified LXX bridges. Four OT farewell addresses (Moses, Joshua, Samuel, Solomon) deliver this same core message - Key finding 5: The basar→euangelizo bridge (PMI 29.44) means the first angel's verb traces to Isaiah's herald of good tidings (Isa 40:9, 52:7, 61:1) - Key finding 6: Isa 52:7 (basar — good tidings) is quoted by Paul as gospel in Rom 10:15. The context is deliverance FROM Babylon (Isa 52:11 — "go ye out from thence"). Babylon's fall IS gospel - Key finding 7: 1 Thess 1:9-10 summarizes Paul's Thessalonian preaching with three compressed elements: "turned to God" (gospel), "turned FROM idols" (exposure), "await coming Retribution" (judgment warning)

    gsp-07-judgment-and-gospel: - Question: "Does judgment contradict the gospel of grace?" - Key finding 1: The NT uses distinct vocabulary: krisis (G2920, judicial process, 47 occurrences) vs. katakrima (G2631, adverse sentence, only 3 occurrences in Rom 5-8). Believers are freed from katakrima but still appear at the bema for evaluative krisis - Key finding 2: Dan 7:22 shows judgment as deliverance: "judgment was given TO/FOR the saints" - Key finding 3: Acts 17:30-31 places judgment at the CLIMAX of Paul's gospel preaching — repentance is commanded BECAUSE judgment is coming, with resurrection as proof - Relevance: Establishes that the judgment element in the NT preaching pattern is not anti-gospel but integral to it — critical for this study's argument that NT preachers were delivering the same pattern as the three angels

    Web Research Findings¶

    The web research agent (Phase 0) produced extensive findings in 00-web-research.md. Key findings summarized here:

    C.H. Dodd — The Apostolic Preaching and Its Developments¶

    • Reconstructed the "kerygma" (core apostolic preaching pattern) from Acts 2-5 and 1 Cor 15:3-8
    • Six elements: (1) prophecy fulfillment, (2) Jesus' historical ministry/death/resurrection, (3) exaltation, (4) Holy Spirit evidence, (5) future consummation, (6) call to repentance
    • Judgment integral to the kerygma: "Judgment is for Paul a function of the universal lordship of Christ"
    • Critical lead: 1 Thess 1:9-10 contains a compressed three-part pattern: "turned to God" (gospel) / "turned FROM idols" (exposure) / "await coming Retribution" (judgment). Dodd identifies this as a summary of Paul's preaching at Thessalonica
    • Paul claims continuity with the broader kerygma: "Whether I or they, it was thus that we preached" (1 Cor 15:11)
    • Claims to verify: Does the Dodd kerygma's six-element structure map onto the three-part pattern across Acts sermons (Acts 2, 3, 7, 10, 13, 17)?

    Mikeal Parsons — Acts 17:22-31 Chiastic Structure¶

    • Identifies chiasm: A (ignorance, vv.22-23) / B (Creator, vv.24-25) / C (humanity-God, vv.26-28) / B' (idols exposed, v.29) / A' (judgment, vv.30-31)
    • Judgment announcement (17:30-31) as "rhetorical turning point" — God moves from "overlooking" ignorance to demanding repentance
    • Paul's critique: Athens worships "a 'what,' not a 'whom'" — fundamental error of idolatry exposed
    • Claims to verify: Does the chiastic structure hold up in the Greek? Does the A/A' frame (ignorance → judgment) parallel the three angels' movement from gospel proclamation to judgment warning?

    Toussaint / Wiersbe / Thomas — Acts 7 Commentary¶

    • Three thematic cords: (1) God's program shows progress and change, (2) blessings transcend geography, (3) Israel consistently opposed God's messengers
    • Stephen "turns the tables" — from defendant to prosecutor, following OT prophetic confrontation patterns
    • Climactic indictment (7:51-53) uses covenant language: "stiff-necked" and "resisting the Holy Spirit" echo Deuteronomy
    • Claims to verify: Does Acts 7 follow the three-part pattern? Gospel history (vv.2-43) → exposure of Israel's rejection pattern (vv.44-50) → judgment warning (vv.51-53)?

    Hodges — Stephen's Typological Framework¶

    • Unifying theme: "Israel's resistance to the Holy Spirit throughout its history"
    • The rejection pattern is typological — OT history foreshadowed Israel's treatment of Christ
    • Joseph and Moses as primary types of the rejected savior
    • Claims to verify: Do the Joseph (Gen 37-50) and Moses (Exo 2-4) rejection-then-acceptance patterns parallel the gospel-exposure-judgment structure?

    J.N. Andrews — The Three Messages of Revelation 14:6-12 (1892)¶

    • Explicitly connects apostolic judgment preaching to the three angels: Paul before Felix (Acts 24:25) and at Athens (Acts 17:30-31)
    • Critical distinction: NT speakers preached judgment as future/anticipated, while the first angel announces "the hour of his judgment IS come" — the NT instances are precursors/patterns, not the fulfillment
    • Daniel's prophecies regarding the "time of the end" were "closed up and sealed" (Dan 8:17, 26; 12:4, 9), so the specific judgment-hour proclamation was reserved for the final generation
    • Claims to verify: Check the Greek tenses in Acts 17:31, Acts 24:25, and Rev 14:7 for temporal differences (future vs. arrived). This anticipation-vs-announcement distinction is potentially the key interpretive framework

    Matthew 23 Structural Analysis¶

    • Three sections: (1) warning against scribes/Pharisees (vv.1-12), (2) seven woes (vv.13-36), (3) judgment lament (vv.37-39)
    • Scholarly debate: Should Matt 23-25 be read as a unified sequence? If so, the macro pattern is exposure (ch.23) → judgment signs (ch.24) → judgment execution parables (ch.25)
    • Claims to verify: Does Jesus' woes-then-judgment sequence in Matt 23-25 follow the three-part pattern at a discourse level?

    Douglas Campbell — Romans 1-3 Rhetorical Analysis¶

    • Traditional reading: gospel power (1:16-17) → Gentile corruption exposure (1:18-32) → Jewish corruption exposure (2:1-29) → universal judgment (3:9-20) → justification by faith (3:21-26)
    • Romans 2:16 explicitly ties judgment to gospel: "according to MY gospel"
    • Claims to verify: Does the traditional reading of Rom 1-3 map the three-part pattern? Does Paul's argument follow gospel declaration → exposure → judgment?

    Academic thesis (Love in Truth) — Gospel Preaching Patterns in Acts¶

    • Four consistent elements across apostolic sermons: (1) core facts proclaimed, (2) error/sin exposed, (3) call to response demanding faith/repentance, (4) eschatological warning
    • All speakers "unfailingly identify the main source of hearers' opposition" — exposure adapts to audience while maintaining structural position
    • Claims to verify: Does the exposure element genuinely adapt to different audiences (Pharisees for John/Jesus, Israel's history for Stephen, idolatry for Athens, Gentile/Jewish sin for Romans) while maintaining the same three-part sequence?

    Summary for Scoping Agent¶

    • 5 prior studies found with relevant findings (3am-02, gsp-10, gsp-07, plus tangential matches from stephens-prayer-receive-spirit and knowledge-accountability-judgment)
    • 12+ external corpus claims identified for biblical verification across EGW and Bohr
    • 10 web research directives from scholarly sources
    • Key leads:
    • The prior OT study (3am-02) established the pattern through six episodes and documented the vocabulary chains (riyb→krisis, shachah→proskyneo, yatsa→exerchomai, chemah→thumos). This study must now trace those SAME chains into the NT preaching instances
    • The gsp-10 study already proved Acts 17:22-31 maps element-by-element onto the first angel's message and that 1 Thess 1:9-10 compresses all three elements into one verse. These are starting points, not conclusions to repeat — the new study must go deeper on additional NT instances (John the Baptist, Stephen, Matthew 23, Romans 1-3)
    • Andrews' anticipation-vs-announcement distinction (NT preachers anticipated judgment; the first angel announces its arrival) is potentially the key interpretive framework — the NT instances are the PATTERN, Revelation 14 is the FULFILLMENT. This parallels the OT study's finding that the pattern escalates across history
    • EGW explicitly connects John the Baptist to the first angel's message and identifies three Elijahs (historical, John, end-time) as delivering the same type of message. Bohr expands this into a formal "Three Elijahs" framework. Both claims need verification against the biblical text
    • The web research identifies consistent structural analyses across individual passages (chiastic structure in Acts 17, three cords in Acts 7, three sections in Matt 23, progressive argument in Rom 1-3) that scholars recognize independently but have not synthesized into the three-part pattern framework. This study's contribution is the synthesis
