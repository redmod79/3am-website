# Web Research Findings

## Study Question
What does phobethete ton theon ('fear God') mean as the first imperative of the everlasting gospel? How does the OT yare/phobos tradition inform this command? Does 'fear God' conflict with 'perfect love casts out fear' (1 John 4:18)? Parse the 1 John 4:18 text — what does phobos meta kolaseos mean? How does fearing God function as the foundation for refusing to fear the beast (Rev 13:15, Matt 10:28)? How does Ecclesiastes 12:13 provide the OT template?

## Sources Found

### Jiří Moskala — "The Meaning of the Fear of God: The Crucial Notion of the Everlasting Gospel — A Biblical Study" (JATS, Vol. 30, Iss. 1, 2019)
**URL:** https://digitalcommons.andrews.edu/jats/vol30/iss1/11/
**Republished in:** Perspective Digest, Vol. 26(3), July 2021 (https://www.perspectivedigest.org/archive/26-3/the-meaning-of-the-fear-of-god)
**Author credentials:** Professor of Old Testament, Andrews University Seminary
**Key claims:**
- The Greek phrase *phobethete ton theon* ("Fear God") in Revelation 14:6 "occurs only once in the entire Bible" in this specific imperative form — the first imperative of the everlasting gospel.
- Revelation 14:7 contains three imperatives: "Fear God," "give him glory," and "worship him who made heaven and earth."
- Moskala identifies five reasons the command is "disturbing": (1) it sounds negative and triggers phobic reactions; (2) it contradicts biblical encouragements not to fear; (3) it creates distorted perceptions of God; (4) fear resulted from sin (Gen 3:10 — Adam hiding); (5) Satan exploits fear to lead people astray.
- His resolution: the command represents "a theological and relational notion in sharp contrast to existential fears." Fearing God means reorienting focus toward God rather than toward personal anxieties.
- Moskala presents four definitions of fearing God: (1) "To fear God means to make all our decisions in respect to God and His will"; (2) "To fear God means to fear to grieve Him or to make Him sad" — involves hating evil; (3) "One can define the fear of God as loving and obeying the Lord" with submission and dedication; (4) "To fear God means to see God. It leads to cultivating the keen awareness that we always live in His presence."
- He identifies a wordplay between two Hebrew words: to see (*ra'ah*) and to fear (*yare*), suggesting etymological connection.
- Key quote: "The concept of love in the notion of fear is no longer present in modern languages" but survives in biblical Hebrew — fear and love are complementary rather than contradictory.
- References Ecclesiastes 7:15-20 and Ecclesiastes 12:13 ("Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole [duty] of man") as the OT template.
- Does NOT discuss 1 John 4:18 or the beast/Revelation 13 connection.
**Relevance:** Directly addresses the core question. Moskala's four-dimensional definition and his ra'ah/yare wordplay are significant leads. His claim about the uniqueness of the imperative form needs verification.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Precept Austin — "1 John 4:18 Commentary" (Compilation of commentators)
**URL:** https://www.preceptaustin.org/1_john_418_commentary
**Key claims:**
- **Marvin Vincent** on the Greek negation: *ou* (not *me*) indicates fear "has no existence" in love — a categorical denial. Distinguishes this from reverent fear in 1 Peter 1:17 and Hebrews 12:28, calling it "slavish fear, not filial reverence."
- **Vincent on kolasis:** means "punishment, penalty" rather than mere torment. Crucially: "*timoria* has only the idea of penalty, *kolasis* has also that of discipline." *Kolasis* appears only twice in the NT: Matthew 25:46 and 1 John 4:18.
- **Vincent on the present tense:** "The punishment is present. Fear by anticipating punishment has it even now." Fear of judgment is itself a form of present suffering.
- **Teleios agape** (perfect love): denotes love that has "reached its goal" or is "expressed in action." The perfect tense of *teleioo* indicates "past completed action with present ongoing result."
- **John Stott:** believers "cannot approach him in love and hide from him in fear at the same time."
- **Bengel** outlines a spiritual progression: "neither love nor fear → fear without love → both fear and love → love without fear."
- **Steven Cole:** "if you still fear God's judgment, at the very least, you are not practicing biblical love for others."
- **Warren Wiersbe:** "An immature Christian is tossed between fear and love; a mature Christian rests in God's love."
- The commentary distinguishes clearly: God commands fear (Luke 12:4-5), but this denotes reverence, not dread of condemnation. Romans 8:1 ("no condemnation") and Romans 8:15 (Spirit of adoption, not slavery) undergird this distinction.
**Greek analysis:**
- *phobos* (G5401) — "separating from, removing oneself" due to dread; can also mean reverence
- *kolasis* (G2851) — "correction, punishment, penalty" — only 2x in NT
- *teleia agape* — love brought to its goal/completion
- *ballei exo* — "casts out" — present active indicative, ongoing action
**Relevance:** Rich data on the exact Greek terms in 1 John 4:18 and the servile/filial fear distinction. Multiple named commentators with direct quotes.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Dr. Eitan Bar — "Hebrew Word Study: FEAR (YIR'AH)" (Bible Scholar article)
**URL:** https://eitan.bar/articles/hebrew-word-study-fear-yirah/
**Key claims:**
- The Hebrew root Y-R-A conveys "reverence, respect, or awe" — fundamentally different from literal terror.
- "While some English translations choose the English word 'fear' to translate YIR'AH, other translations use 'Awe,' 'Respect,' and 'Reverence.'"
- YIR'AH encompasses "experiencing something that surpasses one's abilities and comprehension" rather than terror.
- Key distinction: Two other Hebrew words describe actual fear — **EIMAH** (terror) and **PACHAD** (scare/fear for one's life). These are categorically different from YIR'AH.
- Genesis 22:12 demonstrates the meaning of yare as respect and trust: "Now I know that you YIR'AH God, because you have not withheld from me your son."
- Proverbs 1:7 uses YIRAT (construct form): "The fear of (YIRAT) the LORD is the beginning of knowledge."
- Apprentice-master metaphor: YIR'AH is approaching the master "with respect and a willingness to learn, recognizing the master's expertise and authority."
- "The concept of 'fearing God' may initially appear to imply being scared, terrified, or afraid of God, but this isn't the biblical meaning."
**Hebrew/Greek analysis:** Root Y-R-A → yare (verb), yirah (noun). Distinguished from eimah (H367, terror) and pachad (H6343, dread).
**Relevance:** Provides the Hebrew semantic foundation for the fear-of-God concept. The three-word distinction (yirah vs. eimah vs. pachad) is a significant lead to verify.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### Of Saints & Shepherds — "Phobos Word Study" (Word study resource)
**URL:** https://ofsaintsandshepherds.com/word-studies/authority-words/fear/phobos/
**Key claims:**
- **Phobos** (G5401) — 45 appearances across 42 NT verses.
- Etymology: from *phebomai* meaning "to flee, to be startled." Thus phobos originally meant "flight or terror."
- Connected to "fear of the unknown, fear of the future, and fear of authorities."
- The LXX "often used phobos to translate the Hebrew word yirah" — this cross-linguistic bridge is why NT writers use the same word for two very different responses (terror and reverence).
- In Revelation specifically: phobos appears in Rev 11:11 and Rev 18:10, 18:15 describing people standing "far off in fear" of judgment — contexts emphasizing dread of divine judgment rather than reverent submission.
- The semantic range produces a genuine ambiguity that context must resolve each time the word appears.
**Relevance:** The LXX translation pattern (phobos = yirah) is a critical bridge between the OT and NT fear-of-God traditions. The Revelation usage data is directly relevant.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

## Summary of Scholarly Landscape

The scholarly consensus distinguishes between two fundamentally different types of fear in Scripture: servile fear (dread of punishment, *phobos meta kolaseos*) and filial fear (reverential awe, the biblical *yirat YHWH*). Moskala's JATS article represents the most directly relevant academic treatment, arguing that the fear of God in Revelation 14:7 is a "theological and relational notion" involving four dimensions: decision-making oriented toward God's will, fear of grieving God, loving obedience, and cultivating awareness of God's presence. His observation that biblical Hebrew preserves an integration of love and fear that modern languages have lost is significant.

On the 1 John 4:18 question, commentators consistently identify the *phobos* being cast out as servile fear — the slavish dread of punishment — not the reverent awe that Scripture commands. Vincent's distinction between *kolasis* (corrective punishment) and *timoria* (pure penalty) adds nuance. Bengel's four-stage spiritual progression (no love/no fear → fear without love → fear and love → love without fear) provides a developmental framework. No commentator treats 1 John 4:18 as contradicting the command to fear God; the resolution is semantic — different types of fear are in view.

On the Ecclesiastes 12:13 / Revelation 14:7 parallel, several sources note the structural connection (fear God + obedience + judgment), though no source provides a detailed linguistic comparison of the Hebrew and Greek texts. The fear-obedience-judgment triad appears in both passages and deserves careful research. The Hebrew distinction between yirah (reverent awe), eimah (terror), and pachad (dread) is broadly recognized but needs verification through the tools. The LXX bridge (phobos translating yirah) is critical for understanding how the NT inherits and deploys the OT fear vocabulary.

The beast/fear connection is less developed in the scholarly literature found. Barnes and the Pulpit Commentary explicitly contrast "fear God" with "homage to the beast," and the TheBibleSays commentary's principle that "humans obey what they fear most" provides a theological framework: the first angel's message positions God-fear as the antidote to beast-fear. Matthew 10:28 is widely recognized as the foundational text for this contrast, but detailed scholarly treatment of the fear-God-as-foundation-for-refusing-beast-worship argument was not located in this search.
