    Verses¶

    Question¶

    What does 'everlasting gospel' (euangelion aionion) mean in Rev 14:6? Is this the same gospel Paul preached, or a different one? What does the anarthrous construction imply? How does euangelisai connect to the OT basar vocabulary chain (Isa 40:9, 52:7, 61:1)? What is the content of a gospel called 'everlasting'? How does the imperial euangelia thesis relate? Does it pass the Galatians 1:6-9 test?

    The First Angel: Everlasting Gospel Proclaimed with Judgment-Hour Content¶

    And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

    Context: Rev 14:1-5 introduces the 144,000 with the Lamb on Mount Zion. The first angel in v.6 appears immediately after this scene of the redeemed. The three angels' messages (vv.6-12) form a unified proclamation identified by the term euangelion aionion. This is the crux text for the study: the phrase 'everlasting gospel' appears nowhere else in Scripture.

The creation catalog (heaven, earth, sea, fountains of waters) echoes Exod 20:11.

    Second Angel: Babylon Is Fallen — Structural Component of the Everlasting Gospel¶

    And there followed another angel, saying, Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication.

    Context: The second angel follows the first, extending the everlasting gospel proclamation. The doubled 'is fallen, is fallen' (epesen epesen) is emphatic aorist. The perfect tense pepotiken ('has made drink') presents Babylon's corrupting influence as a completed state with ongoing results.

    Notes: The second angel's message is structurally part of the euangelion aionion announced in v.6. The mirrored genitive chains in vv.8 and 10 (wine of wrath of fornication / wine of wrath of God) link Babylon's cup with God's judgment cup.

    Third Angel: Warning Against Beast Worship — The Testing Truth¶

    And the third angel followed them, saying with a loud voice, If any man worship the beast and his image, and receive his mark in his forehead, or in his hand, The same shall drink of the wine of the wrath of God, which is poured out without mixture into the cup of his indignation; and he shall be tormented with fire and brimstone in the presence of the holy angels, and in the presence of the Lamb: And the smoke of their torment ascendeth up for ever and ever: and they have no rest day nor night, who worship the beast and his image, and whosoever receiveth the mark of his name.

    Context: The third angel's warning completes the three-part proclamation. The conditional protasis (ei tis proskynei... kai lambanei) uses present indicatives describing characteristic/ongoing action. The apodosis uses future tenses for judgment consequences. The articular present participle hoi proskynountes identifies beast-worshipers as a community by their ongoing practice.

    Notes: The third message is the testing truth — it distinguishes two communities by their worship allegiance (beast vs. Creator). EGW identifies this as 'the messages by which the world is to be tested' (2SM 111.3).

    The Saints Identified: Commandments of God and Faith of Jesus¶

    Here is the patience of the saints: here are they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus.

    Context: Rev 14:12 follows the third angel's warning and precedes the harvest scene (vv.14-16). It functions as a descriptive conclusion identifying the saints by their character: hoi terountes (the ones keeping) is an articular present active participle — substantival, not imperative. The saints are described, not commanded.

    OT Herald: Mevasseret Zion — 'Behold Your God!'¶

    O Zion, that bringest good tidings, get thee up into the high mountain; O Jerusalem, that bringest good tidings, lift up thy voice with strength; lift it up, be not afraid; say unto the cities of Judah, Behold your God!

    Context: Isaiah 40 opens the 'Book of Comfort' (chs. 40-66). The herald (mevasseret, feminine Piel participle of basar) is commissioned to announce God's arrival to Zion and Jerusalem. The content is 'Behold your God!' — a theophanic announcement. The feminine form mevasseret may personify Zion/Jerusalem as the herald.

    OT Herald: Mevasser on the Mountains — Fourfold Gospel Announcement¶

    How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace; that bringeth good tidings of good, that publisheth salvation; that saith unto Zion, Thy God reigneth!

    Context: Isaiah 52 continues the servant-herald tradition. The mevasser (masculine Piel participle of basar) appears twice. The fourfold announcement: shalom (peace), tov (good), yeshuah (salvation), malakh (reigns). Paul quotes this in Rom 10:15. Nahum 1:15 uses nearly identical language.

    OT Herald: Spirit-Anointed Proclamation — Jesus' Mission Statement¶

    The Spirit of the Lord GOD is upon me; because the LORD hath anointed me to preach good tidings unto the meek; he hath sent me to bind up the brokenhearted, to proclaim liberty to the captives, and the opening of the prison to them that are bound; To proclaim the acceptable year of the LORD, and the day of vengeance of our God; to comfort all that mourn; To appoint unto them that mourn in Zion, to give unto them beauty for ashes, the oil of joy for mourning, the garment of praise for the spirit of heaviness; that they might be called trees of righteousness, the planting of the LORD, that he might be glorified.

    Context: The herald is now anointed by God's Spirit (mashach, root of mashiyach/Messiah). The Piel infinitive levassar ('to announce good news') specifies the purpose of the anointing. Jesus quotes Isa 61:1-2a in Luke 4:18-21, stopping before 'the day of vengeance,' and declares 'This day is this scripture fulfilled in your ears.'

    Notes: Levassar is the Piel infinitive construct of basar with the preposition le. The mashach + levassar connection links the Messianic anointing directly to the gospel-herald function. Jesus' application of this passage to himself in Luke 4:18-21 creates the definitive basar-euangelizo bridge.

    Paul's Anathema: The 'Different Gospel' Test¶

    I marvel that ye are so soon removed from him that called you into the grace of Christ unto another gospel: Which is not another; but there be some that trouble you, and would pervert the gospel of Christ. But though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed. As we said before, so say I now again, If any man preach any other gospel unto you than that ye have received, let him be accursed.

    Context: Paul opens Galatians with an immediate confrontation, skipping his usual thanksgiving. The Galatian crisis is identified in Gal 2:3-5 (circumcision demanded), 5:2-3 (circumcision nullifies Christ), 6:12-13 (opponents want to glory in your flesh). The 'different gospel' is the addition of circumcision as a salvific requirement.

Anathema esto (v.8) — present imperative of eimi with anathema — 'let him be devoted to destruction.' Paul extends the anathema to include angels (v.8), making it universally applicable.

    Paul's Gospel: Received by Divine Revelation, Not Human Origin¶

    But I certify you, brethren, that the gospel which was preached of me is not after man. For I neither received it of man, neither was I taught it, but by the revelation of Jesus Christ.

    Context: Paul defends the divine origin of his gospel against the Judaizers. This parallels the angel of Rev 14:6 — both the Pauline gospel and the everlasting gospel have divine, not human, origin.

    Notes: To euangelion to euangelisthen hyp emou (v.11) — double article construction with aorist passive participle. The gospel has a divine source (di apokalypseos Iesou Christou, v.12).

    Paul's Gospel Confronted: Circumcision as the Specific Heresy¶

    And I went up by revelation, and communicated unto them that gospel which I preach among the Gentiles, but privately to them which were of reputation, lest by any means I should run, or had run, in vain. But neither Titus, who was with me, being a Greek, was compelled to be circumcised: And that because of false brethren unawares brought in, who came in privily to spy out our liberty which we have in Christ Jesus, that they might bring us into bondage: To whom we gave place by subjection, no, not for an hour; that the truth of the gospel might continue with you.

    Context: Paul identifies the specific issue: circumcision. Titus (a Greek) was NOT compelled to be circumcised. The pseudadelphoi (false brethren) sought to spy out their liberty in Christ and bring them into bondage — the bondage of requiring circumcision for salvation.

    Notes: The Galatian heresy is specifically circumcision-as-salvation-requirement, not the addition of eschatological or ethical content to the gospel message.

    Circumcision vs. Faith Working Through Love¶

    Stand fast therefore in the liberty wherewith Christ hath made us free, and be not entangled again with the yoke of bondage. Behold, I Paul say unto you, that if ye be circumcised, Christ shall profit you nothing. For I testify again to every man that is circumcised, that he is a debtor to do the whole law. Christ is become of no effect unto you, whosoever of you are justified by the law; ye are fallen from grace. For we through the Spirit wait for the hope of righteousness by faith. For in Jesus Christ neither circumcision availeth any thing, nor uncircumcision; but faith which worketh by love.

    Context: Paul draws the conclusion: circumcision for justification = fallen from grace. But the positive formulation in v.6 — 'faith working through love' (pistis di agapes energoumene) — shows that Paul's gospel is not antinomian. Faith produces love, and love fulfills the moral law (Gal 5:14).

    Notes: Gal 5:6 is the Pauline parallel to Rev 14:12. Both texts combine faith and active obedience: pistis di agapes energoumene (faith working through love) parallels hoi terountes tas entolas tou Theou kai ten pistin Iesou (those keeping God's commandments and the faith of Jesus).

    Paul's 'First Importance' Gospel Summary — Priority, Not Exclusivity¶

    Moreover, brethren, I declare unto you the gospel which I preached unto you, which also ye have received, and wherein ye stand; By which also ye are saved, if ye keep in memory what I preached unto you, unless ye have believed in vain. For I delivered unto you first of all that which I also received, how that Christ died for our sins according to the scriptures; And that he was buried, and that he rose again the third day according to the scriptures:

    Context: Paul's most concentrated gospel summary. En protois (v.3) means 'among the first things' or 'of first importance' — dative plural of protos (G4413). This is a priority statement, not an exclusivity statement. Paul gives a DIFFERENT gospel summary in Rom 1:1-4.

    Notes: En protois: en (preposition, 'among/in') + protois (dative plural masculine of protos, G4413). The dative plural with en indicates 'among the first things' — what is most important, not what is exclusively definitive. But there rose up certain of the sect of the Pharisees which believed, saying, That it was needful to circumcise them, and to command them to keep the law of Moses.

    Context: The Jerusalem Council addresses the exact issue Paul condemns in Galatians: circumcision as a salvation requirement. The council's decision (Acts 15:28-29) rejects mandatory circumcision for Gentile believers, confirming that the 'different gospel' Paul condemns is works-righteousness (specifically circumcision-for-salvation), not the addition of ethical or eschatological content.

    Notes: Ean me peritmethete to ethei tou Mouseos, ou dynasthe sothenai (v.1) — 'Unless you are circumcised according to the custom of Moses, you cannot be saved.' This is the formula Paul condemns. The Galatians test targets salvation-by-works, not gospel-content breadth.

    Creator Worship in Revelation's Throne Room¶

    Thou art worthy, O Lord, to receive glory and honour and power: for thou hast created all things, and for thy pleasure they are and were created.

    Context: Rev 4:11 establishes Creator-worship as central to Revelation's theology. The elders worship God specifically as Creator (su ektisas ta panta). Rev 14:7's proskynhsate to poiesanti echoes this throne-room worship.

    Notes: Creator worship in Rev 4:11 precedes and frames the Creator worship in Rev 14:7. The everlasting gospel calls for the same worship that the heavenly beings already render.

    Angel Swears by the Creator — Creation Formula in Revelation¶

    And sware by him that liveth for ever and ever, who created heaven, and the things that therein are, and the earth, and the things that therein are, and the sea, and the things which are therein, that there should be time no longer:

    Context: A second creation catalog in Revelation: heaven, earth, sea (with their contents). This parallels both Exod 20:11 and Rev 14:7, confirming that creation language is a recurring Revelation motif tied to the Creator's sovereignty.

    Notes: Rev 10:6 creation catalog: ton ouranon kai ta en auto kai ten gen kai ta en aute kai ten thalassan kai ta en aute. Rev 14:7: ton ouranon kai ten gen kai thalassan kai pegas hydaton. Both echo Exod 20:11: heaven, earth, sea.

    Protoevangelium — The First Gospel Promise¶

    And I will put enmity between thee and the woman, and between thy seed and her seed; it shall bruise thy head, and thou shalt bruise his heel.

    Context: The first gospel promise in Scripture — the seed of the woman will bruise the serpent's head. This establishes the gospel as coextensive with human history, supporting the 'everlasting' character of the gospel in Rev 14:6.

    Notes: The protoevangelium places the gospel at the very beginning of human history. If the gospel extends from Gen 3:15 to Rev 14:6, it is genuinely aionion — spanning the entire scope of time.

    Angelic Euangelizo at Christ's Birth — Angel Proclaiming Good Tidings¶

    And the angel said unto them, Fear not: for, behold, I bring you good tidings of great joy, which shall be to all people. Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace, good will toward men.

    Context: An angel uses euangelizo to announce Christ's birth — 'I bring you good tidings of great joy.' This parallels Rev 14:6 where an angel has euangelion aionion euangelisai. Both are angelic euangelizo events.

    Notes: Euangelizomai hymin charan megalen — the angel 'gospels' the shepherds with 'great joy.' The angel of Luke 2 and the angel of Rev 14:6 both perform angelic euangelizo.

    Unsearchable Riches of Christ and the Eternal Purpose¶

    Unto me, who am less than the least of all saints, is this grace given, that I should preach among the Gentiles the unsearchable riches of Christ; And to make all men see what is the fellowship of the mystery, which from the beginning of the world hath been hid in God, who created all things by Jesus Christ: To the intent that now unto the principalities and powers in heavenly places might be known by the church the manifold wisdom of God, According to the eternal purpose which he purposed in Christ Jesus our Lord:

    Context: Paul describes the gospel as involving 'unsearchable riches' — far broader than any single summary. The mystery was hidden from the beginning of the world (apo ton aionon) and fulfilled according to an 'eternal purpose' (prothesin ton aionon). The gospel exceeds any formula.

    Notes: Kata prothesin ton aionon (v.11) — 'according to the purpose of the ages.' The gospel operates according to God's eternal purpose, confirming the 'everlasting' character of Rev 14:6.

    Vengeance on Those Who 'Obey Not the Gospel' — Gospel Demands Obedience¶

    In flaming fire taking vengeance on them that know not God, and that obey not the gospel of our Lord Jesus Christ:

    Context: Paul speaks of judgment on those who 'obey not the gospel.' The verb hypakouō (obey) applied to the gospel proves it demands obedience, not merely intellectual assent. This connects to Rev 14:12's commandment-keeping as a gospel result.

    Notes: Tois me hypakouousin to euangelio — 'those not obeying the gospel.' If the gospel demands obedience (Paul's word), then Rev 14:12's commandment-keeping is not an addition to the gospel but a response demanded by it.

    The Judaizers' True Motive: Glory in Your Flesh¶

    As many as desire to make a fair shew in the flesh, they constrain you to be circumcised; only lest they should suffer persecution for the cross of Christ. For neither they themselves who are circumcised keep the law; but desire to have you circumcised, that they may glory in your flesh.

    Context: Paul identifies the Judaizers' motive: avoiding persecution and glorying in flesh. The heresy is not adding eschatological content to the gospel but imposing circumcision as a works-righteousness salvation condition.

    Notes: The Judaizers' 'different gospel' is specifically circumcision-for-salvation. Paul condemns the substitution of human merit for divine grace, not the inclusion of judgment, creation, or commandment-keeping in gospel proclamation.

    Paul Establishes the Law Through Faith¶

    Do we then make void the law through faith? God forbid: yea, we establish the law.

    Context: Paul explicitly affirms that faith does not void the law but establishes it. This connects to Rev 14:12's coordination of faith of Jesus with commandments of God — the same Pauline principle in eschatological application.

    Notes: Nomon histanomen — 'we establish the law.' Paul's affirmation that faith establishes (not abolishes) the law is the theological foundation for Rev 14:12's combined commandment-keeping and faith-of-Jesus description.

    Additional Isaiah Basar Passage — Herald Promised to Jerusalem¶

    The first shall say to Zion, Behold, behold them: and I will give to Jerusalem one that bringeth good tidings.

    Context: Isaiah 41:27 is one of five Isaiah passages using basar (H1319). God promises to give Jerusalem a mevasser (one who brings good tidings). This confirms the herald tradition that culminates in Isa 52:7 and 61:1.

    Notes: Mevasser appears here as a promised figure — God will GIVE a herald to Jerusalem. This is part of the cumulative basar vocabulary chain (Isa 40:9, 41:27, 52:7, 60:6, 61:1) that the NT inherits through euangelizo.

    Additional Isaiah Basar Passage — Nations Proclaiming God's Praises¶

    The multitude of camels shall cover thee, the dromedaries of Midian and Ephah; all they from Sheba shall come: they shall bring gold and incense; and they shall shew forth the praises of the LORD.

    Context: Isaiah 60:6 is the fifth of five Isaiah passages using basar. Here the nations themselves join in proclaiming (yevaseru, Piel of basar) the praises of the LORD. The good news extends beyond Israel — universal scope matching Rev 14:6.

    Notes: The nations 'shew forth the praises of the LORD' using basar. The universal scope of this proclamation — nations bringing offerings and proclaiming God's praises — parallels Rev 14:6's 'every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people.'

    Peter Echoing Isa 52:7: Preaching Peace by Jesus Christ¶

    The word which God sent unto the children of Israel, preaching peace by Jesus Christ: (he is Lord of all:)

    Context: Peter's sermon to Cornelius uses euangelizo with eirene (peace) — directly echoing Isa 52:7's mevasser mashmi'a shalom. Peter bridges the OT basar-shalom tradition to NT proclamation of peace through Christ. The parenthetical 'he is Lord of all' parallels Isa 52:7's 'Thy God reigneth!'

    Notes: Euangelizomenos eirenen dia Iesou Christou — 'preaching peace through Jesus Christ.' Direct echo of Isa 52:7. This NT bridge confirms the basar-euangelizo vocabulary chain is not a modern scholarly construction but an apostolic conviction.

    Mystery of God's Will + Gospel of Salvation — Gospel Encompasses Cosmic Purpose¶

    Having made known unto us the mystery of his will, according to his good pleasure which he hath purposed in himself: That in the dispensation of the fulness of times he might gather together in one all things in Christ, both which are in heaven, and which are on earth; even in him: In whom ye also trusted, after that ye heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation: in whom also after that ye believed, ye were sealed with that holy Spirit of promise,

    Context: Paul describes the gospel as encompassing the mystery of God's cosmic purpose — gathering all things in Christ. The gospel is called 'the word of truth' and 'the gospel of your salvation' (to euangelion tes soterias hymon). This breadth exceeds any reductionist definition.

    Notes: Three gospel designations in one passage: 'mystery of his will,' 'word of truth,' 'gospel of your salvation.' Each highlights a different facet. The cosmic scope (gather all things in Christ, heaven and earth) parallels the universal scope of Rev 14:6.

    Gospel of Peace — One of 22+ NT Gospel Designations¶

    And your feet shod with the preparation of the gospel of peace;

    Context: Paul uses yet another designation: 'the gospel of peace' (to euangelion tes eirenes). This echoes Isa 52:7 (mashmi'a shalom) and Acts 10:36 (euangelizomenos eirenen). Each designation reveals a different dimension of the one gospel.

    Notes: To euangelion tes eirenes — articular, with genitive of content. One of the 22+ NT designations cataloged in the G2098 word study. Shows the gospel's multidimensional character — no single designation captures its full content.

    Gospel Preached in All the World + Mystery Hidden from Ages¶

    For the hope which is laid up for you in heaven, whereof ye heard before in the word of the truth of the gospel; Which is come unto you, as it is in all the world; and bringeth forth fruit, as it doth also in you, since the day ye heard of it, and knew the grace of God in truth: If ye continue in the faith grounded and settled, and be not moved away from the hope of the gospel, which ye have heard, and which was preached to every creature which is under heaven; whereof I Paul am made a minister; Even the mystery which hath been hid from ages and from generations, but now is made manifest to his saints: To whom God would make known what is the riches of the glory of this mystery among the Gentiles; which is Christ in you, the hope of glory:

    Context: Paul describes the gospel as (1) preached 'in all the world' (v.6, universal scope matching Rev 14:6), (2) preached 'to every creature which is under heaven' (v.23), and (3) a mystery 'hid from ages and from generations' (v.26). The hiddenness-then-manifestation pattern confirms the gospel's pre-creation origin.

    Notes: Three features parallel Rev 14:6: universal scope (all the world / every creature = every nation), pre-creation hiddenness (hid from ages = everlasting), and present manifestation (now made manifest = the angel's proclamation). 'Christ in you, the hope of glory' (v.27) defines the mystery's content.

    Harvest Scene Following the Three Angels — Proclamation Leads to the Second Coming¶

    And I looked, and behold a white cloud, and upon the cloud one sat like unto the Son of man, having on his head a golden crown, and in his hand a sharp sickle. And another angel came out of the temple, crying with a loud voice to him that sat on the cloud, Thrust in thy sickle, and reap: for the time is come for thee to reap; for the harvest of the earth is ripe. And he that sat on the cloud thrust in his sickle on the earth; and the earth was reaped.

    Context: The harvest scene immediately follows the three angels' messages (vv.6-12). The Son of man comes 'on a white cloud' to reap the earth. The everlasting gospel proclamation directly precedes and prepares for the second coming. The sequence is: gospel proclaimed → world divides into two communities (v.12 vs. v.11) → harvest/judgment.

    Notes: The structural sequence: everlasting gospel (v.6-7) → Babylon's fall (v.8) → beast-worship warning (vv.9-11) → saints identified (v.12) → Son of man comes to harvest (vv.14-16). The three angels' messages prepare for the eschatological harvest.

    Paul's Pre-Conversion Zeal — Context for the Galatian Heresy¶

    For ye have heard of my conversation in time past in the Jews' religion, how that beyond measure I persecuted the church of God, and wasted it: And profited in the Jews' religion above many my equals in mine own nation, being more exceedingly zealous of the traditions of my fathers.

    Context: Paul describes his pre-conversion life in Judaism — zealous for 'the traditions of my fathers.' This provides context for understanding the Galatian heresy: the Judaizers wanted to impose the same traditions (circumcision, ceremonial law) on Gentile believers as a salvation requirement.

    Notes: Perisspoteros zelotes hyparchon ton patrikon mou paradoseon — 'being more exceedingly zealous of the traditions of my fathers.' Paul's former zeal for Jewish traditions parallels the Judaizers' demand for circumcision. The 'different gospel' he condemns is a return to this works-based system.

    Glorious Gospel of Christ — Gospel Vocabulary Diversity¶

    But if our gospel be hid, it is hid to them that are lost: In whom the god of this world hath blinded the minds of them which believe not, lest the light of the glorious gospel of Christ, who is the image of God, should shine unto them.

    Context: Paul uses another gospel designation: 'the glorious gospel of Christ' (to euangelion tes doxes tou Christou). The genitive chain (gospel of the glory of Christ) connects glory and gospel — the same connection as Rev 14:7's 'give him glory.' The gospel is described as 'light' that illuminates.

    Notes: To euangelion tes doxes tou Christou — anarthrous euangelion in some manuscripts, articular in others. The 'glory' (doxa) connection links to Rev 14:7's dote auto doxan ('give him glory'). Another of the 22+ NT gospel designations.

    Striving Together for the Faith of the Gospel — Pistis + Euangelion¶

    Only let your conversation be as it becometh the gospel of Christ: that whether I come and see you, or else be absent, I may hear of your affairs, that ye stand fast in one spirit, with one mind striving together for the faith of the gospel;

    Context: Paul combines pistis (faith) and euangelion (gospel): te pistei tou euangeliou ('the faith of the gospel'). This connects to Rev 14:12's ten pistin Iesou coordinated with the everlasting gospel. The gospel has a 'faith' that believers 'strive together for.'

    Notes: Te pistei tou euangeliou — the faith of/belonging to the gospel. Euangelion here is anarthrous in the genitive (tou euangeliou), like Rev 14:6. The combination of faith + gospel in Phil 1:27 parallels the combination in Rev 14:6+12.

    Gospel of God + Word of God — Gospel as Divine Word, Not Human Invention¶

    So being affectionately desirous of you, we were willing to have imparted unto you, not the gospel of God only, but also our own souls, because ye were dear unto us. For ye remember, brethren, our labour and travail: for labouring night and day, because we would not be chargeable unto any of you, we preached unto you the gospel of God. For this cause also thank we God without ceasing, because, when ye received the word of God which ye heard of us, ye received it not as the word of men, but as it is in truth, the word of God, which effectually worketh also in you that believe.

    Context: Paul uses 'the gospel of God' (to euangelion tou Theou) twice (vv.8-9) and then identifies it as 'the word of God' (logon Theou) which is 'not the word of men.' The gospel has divine origin — paralleling both Gal 1:11-12 (not after man) and Rev 14:6 (an angel carrying the gospel from heaven).

    Notes: To euangelion tou Theou (vv.8-9) — the gospel belongs to God, not to human inventors. Logon Theou (v.13) — the gospel IS the word of God. Divine origin confirms the gospel is one and unchanging — the same whether preached by Paul or proclaimed by the angel of Rev 14:6.
