# Web Research Findings

## Study Question
What does hode he hupomone ton hagion estin ("here is the patience of the saints") in Rev 14:12 mean? How does hupomone (G5281, patient endurance/constancy) function as the summary response to the three messages? What is the relationship between hupomone in 14:12 and the hupomone inclusio that opens at 13:10 ("here is the patience and the faith of the saints")? How does the Romans 5:3-5 chain (tribulation → patience → experience → hope) explain how endurance is produced? How does Hebrews 10:36 ("ye have need of patience, that, after ye have done the will of God, ye might receive the promise") connect? How does James 5:7-11 use Job and the prophets as endurance models? What does Revelation 12:11 ("they overcame him by the blood of the Lamb, and by the word of their testimony; and they loved not their lives unto the death") describe about the mechanism of endurance? Is this passive resignation or active, hopeful perseverance?

### Meyers, Jeffrey D. — "Hypomonē as 'Enduring Resistance': Finding Nonviolence in the Book of Revelation" (*The Bible Translator* 69.1, 2018, pp. 40-55)
**URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2051677018764266
**Institutional Repository:** https://digitalcommons.whitworth.edu/libraryfaculty/29/
**Key claims:**
- "Hypomonē frequently has a more active quality than translations such as 'patience' or 'endurance' suggest."
- Argues that "enduring resistance" is the most suitable translation for hypomonē in Revelation.
- This rendering "highlights hypomonē as part of Revelation's call for its audience to engage in nonviolent resistance to the evils and seductions of the Roman Empire."
- Hypomonē is characterized as "an act of endurance amidst suffering and even death, described as an act of courage and bravery."
- The term does not focus on individual heroic actions but on "lifelong resistance work."
- Hypomonē describes "an eschatological hope that leads to 'a life in opposition to the practice and goals of the society.'"
**Greek analysis:** Argues the standard translations ("patience," "endurance") flatten the active, resistant quality of the Greek term. The word connotes sustained, deliberate opposition — not merely passive waiting.
**Relevance:** Directly addresses whether hupomone in Revelation is passive resignation or active perseverance. Meyers' thesis — that it means "enduring resistance" — is a scholarly lead that should be verified against the actual usage in Rev 1:9, 2:2-3, 2:19, 3:10, 13:10, and 14:12.
**Status:** Lead to verify against Scripture

### List, Nicholas — "Job's Endurance (Jas 5:11b): Greco-Roman Virtue in the Letter of James" (*Novum Testamentum* 64.4, 2022, pp. 469ff.)
**URL:** https://brill.com/view/journals/nt/64/4/article-p469_4.xml
**Semantic Scholar:** https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Job%E2%80%99s-Endurance-(Jas-5:11b)-List/843e921ba62b682a39295254058c8fb0270fcf1f
**Key claims:**
- Interpreters have struggled to account for how James employs Job as an example of ὑπομονή, since a "steadfast" or "patient Job" is incongruous with the book of Job (Job complains, challenges God, expresses anguish).
- The Testament of Job is often forwarded as the preferred source of James' Joban tradition, but List argues against this dependency.
- James' language of ὑπομονή should be read against its wider Greco-Roman literary background.

- James rereads the book of Job within a Greco-Roman literary framework, resulting in "a congruent, though thoroughly Hellenistic, reading of Septuagint Job."
**Greek analysis:** Argues hupomone in Greco-Roman usage is not passive patience but an active, even aggressive virtue — the refusal to be defeated by circumstances.
**Relevance:** Directly relevant to James 5:7-11 as an endurance model. The claim that hupomone is "active, aggressive" rather than passive aligns with Meyers' findings on Revelation. The distinction between the Testament of Job and the canonical Job as sources for James' concept is a scholarly question worth noting.

## The hode (ὧδε) Formula in Revelation

The Greek word ὧδε (hode, "here") appears as a structural formula at key points in Revelation:
- **Rev 13:10:** ὧδε ἐστιν ἡ ὑπομονὴ καὶ ἡ πίστις τῶν ἁγίων ("Here is the patience and the faith of the saints")
- **Rev 13:18:** ὧδε ἡ σοφία ἐστίν ("Here is wisdom")
- **Rev 14:12:** ὧδε ἡ ὑπομονὴ τῶν ἁγίων ἐστίν ("Here is the patience of the saints")
- **Rev 17:9:** ὧδε ὁ νοῦς ὁ ἔχων σοφίαν ("Here is the mind which has wisdom")

The formula functions as a direct-address commentary device, breaking the narrative to call the reader's attention to something requiring spiritual discernment. In 13:18 and 17:9 it calls for wisdom (sophia); in 13:10 and 14:12 it calls for endurance (hupomone). The 13:10/14:12 pair brackets the third angel's message, creating an inclusio around the beast-worship warning. No specific scholarly article on the hode formula as an inclusio device was located, but the structural parallelism is evident from the Greek text itself.

**Status:** Structural observation to verify through Greek parser and literary analysis

## Summary of Scholarly Landscape

The scholarly consensus on hupomone in Revelation converges on several points. First, the term carries a significantly more active meaning than the English word "patience" suggests. Meyers (2018) argues for "enduring resistance" as the best translation, and List (2022) independently arrives at the same rendering for James 5:11, arguing that hupomone in Greco-Roman usage is "an active, aggressive virtue." This is not passive resignation but sustained, deliberate opposition to forces that would destroy faith. The etymology (hupo = under, mone = remaining) conveys remaining steadfast under pressure — "a person under the strain of a heavy load who keeps pressing forward."

Second, scholars across traditions (Adventist, evangelical, academic) agree that hupomone in Rev 14:12 functions as the defining characteristic of God's people in the eschatological crisis. The Adventist sources (Vine, ADvindicate, Adventist Record) frame this within the Three Angels' Messages as the character trait produced by responding to the final proclamation. Evangelical sources (GotQuestions, BibleRef) frame it within a tribulation context but agree on the essential meaning: endurance = refusing beast worship while maintaining faith in Christ and obedience to God's commandments.

Third, the relationship between hupomone (endurance) and nikao (overcoming) is a key structural feature of Revelation. Nikao appears 17 times in Revelation (out of 28 NT occurrences), making it a dominant term. The seven churches each receive an "overcomer" promise, and Rev 12:11 describes the mechanism of overcoming: the blood of the Lamb, the word of testimony, and willingness to die. Endurance is the sustained posture; overcoming is the outcome. The scholarly question of whether hupomone describes the means of overcoming or a parallel concept remains open but the texts suggest complementarity rather than identity.

Fourth, the "here is" (hode) formula in Revelation functions as a structural marker calling for reader discernment. Its use in both 13:10 and 14:12 to call for hupomone creates a bracketing structure around the third angel's warning about beast worship. This structural observation, while noted by commentators, has not been the subject of a dedicated scholarly monograph that the web search could locate.
