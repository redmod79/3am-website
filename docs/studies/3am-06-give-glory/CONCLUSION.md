# "Give Glory to Him": The Second Imperative of the Everlasting Gospel

## Question
What does dote auto doxan ("give glory to him") mean as a gospel imperative? How is glory (doxa/kabod) given to God — through confession (Josh 7:19), character acknowledgment (Isa 42:12), conduct (Matt 5:16, 1 Pet 2:12), fruit-bearing (John 15:8), bodily stewardship (1 Cor 6:20, 10:31), beholding-transformation (2 Cor 3:18), or something else? What is the relationship between giving glory and the judgment announcement that immediately follows? How does Romans 1:21 ("they glorified him not as God") describe the exact failure the first angel addresses? Why does Rev 16:9 record that under the plagues they "repented not to give him glory" — a precise verbal echo of 14:7? How does John 17:4 ("I have glorified thee on the earth: I have finished the work") establish Christ as the model of giving glory?

## Summary Answer
"Give glory to him" (dote auto doxan, Rev 14:7) is a command to give God the full weight of recognition He deserves — acknowledging His character, confessing His rightness, completing His assigned work, bearing His fruit, stewarding the body He purchased, and being transformed into His image through sustained beholding. The kabod/doxa semantic bridge shows that "glory" means character-substance, not merely visible radiance: when Moses asked to see God's glory (Exod 33:18), God proclaimed His character attributes (Exod 34:6-7). The command is comprehensive, not narrow. It encompasses at least seven biblically attested modes of glorification, all of which flow from one source — the Spirit's work through believers who abide in Christ. The judgment announcement follows because the Creator who reveals His character has the right to evaluate how His creatures respond to that revelation. Romans 1:21 describes the precise failure the first angel addresses ("they glorified him not as God"), and Revelation 16:9 records the eschatological refusal ("they repented not to give him glory") using the same three Greek lexemes as 14:7 — making the plagues the direct consequence of the rejected gospel call.

## Key Verses

> **The Command to Give Glory (Revelation 14:7)** Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

> **The Refusal to Give Glory (Revelation 16:9)** And men were scorched with great heat, and blasphemed the name of God, which hath power over these plagues: and they repented not to give him glory.

> **Christ's Model of Glorification (John 17:4)** I have glorified thee on the earth: I have finished the work which thou gavest me to do.

> **The Failure to Glorify (Romans 1:21)** Because that, when they knew God, they glorified him not as God, neither were thankful; but became vain in their imaginations, and their foolish heart was darkened.

> **The Beholding-Transformation Mechanism (2 Corinthians 3:18)** But we all, with open face beholding as in a glass the glory of the Lord, are changed into the same image from glory to glory, even as by the Spirit of the Lord.

> **Bodily Glorification Commanded (1 Corinthians 6:19-20)** What? know ye not that your body is the temple of the Holy Ghost which is in you, which ye have of God, and ye are not your own? For ye are bought with a price: therefore glorify God in your body, and in your spirit, which are God's.

> **Faith as Glory-Giving (Romans 4:20)** He staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief; but was strong in faith, giving glory to God.

> **Fruit-Bearing as Glorification (John 15:8)** Herein is my Father glorified, that ye bear much fruit; so shall ye be my disciples.

> **Glory Equals Character (Exodus 33:18-19)** And he said, I beseech thee, shew me thy glory. And he said, I will make all my goodness pass before thee, and I will proclaim the name of the LORD before thee.

> **The Confession Idiom (Joshua 7:19)** And Joshua said unto Achan, My son, give, I pray thee, glory to the LORD God of Israel, and make confession unto him; and tell me now what thou hast done; hide it not from me.

## Analysis

### What Is Glory? The Kabod/Doxa Foundation

Before asking how glory is given, the question of what glory IS must be settled. The Hebrew word kabod (H3519, 214 occurrences in the OT) comes from the root kabad (H3513, 141 occurrences), which means "to be heavy." The root meaning is literal weight — what has substance commands honor. When applied to God, kabod denotes the full weight of who He is: His character, His attributes, His presence.

The decisive passage is Exodus 33:18-19. Moses asks to see God's glory (kebodeka). If glory were primarily visible radiance, God would have responded with a light display. Instead, He promises: "I will make all my goodness pass before thee, and I will proclaim the name of the LORD." The next chapter fulfills the promise (Exod 34:5-7): God proclaims His name as "The LORD, The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth, keeping mercy for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear the guilty." The content of God's glory is a list of character attributes — mercy, grace, patience, goodness, truth, forgiveness, and justice. This equation — glory equals character — is the interpretive key for every passage in this study.

The LXX translators rendered kabod as doxa (G1391, 168 occurrences in the NT) in approximately 170 co-occurrences. In classical Greek, doxa meant "opinion" or "reputation." Through the LXX, it absorbed kabod's meanings: weight, substance, character, radiance. The word study data confirms this semantic bridge. Paul may preserve the kabod etymology deliberately in 2 Corinthians 4:17, where he describes an "eternal weight of glory" (baros doxes) — the Greek word for "weight" (baros) mirroring kabod's root meaning. When the first angel commands dote auto doxan ("give him glory"), the kabod/doxa tradition means: give God the full weight of recognition His character deserves.

John 1:14 carries this equation into the incarnation: "We beheld his glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father, full of grace and truth." The phrase "grace and truth" (charis kai aletheia) echoes Exodus 34:6's "goodness and truth" (chesed ve-emeth). What Moses encountered on Sinai, the disciples encountered in Christ. The glory of God — His character — became visible in a human life.

### The Confession Dimension: "Give Glory" as Truth-Telling Before God

One layer of "give glory" carries specific forensic force. In Joshua 7:19, after Israel's defeat at Ai, Joshua commands Achan: "Give glory to the LORD God of Israel, and make confession unto him; and tell me now what thou hast done." The Hebrew uses sim kabod la-YHWH ("put/place glory to YHWH") in parallel with ten lo todah ("give him confession"). The two commands are equated — giving glory IS making confession. This is a judicial-oath formula: a demand for truthful testimony before God.

The dual meaning of the Hebrew verb yadah (H3034, 136 occurrences) underlies this connection. In the Hiphil stem, yadah means "to praise/give thanks." In the Hithpael, it means "to confess." Both senses involve acknowledging that God is right — praise acknowledges His worthiness; confession acknowledges His justice. The noun todah (H8426, from yadah) carries the same duality: thanksgiving and confession. In Joshua 7:19, both meanings activate simultaneously.

John 9:24 proves this idiom survived into first-century Greek usage. When the Pharisees investigate the healing of the blind man, they demand: "Give God the praise" (dos doxan to theo, using didomi G1325 + doxa G1391 + dative). This is the identical construction to Revelation 14:7's dote auto doxan, used in a judicial setting demanding honest testimony. The formula is consistent across Testaments.

Malachi 2:2 adds the consequence dimension: "If ye will not lay it to heart, to give glory unto my name... I will send a curse upon you." The Hebrew latheth kabod lishmi (nathan H5414 + kabod H3519) uses the same construction. Failure to give glory brings cursing — the prophetic precedent for what happens in Revelation 16 when glory is refused.

The confession dimension means that when the first angel says "give glory," one thing he means is: tell the truth about God. Acknowledge that He is right and you are wrong. Stop suppressing what you know about Him. This connects directly to Romans 1:18, where the problem begins with those who "hold the truth in unrighteousness" — they possess truth about God but refuse to acknowledge it.

### Romans 1:18-25: The Exact Inverse of the First Angel's Message

Prior study 3am-03 identified Romans 1:18-32 as a structural antiparallel to Revelation 14:7. This study's Greek-level analysis confirms the identification. The chain in Romans 1 is explicit:

1. **Knew God through creation** (vv.19-20) — parallels Rev 14:7's "worship him that made heaven, and earth."
2. **"Glorified him not as God"** (v.21, edoxasan, V-AAI-3P of doxazo G1392) — inverts Rev 14:7's "give him glory" (dote doxan).
3. **"Became vain in their imaginations, and their foolish heart was darkened"** (v.21b) — the cognitive consequence of refusing to glorify.
4. **"Changed the glory of the uncorruptible God into an image"** (v.23, ellaxan ten doxan... en homoiomati eikonos) — the glory-exchange, using eikon (G1504), the same word for the beast's "image" in Revelation 13.
5. **God gave them up** (vv.24, 26, 28, paredoken) — parallels the plagues in Revelation 14:9-11 and 16:1-11.

The verbal connection between Romans 1:21 (edoxasan, aorist of doxazo) and Revelation 14:7 (doxan, accusative of doxa) is through the same root: doxa/doxazo (G1391/G1392). The constructions differ — Romans uses the verb doxazo while Revelation uses didomi + the noun doxa — but the semantic field is identical. What Romans 1:21 describes as humanity's fundamental failure ("glorified him not as God"), Revelation 14:7 addresses as God's remedy ("give him glory"). The first angel's message is the divine answer to the human condition Paul diagnoses.

The parallel extends to the end of each sequence. Romans 1:25 describes the result of the glory-exchange: "worshipped and served the creature rather than the Creator." Revelation 14:7 commands the opposite: "worship him that made." The ktisis/ktisas (creature/Creator) vocabulary in Romans 1:25 parallels the poieo (to make) vocabulary in Revelation 14:7. Both passages deal with the Creator-creature relationship, but Romans describes its perversion while Revelation commands its restoration.

### The Rev 14:7 / Rev 16:9 Verbal Echo: The Seal-Plague Inclusio

The Greek of Revelation 14:7 reads: dote (V-2AAM-2P, second aorist active imperative of didomi G1325) auto (P-DSM) doxan (N-ASF of doxa G1391). Three lexemes: didomi, autos, doxa.

The Greek of Revelation 16:9 reads: dounai (V-2AAN, aorist active infinitive of didomi G1325) auto (P-DSM) doxan (N-ASF of doxa G1391). The same three lexemes: didomi, autos, doxa.

This is a precise verbal echo, not merely a thematic parallel. The only grammatical change is the mood: imperative (dote, the command) becomes infinitive (dounai, the purpose they refused). The shift is theologically significant. The imperative offered in the gospel call (14:7) becomes the infinitive refused under the plagues (16:9). What was presented as a possibility ("give glory!") is now recorded as a failure ("they repented not so as to give glory"). The command did not change — the response changed.

The ou metenoasan (V-AAI-3P of metanoeo G3340, "they did not repent") connects the refusal to glorify with the refusal to repent. Repentance (metanoeo, 36 occurrences in the NT) is fundamentally a cognitive reorientation — a deliberate change of mind and will. The refusal to repent IS the refusal to glorify, because giving glory requires acknowledging God's rightness, which IS repentance. This is why the verbal echo matters: the same people who heard the command in 14:7 are the ones who refuse it in 16:9. The plagues do not fall on the uninformed but on those who rejected the gospel call.

Revelation 11:13 provides a positive counterpart: "The remnant were affrighted, and gave glory to the God of heaven" (edokan doxan to theo tou ouranou, using the same didomi + doxa construction). Some do respond — the construction that records the refusal in 16:9 also records the acceptance in 11:13. Revelation 4:9 establishes the heavenly pattern (hotan dososin ta zoa doxan, "when the living creatures give glory"), and Revelation 15:4 projects universal glorification after the judgment ("who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name?").

### Christ as the Model of Giving Glory: John 17:4

"I have glorified thee on the earth: I have finished the work which thou gavest me to do" (John 17:4). The Greek reads: ego se edoxasa epi tes ges, to ergon teleiosas ho dedokas moi hina poieso. Two verbs carry the weight: edoxasa (V-AAI-1S of doxazo G1392, "I have glorified") and teleiosas (V-AAP-NSM of teleioo G5048, "having finished"). The aorist participle teleiosas is circumstantial and epexegetical — it explains HOW the glorification happened. Bengel's observation is grammatically supported: "I have finished" explains "I have glorified." Glorification equals completing the assigned work.

The aorist tense treats Christ's entire earthly mission as one accomplished act. Meyer notes that Jesus stands at the goal of His earthly activity, already including His death as accomplished. The perfect tense dedokas ("which thou hast given me," V-RAI-2S of didomi G1325) indicates the Father's assignment was settled and permanent. Christ received a specific mission and completed it — that completion IS glorification.

This establishes a model for believers. If Christ glorified the Father by finishing the work, then believers glorify God by faithfully completing what He assigns. The parallel in John 4:34 confirms this: "My meat is to do the will of him that sent me, and to finish his work" (teleioso, V-AAS-1S of teleioo G5048). Christ's sustenance — His deepest motivation — was work-completion as glorification.

The cross itself is the supreme act of glory-giving. In John 13:31-32, the word doxazo appears five times in two verses, triggered by Judas' departure to betray Jesus. The betrayal does not defeat glorification — it initiates it. John 12:28 frames the cross with the Father's declaration: "I have both glorified it, and will glorify it again." The past and future glorification bracket the cross as the central act. And John 19:30's tetelestai ("It is finished," from teleo G5055, 30 occurrences) is the dying counterpart to John 17:4's teleiosas — both are completion declarations, both are acts of glorification.

John 17:22 extends the model to believers: "The glory which thou gavest me I have given them." The perfect tense (dedoka) indicates settled, permanent transmission. Glory flows from the Father through the Son to believers. This answers the paradox of Romans 3:23 ("all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God"): believers can give glory because they have received glory through Christ. The supply chain is Father → Son → believers → world.

### The Beholding-Transformation Mechanism: How the Glory-Deficit Is Resolved

Second Corinthians 3:18 describes the process by which glory is both received and given: "We all, with open face beholding as in a glass the glory of the Lord, are changed into the same image from glory to glory, even as by the Spirit of the Lord."

The Greek reveals three key elements. First, katoptrizomenoi (V-PMP-NPM of katoptrizomai G2734, a hapax legomenon — only 1 occurrence in the NT) is a present middle participle meaning "beholding as in a mirror." The middle voice favors "beholding" over "reflecting" — the subject is actively gazing, not passively mirroring. Second, metamorphoumetha (V-PPI-1P of metamorphoo G3339, present passive indicative) means "we are being transformed." The present tense indicates ongoing process; the passive voice identifies the Spirit as the agent. Metamorphoo (4 occurrences total) is the same verb used for Christ's transfiguration (Matt 17:2, Mark 9:2), showing the transformation is real and radical — a change of essential form, not superficial appearance. Third, apo doxes eis doxan ("from glory to glory") describes progressive transformation. The doxa at the start (received from the Lord) becomes the doxa at the end (reproduced in the believer).

This passage is the mechanism that makes the first angel's command possible. Romans 3:23 states the problem: all fall short of God's glory. John 17:22 provides the supply: Christ transmits His received glory to believers. But 2 Corinthians 3:18 describes the process: sustained contemplation of Christ's glory, mediated by the Spirit, produces progressive transformation into the same image. Glory is not produced by the believer but received through beholding and reproduced through transformation.

The OT background is Exodus 34:29-35, where Moses' face shone after encountering God's glory on Sinai. But Moses' glory faded — he had to wear a veil. Paul's point is that the new covenant surpasses this: the veil is removed, the beholding is direct, and the glory does not fade but increases ("from glory to glory"). Romans 8:29 identifies the predestined goal of this process: "conformed to the image of his Son." First John 3:2 identifies its eschatological completion: "We shall be like him; for we shall see him as he is." The beholding-transformation mechanism, begun now, will be perfected when Christ appears.

### Seven Modes of Giving Glory

The evidence reveals at least seven distinct but interconnected modes of giving glory to God:

**1. Confession and truth-telling.** Joshua 7:19 and John 9:24 establish the forensic dimension: giving glory means acknowledging God's rightness and telling the truth about one's own condition. Philippians 2:11 projects this to its eschatological completion: every tongue confessing (exomologesetai, from exomologeo G1843, 11 occurrences) that Jesus Christ is Lord, "to the glory of God the Father."

**2. Character acknowledgment.** Exodus 33-34 equates glory with character. Isaiah 42:12 commands: "Let them give glory unto the LORD, and declare his praise in the islands." Declaring God's character attributes — His mercy, justice, faithfulness — IS giving Him glory.

**3. Visible conduct.** Matthew 5:16 and 1 Peter 2:12 describe a communal mode: believers' "beautiful works" (kala erga, using kalos G2570, 102 occurrences — meaning aesthetically compelling, not just morally correct) cause observers to glorify God. This is glory-giving through others — the believer's life becomes the arena where outsiders encounter God's character and respond with praise. Galatians 1:24 illustrates it concretely: the Judean churches "glorified God in me" — Paul's transformed life was the trigger.

**4. Fruit-bearing.** John 15:8 states directly: "Herein is my Father glorified, that ye bear much fruit." The fruit is not self-generated — "without me ye can do nothing" (John 15:5). The mechanism is abiding (menete, present imperative — continuous connection), not effort. Philippians 1:11 specifies: "fruits of righteousness, which are by Jesus Christ" (dia Iesou Christou) — Christ is the source. The believer's role is to stay connected; Christ produces the fruit; the Father receives the glory.

**5. Bodily stewardship.** First Corinthians 6:20 commands: "Glorify God in your body" (doxasate, V-AAM-2P of doxazo G1392, aorist imperative — demanding decisive action). The purchase-price argument (egorasthete, "you were bought," from agorazo G59) establishes ownership: the body was ransomed and now belongs to its purchaser. The body is called naos (G3485, 46 occurrences) — not the outer temple precinct (hieron) but the inner sanctuary where God's presence dwells. First Corinthians 10:31 universalizes the scope: "Whether therefore ye eat, or drink, or whatsoever ye do, do all to the glory of God." The immediate context in 1 Corinthians 6 addresses sexual purity, but the principle extends to all bodily conduct — eating, drinking, everything. Romans 12:1 adds: "Present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable service" (logiken latreian — "logical worship," from logos G3056). Bodily presentation is not irrational devotion but the logically necessary response to God's mercies.

**6. Beholding-transformation.** Second Corinthians 3:18 describes the mechanism by which glory is received and progressively reproduced. The believer contemplates Christ's glory (katoptrizomenoi); the Spirit transforms the believer into the same image (metamorphoumetha); the transformation proceeds from glory to glory. This mode resolves the paradox: those who lack glory (Rom 3:23) receive it through beholding and then transmit it through transformed character.

**7. Completed work.** John 17:4 establishes the model: "I have glorified thee on the earth: I have finished the work." Christ glorified the Father by completing the assigned mission. The pattern extends to believers: faithfully doing what God assigns, finishing the work, is itself an act of glorification. Abraham illustrates this through faith specifically — Romans 4:20 describes him as "giving glory to God" (dous doxan to theo, using the same didomi + doxa + dative construction as Rev 14:7) by trusting the promise despite impossible circumstances. Faith IS glory-giving because it acknowledges God's faithfulness and power — the weight of His character.

These seven modes are not competing definitions but facets of one reality. The first angel's command encompasses all of them because glory-giving is the comprehensive response of the whole person — mind (confession), mouth (praise), character (transformation), conduct (visible works), body (stewardship), connection (fruit-bearing), and faithfulness (completed work) — to the full character of God.

### The Glory-Judgment Connection: Why "The Hour of His Judgment Is Come" Follows "Give Glory"

The hoti (G3754) clause in Revelation 14:7 connects the glory imperative to the judgment announcement: "Give glory to him, FOR (hoti) the hour of his judgment is come." The most natural grammatical reading is causal: give glory BECAUSE judgment has arrived. But the structure also works as content-declarative: the everlasting gospel proclaims THAT judgment has come.

Either way, the logic is consistent across Scripture. Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 provides the OT template: "Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man. For God shall bring every work into judgment." The structure — fear God → obedience → because judgment — anticipates the first angel. Acts 17:30-31 shows Paul using identical logic at Athens: "God now commandeth all men every where to repent: Because he hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world." Creator-right-to-judge is the theological link: the God who made heaven and earth has the authority to evaluate His creatures' response to His character. This is why the creation language in Revelation 14:7 ("worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters," echoing Exodus 20:11) directly precedes the judgment announcement. The Creator IS the Judge.

The word krisis (G2920, 47 occurrences) means judicial process — not automatically condemnation (katakrima, G2631). The covenant-lawsuit background (riyb H7378 → krisis through the LXX) shows that God's judgment is a legal proceeding in which His character is both the standard and the vindication. When the righteous give glory, they acknowledge that God's judgment is fair. When the wicked refuse to give glory (Rev 16:9), they deny what the judgment reveals.

### The Negative Evidence: What Happens When Glory Is Refused

The study identifies a consistent pattern of consequences for refusing to glorify God. Daniel 5:23: Belshazzar praised gods of gold, silver, brass, iron, wood, and stone — but "the God in whose hand thy breath is, and whose are all thy ways, hast thou not glorified." Judgment came that night (Dan 5:30). Acts 12:23: Herod accepted divine acclamation without redirecting glory to God — "because he gave not God the glory" (ouk edoken ten doxan to theo, using the same didomi + doxa construction), he was struck dead immediately. Leviticus 10:3: Nadab and Abihu offered unauthorized worship and were consumed; God's principle: "I will be glorified in them that come nigh me" (ekkabbed, Niphal of kabad H3513). Numbers 20:12: Moses' failure to sanctify/glorify God through unbelief barred him from the promised land.

The severity is proportional to the privilege. Belshazzar knew God's history with his grandfather Nebuchadnezzar. Herod stood before a crowd that had just heard apostolic preaching. Nadab and Abihu served in the sanctuary itself. Moses had spoken with God face to face. The closer the proximity to revelation, the greater the obligation to glorify — and the greater the consequence of refusal.

Revelation 16:9 is the eschatological climax of this pattern. The wicked under the fourth plague know God has authority (exousia G1849) over the plagues — they know who He is — yet "they repented not to give him glory." Their refusal is not ignorance but defiance. The same people who heard the first angel's command (Rev 14:7) now refuse it under judgment (Rev 16:9). The plagues are not arbitrary punishments but the consequence of rejected glory.

## Word Studies

The kabod (H3519) → doxa (G1391) bridge is the linguistic foundation. Kabod (214 occurrences) from kabad (H3513, 141 occurrences, meaning "to be heavy") was rendered as doxa (168 occurrences) in approximately 170 LXX co-occurrences. Classical doxa ("opinion/reputation") was infused with kabod's meanings of weight, substance, character, and radiance.

The doxazo verb (G1392, 71 occurrences) concentrates in John's Gospel (16 of 54 unique verses, 30%), especially in chapters 12-17. Christ's edoxasa ("I have glorified," John 17:4) and humanity's ouk edoxasan ("they glorified not," Rom 1:21) use the same verb in opposite directions.

The didomi (G1325, 430 occurrences) + doxa construction appears across both Testaments: Rev 14:7 (dote, imperative), Rev 16:9 (dounai, infinitive), Rev 11:13 (edokan, indicative), Rom 4:20 (dous, participle), Acts 12:23 (ouk edoken, negative), John 9:24 (dos, imperative), Luke 17:18 (dounai, infinitive). The Hebrew equivalents: Josh 7:19 (sim + kabod), Mal 2:2 (latheth + kabod), 1 Chr 16:28-29 (habuw + kabod). This construction is a recognized biblical idiom across languages, centuries, and literary genres.

The hapax katoptrizomai (G2734, 1 occurrence) in 2 Cor 3:18 and the rare metamorphoo (G3339, 4 occurrences) describe the transformation process. The middle voice of katoptrizomai favors "beholding" over "reflecting." Metamorphoo — the transfiguration verb — indicates real change of essential form, not surface adjustment.

Metanoeo (G3340, 36 occurrences) in Rev 16:9 connects repentance to glory-giving. The cognitive nature of metanoeo (change of mind/perception) aligns with the knowledge dimension of glory: recognizing who God is leads to acknowledging His glory, which IS repentance.

## Difficult Passages

### The Glory-Deficit Paradox (Romans 3:23)
If "all have sinned and come short of the glory of God," then no human being possesses glory to give. The command to "give glory" (Rev 14:7) seems to demand what is impossible. The resolution comes from the supply chain: John 17:22 (Christ transmits received glory to believers), 2 Corinthians 3:18 (sustained beholding produces progressive transformation), and John 15:5 ("without me ye can do nothing" — fruit is Christ-generated). Glory-giving is not a human achievement but a grace-enabled response. The believer receives glory through connection to Christ and transmits it through transformed living. This means "give glory" is not a command to produce something from nothing but an invitation to enter a relationship that produces what is commanded.

### The Hoti Clause in Revelation 14:7 — Causal or Declarative?
If hoti is causal ("give glory BECAUSE judgment has come"), then the impending judgment motivates glory-giving. If content-declarative ("the gospel, namely THAT the hour of judgment has come"), then the judgment announcement is part of the gospel content itself. Both readings have grammatical support. The causal reading fits the immediate syntax more naturally, but the declarative reading fits the larger context where the angel is proclaiming what the everlasting gospel says. The two readings are not mutually exclusive — the judgment both motivates the response and constitutes part of the message. Prior study gsp-07 established that judgment is not contrary to gospel but integral to it (Acts 17:30-31; Rom 2:16).

### Katoptrizomai in 2 Corinthians 3:18 — Beholding or Reflecting?
The debate between "beholding as in a mirror" and "reflecting as a mirror" affects the theology of glory-giving. If "beholding," the emphasis falls on reception — the believer gazes at Christ's glory and is changed. If "reflecting," the emphasis falls on transmission — the believer mirrors Christ's glory to the world. The middle voice of katoptrizomai (G2734) favors "beholding" in standard Greek usage, and the Exodus background (Moses beheld God's glory on the mountain) supports this. But Moses also reflected the glory afterward (his face shone). Both are likely present: beholding produces reflecting. The transformation described is both reception and transmission — you cannot give what you have not received, but what you receive you inevitably display.

### The Scope of Bodily Glorification (1 Corinthians 6:20)
Paul's immediate context addresses sexual immorality (porneia, v.18). The question is whether the principle "glorify God in your body" extends beyond sexual ethics to all bodily stewardship — diet, health, physical habits. The answer is yes, based on two pieces of evidence: (1) the purchase-price argument ("ye are bought with a price") does not limit itself to one category of bodily behavior — if the body belongs to God, all bodily conduct falls under the obligation; (2) 1 Corinthians 10:31 explicitly universalizes the principle two chapters later: "Whether therefore ye eat, or drink, or whatsoever ye do, do all to the glory of God." Paul himself extends the scope from sexual purity to eating and drinking to "whatsoever ye do." The principle is broader than the immediate context.

## Implications for the Three Angels' Messages

The second imperative of the everlasting gospel — "give glory to him" — is not an isolated command but the relational center of the first angel's message. Study 3am-05 established that "fear God" is the internal orientation (reverential awe). "Give glory" is the comprehensive response — the full acknowledgment of God's character through confession, conduct, transformation, and faithful living. "Worship the Creator" is the behavioral expression. The three imperatives are one reality viewed from three angles: internal, relational, external.

The Romans 1:18-25 inverse framework reveals that "give glory" is the precise remedy for humanity's fundamental condition. The human race's problem is not primarily behavioral but relational — "they glorified him not as God" (Rom 1:21). Behavior deteriorates as a consequence of that relational failure. The first angel addresses the root, not the symptoms: restore the glory-giving relationship, and everything downstream changes.

The Rev 14:7/16:9 verbal echo shows that the same gospel call that offers salvation in the seal period becomes the standard of judgment in the plague period. Those who reject the command to give glory (14:7) are recorded as those who refused to give glory (16:9). The plagues are not arbitrary — they are the consequence of a rejected gospel. This means the proclamation of "give glory" is simultaneously an offer of grace and a solemn warning.

Christ's model in John 17:4 makes glory-giving concrete rather than abstract. It is not mystical contemplation detached from life but the faithful completion of assigned work. It is bearing fruit through abiding in the vine (John 15:8). It is bodily stewardship grounded in the purchase-price of redemption (1 Cor 6:20). It is conduct that others can observe and be drawn to the Father (Matt 5:16). And it is transformation through sustained beholding of Christ's character (2 Cor 3:18) — not self-improvement but Spirit-driven change from glory to glory.

The first angel's message, then, is not merely an announcement but a comprehensive call to reorient all of life — mind, body, relationships, work, worship — toward giving God the full weight of recognition His character deserves. It is the cure for the glory-exchange described in Romans 1 and the antidote to the beast-worship described in Revelation 13. It is what Habakkuk 2:14 envisions: the earth filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD, as the waters cover the sea.

## Conclusion

"Give glory to him" encompasses everything this study has examined — confession, character acknowledgment, conduct, fruit-bearing, bodily stewardship, beholding-transformation, and completed work — because glory-giving is the creature's comprehensive response to the Creator's revealed character. The kabod/doxa foundation shows that glory means the full weight of who God is (Exod 33:18-19; 34:5-7). The confession idiom (Josh 7:19; John 9:24) adds the forensic dimension of truth-telling before God. Christ's model (John 17:4) establishes that glorification equals faithful work-completion. The beholding-transformation mechanism (2 Cor 3:18) resolves the paradox of how glory-deficient humans (Rom 3:23) can obey the command — by receiving glory through sustained contemplation of Christ, enabled by the Spirit. The bodily passages (1 Cor 6:20; 10:31; Rom 12:1) extend glory-giving into every physical activity. The fruit-bearing passages (John 15:8; Phil 1:11) show that the glory given is Christ-generated through abiding. Abraham's faith (Rom 4:20) identifies belief itself as glory-giving, using the same didomi + doxa construction as Revelation 14:7.

The judgment follows because the Creator who reveals His character has the right to evaluate the creature's response (Rev 14:7; Eccl 12:13-14; Acts 17:30-31). Romans 1:18-25 describes the exact failure the first angel addresses — the refusal to glorify the known God — and Revelation 16:9 records the eschatological consequence of that refusal in the same three Greek words that carried the original command. The first angel's message is the gospel's call to reverse the glory-exchange, to acknowledge who God truly is, and to live every dimension of life as a response to that acknowledgment — fear within, glory expressed, worship rendered to the One who made heaven and earth.
