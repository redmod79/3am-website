    Web Research Findings¶

    Study Question¶

    What is the charagma (mark, G5480) and what is the sphragis (seal, G4973)? How do they relate to the Deuteronomy 6:6-8 / Exodus 13:9 hand-and-forehead loyalty pattern? How does the Ezekiel 9:4 tav (mark on the forehead of those who sigh and cry) prefigure the seal of God? What is the relationship between God's seal and the Sabbath — does Exodus 31:13,17 and Ezekiel 20:12,20 establish the Sabbath as the seal's content? How does a seal function — identifying who seals (God's name), what authority they have (Creator), and over what domain (heaven and earth) — and which commandment contains all three elements? How does the Spirit-seal connection (Eph 1:13, 4:30) relate to the Sabbath-seal claim? What is the composite argument for the seal being connected to Sabbath observance?

    1. Paul Kroll — "Revelation 13 and the Mark of the Beast" (GCI Archive)¶

    URL: https://archive.gci.org/articles/revelation-13-and-the-mark-of-the-beast/ Key claims: - Charagma was "the technical term for the Roman imperial stamp that appeared on various documents" - It functioned as "a seal stamped with the name and date of the emperor and attached to commercial documents" and "stood for the emperor's head stamped on coins" - "Disobedient slaves were often branded with marks of ownership, much like cattle are today" - "Religious tattooing was widespread. Soldiers had a custom of branding themselves with the name of a favorite general. Devotees of a god labeled themselves with tattoos to designate their loyal devotion" - References M. Eugene Boring's interpretation that understanding the mark required recognizing "what it was, that it was in fact the beast empowered by Satan" Greek analysis: Charagma identified as a technical Roman term encompassing imperial stamps, coin impressions, slave brands, and religious tattoos Relevance: Establishes the Greco-Roman background for charagma — economic control, ownership marking, loyalty branding Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    2. Biblical Blueprints — "The Mark of the Beast" (Revelation 13:16-18 Sermon)¶

    URL: https://biblicalblueprints.com/Sermons/New%20Testament/Revelation/Revelation%2013/Revelation%2013-16-18 Key claims: - Cites Adolf Deissmann, Light from the Ancient East (pp. 276-277, 340f.): charagma denoted "an engraved, etched, branded, or inscribed 'mark' or 'sign'" and the impress on coins gave the term its broader sense of "money" - Slave branding: runaway slaves bore letters "FUG" (fugitivus); all enslaved termed stigmatias due to branding with master's name - Military branding: soldiers marked on hands "with the name of their general" (citing Aelian and Ambrose) - Pompeii inscriptions referenced women by gematrial number values ("I love her whose number is 545") - Lexical sources cited: BDAG defines charagma as "a mark that is engraved, etched, branded, cut, imprinted"; TDNT references inscriptions, document stamps, animal brands, coin impressions, slave stigmatization; Montanari's Brill Dictionary: "stamped sign, brand...coin money" - "While God's elect receive an invisible seal (using different Greek vocabulary allowing both visible/invisible interpretation), charagma specifically denotes visible, physical marking" Greek analysis: Extensive lexical survey distinguishing charagma (visible, physical) from sphragis terminology Relevance: Core scholarly background for charagma with multiple lexical references; the visible/invisible distinction between mark and seal is significant Status: Lead to verify — especially the claim that charagma is exclusively visible while sphragis allows invisible interpretation

    Summary of Scholarly Landscape¶

    The Charagma/Sphragis Distinction¶

    Scholars broadly agree that charagma in its Greco-Roman context referred to imperial stamps on documents and coins, slave brands, military tattoos, and religious devotional marks. Deissmann's Light from the Ancient East remains a foundational reference. The term carried connotations of ownership, economic participation, and visible allegiance. By contrast, sphragis functioned as authentication, protection, and ownership verification — with both visible and invisible applications. The distinction between charagma (imposed mark of subjection) and sphragis (authenticating seal of relationship) maps onto the Revelation contrast between beast-mark and God-seal.

    The Ezekiel 9:4 Tav Background¶

    The tav mark in Ezekiel 9:4 is widely recognized as background for Revelation's sealing imagery. The Babylonian Talmud (Shabbat 55a) preserves the striking tradition that the tav represents "the seal of the Holy One" because God's seal is emet (truth), which ends with tav. The paleo-Hebrew tav was cross-shaped. The mark functioned as protective exemption from judgment for those who "sigh and cry" over abominations — a moral/loyalty criterion, not ritual compliance. This provides the OT template for Revelation 7:1-3.

    The Sabbath-Seal Debate¶

    This is the most contested area. The Adventist position builds a composite argument: (1) Exodus 31:13,17 and Ezekiel 20:12,20 identify the Sabbath as God's "sign"; (2) Romans 4:11 shows "sign" and "seal" can be interchangeable; (3) the fourth commandment uniquely contains the three elements of a seal (name, title, territory); (4) therefore the Sabbath is the content of God's seal. Critics counter with: (1) the linguistic separation of "sign" (oth/semeion) from "seal" (chotham/sphragis) — they are never interchanged in translation; (2) the NT explicitly identifies the seal as the Holy Spirit (Eph 1:13, 4:30, 2 Cor 1:22); (3) the seal is received by faith, not by works. The most substantive scholarly exchange concerns whether the sign/seal vocabulary separation is absolute (Life Assurance Ministries) or whether Romans 4:11 demonstrates functional overlap. No peer-reviewed journal article was found that directly arbitrates this specific linguistic question.

    The Spirit-Seal Question¶

    Mainstream evangelical scholarship treats Ephesians 1:13 and 4:30 as definitive: the seal is the Holy Spirit given at conversion. The Adventist response (found in popular sources but not well-represented in the scholarly articles retrieved) is that the Spirit and Sabbath are not competing alternatives — the Spirit is the agent of sealing while the Sabbath is the content or sign of what is sealed. This harmonization needs biblical verification.

    Research Directives for Downstream Agents¶

    1. Verify the sign/seal linguistic separation claim: Life Assurance Ministries claims chotham and oth are never interchanged. Check Romans 4:11 in Greek — does Paul use both semeion and sphragis for circumcision? If so, the strict separation breaks down.

    2. Verify the name-title-domain argument: Check whether the fourth commandment (Exodus 20:8-11) actually contains all three seal elements (name of God, title as Creator, domain as heaven and earth). Check whether any other commandment contains these elements.

    3. Examine Exodus 31:13,17 and Ezekiel 20:12,20: What Hebrew word is used for "sign" in these passages? Is it oth? What is the precise covenant context — is the Sabbath sign limited to Israel or universal?

    4. Examine Ephesians 1:13 and 4:30 in Greek: What is the precise grammatical relationship between "seal" and "Holy Spirit"? Is the Spirit the seal itself, or the agent who applies the seal? Check the instrumental dative vs. appositional reading.

    5. Verify the Deut 6:6-8 / Exodus 13:9 hand-forehead pattern: Confirm the Hebrew text places God's words as signs on the hand and frontlets between the eyes. Verify that Revelation's mark-on-hand-and-forehead language echoes this OT loyalty pattern.

    6. Check Ezekiel 9:4 in Hebrew: What is the Hebrew word for "mark"? Is it tav (the letter name) or another word? Verify the connection to Revelation 7:2-3 sealing.

    7. Evaluate the Talmudic claim: Shabbat 55a says the tav is "the seal of the Holy One" (emet). While the Talmud is not Scripture, this shows how Jewish tradition understood the Ezekiel 9 mark — as God's seal. Check whether Revelation's sealing language draws on this tradition.

    8. Test the composite Sabbath-seal argument: The argument chains together multiple links (Sabbath = sign, sign = seal, seal contains name/title/domain, fourth commandment = only commandment with all three). Each link must be independently verified. A break in any link weakens the chain.

    9. Examine the Deissmann charagma evidence: Verify that charagma was used for imperial stamps and slave brands in Roman practice (Deissmann, Light from the Ancient East, pp. 276-277, 340f.). The BDAG, TDNT, and Montanari lexical entries should be checked.

    10. Address the works-salvation critique: Critics argue the Sabbath-seal position makes salvation dependent on works. The study should examine whether the biblical seal/mark framework is about salvific merit or eschatological loyalty identification — these are different categories.
