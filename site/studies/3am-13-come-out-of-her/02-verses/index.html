    Verses¶

    Question¶

    Rev 18:4 'come out of her my people.' God's people inside Babylon. OT come-out tradition (Abraham, Exodus, literal Babylon). 2 Cor 6:14-18. Hina-me purpose clause. John 10:16. Fall-then-call sequence.

    Expanded fall announcement — demon-dwelling imagery preceding the come-out call¶

    And after these things I saw another angel come down from heaven, having great power; and the earth was lightened with his glory. And he cried mightily with a strong voice, saying, Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird. For all nations have drunk of the wine of the wrath of her fornication, and the kings of the earth have committed fornication with her, and the merchants of the earth are waxed rich through the abundance of her delicacies.

    Context: Rev 18 opens with an angel of great authority repeating the second angel's message of 14:8 with expanded detail. The egeneto (is become) marks progressive deterioration. Demon-dwelling imagery absent from 14:8 is now added.

    Notes: Expansion from Rev 14:8 to 18:2 confirms Bohr's claim: demon-dwelling imagery is ADDED in 18:2 that was absent in 14:8. The egeneto verb (V-2ADI-3S, aorist middle indicative of ginomai) marks transition — Babylon HAS BECOME this. The doubled epesen epesen is prophetic aorist (V-2AAI-3S) as established in 3am-12.

    CRUX TEXT — 'Come out of her, my people' with dual hina-me purpose clauses¶

    And I heard another voice from heaven, saying, Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues. For her sins have reached unto heaven, and God hath remembered her iniquities.

    Context: Another voice from heaven (allon phonen) — distinguished from the angel of 18:1. The possessive 'my people' (ho laos mou) is a covenant designation. The dual hina-me clauses give the PURPOSE of the command: (1) not sharing sins, (2) not receiving plagues.

    Notes: CRITICAL PARSING: Exelthate (V-2AAM-2P) = aorist active imperative, 2nd person plural of exerchomai — decisive, urgent command. Ho laos mou = nominative-for-vocative (article T-NSM + noun N-NSM in direct address). Two hina-me clauses: (1) hina me synkoinonesete (V-AAS-2P, aorist subjunctive of synkoinoneo G4790) tais hamartiais autes — 'lest you co-participate in her sins'; (2) hina me labete (V-2AAS-2P, aorist subjunctive of lambano G2983) — 'lest you receive of her plagues.' The aorist subjunctive in both views the action as a bounded potential event — they have NOT YET done it. Rev 18:5: ekollethesan (V-API-3P, aorist passive of kollao G2853) — sins 'were glued together' reaching heaven. Emnemoneussen (V-AAI-3S of mnemoneuo G3421) — God remembered.

    Judgment specification following the come-out call — double recompense and 'in one hour'¶

    Reward her even as she rewarded you, and double unto her double according to her works: in the cup which she hath filled fill to her double. How much she hath glorified herself, and lived deliciously, so much torment and sorrow give her: for she saith in her heart, I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow. Therefore shall her plagues come in one day, death, and mourning, and famine; and she shall be utterly burned with fire: for strong [is] the Lord God who judgeth her.

    Context: The come-out call is immediately followed by the judgment decree. The double-for-double recompense pattern echoes the lex talionis. The 'in one day/hour' refrain (18:8,10,17,19) emphasizes sudden destruction. Babylon's self-delusion ('I sit a queen, am no widow') parallels Isa 47:7-8.

    Notes: The sequence is critical: come-out call (v.4) → reason (v.5, sins reached heaven) → judgment decree (vv.6-8). This confirms the fall-then-call-then-judgment pattern.

    Vindication of holy ones — God avenges against Babylon¶

    Rejoice over her, [thou] heaven, and [ye] holy apostles and prophets; for God hath avenged you on her.

    Context: Rev 18:20 uses mixed vocative construction: ourane (true vocative) + hoi hagioi kai hoi apostoloi kai hoi prophetai (articular nominative-for-vocative). This answers the martyrs' 'how long?' from Rev 6:10.

    Notes: The nominative-for-vocative in the address to saints, apostles, and prophets matches the grammar reference entry pattern (Rev 18:20 is explicitly cited there).

    Blood of prophets and saints — ultimate reason for the come-out call¶

    And in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth.

    Context: The chapter's climactic indictment: Babylon is guilty of the blood of the righteous. This provides the final theological reason for the come-out call — the system is not merely wrong but murderous.

    Notes: Connects to Rev 17:6 (drunk with the blood of saints) and the SP037 altar vindication arc (Rev 6:10 → 18:20,24).

    OT come-out command — 'Go ye forth of Babylon, flee ye from the Chaldeans'¶

    Go ye forth of Babylon, flee ye from the Chaldeans, with a voice of singing declare ye, tell this, utter it [even] to the end of the earth; say ye, The LORD hath redeemed his servant Jacob.

    Context: Isaiah's command to leave literal Babylon after the Persian conquest. The tone is joyful — 'with a voice of singing.' The exodus from Babylon is framed as redemption.

    Notes: The verb is yatsa (H3318) in imperative. The command pairs departure with proclamation — those who leave are to DECLARE what God has done.

    OT come-out command — doubled 'depart' with touch-not prohibition¶

    Depart ye, depart ye, go ye out from thence, touch no unclean [thing]; go ye out of the midst of her; be ye clean, that bear the vessels of the LORD.

    Context: The second Isaianic come-out command, immediately following the gospel announcement of 52:7-10. Doubled imperative (suru suru) intensifies the urgency. The 'touch no unclean thing' prohibition is quoted by Paul in 2 Cor 6:17.

    • סוּרוּ () — Verb.Qal.Impv.2mp — — turn aside, DOUBLED) | צְאוּ (H3318 Verb.Qal.Impv.2mp — go out, appears TWICE) | מִשָּׁם (H4480+H8033 — from there) | טָמֵא (H2930 Adj.ms.Abs — unclean) | אַל תִּגָּעוּ (H408+H5060 Neg + Verb.Qal.Impf.2mp — do not touch) | מִתֹּוכָהּ (H4480+H8432+3fs — from the midst of her) | הִבָּרוּ (H1305 Verb.Niphal.Impv.2mp — purify yourselves) | נֹשְׂאֵי כְּלֵי יְהוָה (H5375 Verb.Qal.Ptcp + H3627 Noun.mp.Cst + H3068 — bearers of the vessels of YHWH)

    Notes: HEBREW PARSING: suru suru (H5493, Qal Impv 2mp DOUBLED — 'turn aside, turn aside'), tseu (H3318, Qal Impv 2mp — 'go out') appears TWICE, tame (H2930 — 'unclean'), al-tiggau (H5060, Qal Impf 2mp — 'do not touch'), mittokah (H8432 — 'from the midst of her'), hibbaru (H1305, Niphal Impv 2mp — 'purify yourselves'). The LXX renders tseu with exerchomai (G1831) — the same root as exelthate in Rev 18:4 and 2 Cor 6:17.

    OT come-out command — 'Remove out of the midst of Babylon'¶

    Remove out of the midst of Babylon, and go forth out of the land of the Chaldeans, and be as the he goats before the flocks.

    Context: Jeremiah's first come-out command in the oracle against Babylon (Jer 50-51). The 'he goats before the flocks' imagery suggests leading the way out.

    Notes: Part of the 12 verbal parallels between Jer 50-51 and Rev 17-18 established in 3am-12.

    OT come-out command — 'Flee out of the midst of Babylon, deliver every man his soul'¶

    Flee out of the midst of Babylon, and deliver every man his soul: be not cut off in her iniquity; for this [is] the time of the LORD'S vengeance; he will render unto her a recompence.

    Context: The urgency intensifies: 'flee' (nus, H5127). The reason is explicit: 'be not cut off in her iniquity' — staying means sharing her punishment. 'The time of the LORD's vengeance' parallels Rev 18:8.

    Notes: The warning 'be not cut off in her iniquity' parallels the first hina-me clause of Rev 18:4 ('lest you share her sins'). The vengeance/recompense language parallels Rev 18:6.

    Closest OT parallel to Rev 18:4 — 'My people, go ye out of the midst of her'¶

    My people, go ye out of the midst of her, and deliver ye every man his soul from the fierce anger of the LORD.

    Context: The closest verbal parallel to Rev 18:4. Contains both 'my people' (ammi, H5971+1cs) and 'go out of the midst of her' (tseu mittokah). The reason is 'the fierce anger of the LORD' — paralleling 'her plagues' in Rev 18:4.

    • צְאוּ () — Verb.Qal.Impv.2mp — — go out) | מִתֹּוכָהּ (H4480+H8432+3fs — from the midst of her) | עַמִּי (H5971+1cs — my people) | וּמַלְּטוּ (H4422 Verb.Piel.Impv.2mp — escape/deliver) | אִישׁ (H376 Noun.ms.Abs — man/each) | אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ (H853+H5315+3ms — his soul) | מֵחֲרוֹן (H4480+H2740 — from the burning/fierce) | אַף יְהוָה (H639+H3068 — anger of YHWH)

    Notes: HEBREW PARSING: tseu (H3318, Qal Impv 2mp — 'go out'), mittokah (H8432+3fs — 'from her midst'), ammi (H5971+1cs — 'my people'), umaltu (H4422, Piel Impv 2mp — 'escape/deliver'), ish et-nafsho ('each man his soul'), mekharon af-YHWH ('from the fierce anger of YHWH'). This is the text Rev 18:4 most directly alludes to.

    Post-exilic come-out call — 'Flee from the land of the north'¶

    Ho, ho, [come forth], and flee from the land of the north, saith the LORD: for I have spread you abroad as the four winds of the heaven, saith the LORD. Deliver thyself, O Zion, that dwellest [with] the daughter of Babylon.

    Context: Post-exilic prophet still calling Jews to leave Babylon, showing many remained even after the Persian conquest. 'Dwellest with the daughter of Babylon' — covenant people inside Babylon by circumstance.

    Notes: Significant: even after Babylon's political fall, God's people remained inside and needed calling out. This supports the progressive-fall reading — the come-out call continues after the initial fall announcement.

    Abraham's original come-out call — proto-Babylon departure¶

    Now the LORD had said unto Abram, Get thee out of thy country, and from thy kindred, and from thy father's house, unto a land that I will shew thee:

    Context: The first 'come out' call in Scripture. Abraham is called from Ur of the Chaldeans — Chaldea being a synonym for Babylon. The call requires leaving country, kindred, and father's house — total separation.

    Notes: Stephen confirms: 'The God of glory appeared unto our father Abraham, when he was in Mesopotamia' (Acts 7:2). The LXX uses exerchomai. Heb 11:8: 'he went out, not knowing whither he went.' Gal 3:8 connects Abraham's call to the everlasting gospel.

    Lot's come-out from Sodom — urgency and shared destruction¶

    And the men said unto Lot, Hast thou here any besides? son in law, and thy sons, and thy daughters, and whatsoever thou hast in the city, bring [them] out of this place: For we will destroy this place, because the cry of them is waxen great before the face of the LORD; and the LORD hath sent us to destroy it. And Lot went out, and spake unto his sons in law, which married his daughters, and said, Up, get you out of this place; for the LORD will destroy this city. But he seemed as one that mocked unto his sons in law. And when the morning arose, then the angels hastened Lot, saying, Arise, take thy wife, and thy two daughters, which are here; lest thou be consumed in the iniquity of the city. And while he lingered, the men laid hold upon his hand, and upon the hand of his wife, and upon the hand of his two daughters; the LORD being merciful unto him: and they brought him forth, and set him without the city. And it came to pass, when they had brought them forth abroad, that he said, Escape for thy life; look not behind thee, neither stay thou in all the plain; escape to the mountain, lest thou be consumed.

    Context: Lot is a righteous man (2 Pet 2:7) inside a corrupt city. The angels compel departure. The warning 'lest thou be consumed in the iniquity of the city' directly parallels the first hina-me clause of Rev 18:4.

    Notes: The 'cry' of Sodom 'waxen great before the LORD' (Gen 19:13) parallels 'her sins have reached unto heaven' (Rev 18:5). 2 Pet 2:6-7: 'delivered just Lot, vexed with the filthy conversation of the wicked.' Lot was righteous but proximity brought danger.

    Korah rebellion — 'Depart from the tents of these wicked men, touch nothing'¶

    And he spake unto the congregation, saying, Depart, I pray you, from the tents of these wicked men, and touch nothing of theirs, lest ye be consumed in all their sins.

    Context: Moses commands physical separation from Korah's faction before divine judgment falls. The 'touch nothing' prohibition echoes Isa 52:11 and 2 Cor 6:17. Proximity = shared destruction.

    Notes: The pattern: (1) rebellion identified, (2) separation commanded, (3) those who remain are destroyed. Num 16:41-50 shows that even AFTER separation, 14,700 died from plague — proximity to rebellion brings consequences.

    Lo-ammi / ammi reversal — 'not my people' becomes 'my people'¶

    Then said [God], Call his name Loammi: for ye [are] not my people, and I will not be your [God]. Yet the number of the children of Israel shall be as the sand of the sea, which cannot be measured nor numbered; and it shall come to pass, [that] in the place where it was said unto them, Ye [are] not my people, [there] it shall be said unto them, [Ye are] the sons of the living God.

    Context: Hosea's children symbolize Israel's covenant status. Lo-ammi ('not my people') represents covenant revocation. But the reversal is promised: in the same place, 'not my people' will become 'sons of the living God.'

    Notes: The lo-ammi/ammi reversal illuminates who ho laos mou are in Rev 18:4 — people who were formerly 'not my people' being reclaimed through the gospel call. Applied by Paul in Rom 9:25-26 and Peter in 1 Pet 2:10.

    Hosea reversal fulfilled — 'I will say to them not my people, Thou art my people'¶

    And I will sow her unto me in the earth; and I will have mercy upon her that had not obtained mercy; and I will say to [them which were] not my people, Thou [art] my people; and they shall say, [Thou art] my God.

    Context: The covenant restoration promise. God reverses the lo-ammi designation. The NT applies this to Gentile inclusion (Rom 9:25) and to the new covenant community (1 Pet 2:10).

    Notes: The ammi (H5971+1cs, 'my people') designation is explicitly restored. This is the OT foundation for God calling people 'my people' who are currently outside the visible covenant community — exactly what Rev 18:4 does.

    Paul quotes Hosea reversal — Gentile inclusion as 'my people'¶

    As he saith also in Osee, I will call them my people, which were not my people; and her beloved, which was not beloved. And it shall come to pass, [that] in the place where it was said unto them, Ye [are] not my people; there shall they be called the children of the living God.

    Context: Paul applies the Hosea reversal to explain Gentile inclusion in God's people. The principle: God can designate people as 'my people' before they are visibly part of the covenant community.

    Notes: kaleo (G2564) — 'I will CALL them my people.' The calling creates the identity. Applied to Rev 18:4: God CALLS people 'my people' while they are still inside Babylon.

    Peter quotes Hosea — 'not a people' becomes 'the people of God'¶

    But ye [are] a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, an holy nation, a peculiar people; that ye should shew forth the praises of him who hath called you out of darkness into his marvellous light: Which in time past [were] not a people, but [are] now the people of God: which had not obtained mercy, but now have obtained mercy.

    Context: Peter applies the Hosea reversal to the new covenant community. The phrase 'called you out of darkness into his marvellous light' is itself a come-out formulation.

    Notes: PARSING: laos (G2992) appears twice in v.10 — 'not a people' / 'people of God.' kalesantos (G2564, Aor Act Ptcp) = 'the one who called you OUT OF darkness INTO light.' The come-out call is embedded in the ecclesiology: God's people are CALLED OUT of one sphere INTO another.

    True shepherd enters by the door — sheep hear his voice¶

    Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that entereth not by the door into the sheepfold, but climbeth up some other way, the same is a thief and a robber. But he that entereth in by the door is the shepherd of the sheep. To him the porter openeth; and the sheep hear his voice: and he calleth his own sheep by name, and leadeth them out. And when he putteth forth his own sheep, he goeth before them, and the sheep follow him: for they know his voice. And a stranger will they not follow, but will flee from him: for they know not the voice of strangers.

    Context: The framework for understanding the come-out call as the shepherd's voice. The sheep HEAR and FOLLOW. The stranger's voice they will NOT follow. Recognition of the shepherd's voice is the prerequisite for leaving.

    Notes: The phone (G5456, voice) of the shepherd is the mechanism of the come-out call. Rev 18:4 uses phonen ek tou ouranou — 'a voice from heaven.' John 10:3 describes the shepherd 'calling his own sheep by name and leading them out' — exerchomai again.

    Other sheep not of this fold — aule vs poimne distinction¶

    And other sheep I have, which are not of this fold: them also I must bring, and they shall hear my voice; and there shall be one fold, [and] one shepherd.

    Context: Jesus declares he has sheep OUTSIDE the Jewish institutional fold (aule). The dei (divine necessity, G1163) indicates this is God's mandate. The KJV 'one fold' mistranslates poimne (flock).

    Notes: CRITICAL PARSING: aules (G833, N-GSF — fold/courtyard) ≠ poimne (G4167, N-NSF — flock). The sheep are not of this AULE (institutional fold) but will become one POIMNE (living flock) under one POIMEN (shepherd). Dei (G1163, V-PAI-3S) = divine necessity. The asyndetic construction 'mia poimne, heis poimen' (one flock, one shepherd) without conjunction emphasizes unified identity. This explains how God can have 'my people' in Babylon — they belong to his FLOCK even while in the wrong FOLD.

    My sheep hear my voice — recognition as the basis of the come-out call¶

    My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me: And I give unto them eternal life; and they shall never perish, neither shall any [man] pluck them out of my hand.

    Context: The sheep-shepherd relationship is defined by VOICE RECOGNITION and FOLLOWING. This is the mechanism by which the come-out call of Rev 18:4 functions — God's people inside Babylon HEAR the shepherd's voice and follow.

    Notes: The ou me apoloontai (emphatic double negation — 'shall NEVER perish') provides security assurance for those who respond to the shepherd's voice. The come-out call is not abandonment but rescue.

    First synkoinoneo parallel — 'Have no fellowship with unfruitful works of darkness'¶

    And have no fellowship with the unfruitful works of darkness, but rather reprove [them].

    Context: Paul's prohibition against co-participation in 'works of darkness.' The verb synkoinoneite (G4790) is present imperative — an ongoing prohibition. Paired with elenchete (reprove) — exposure goes with separation.

    Notes: PARSING: synkoinoneite (G4790, V-PAM-2P, Pres Act Impv) — present tense = ongoing prohibition ('keep not co-participating'). This contrasts with Rev 18:4's aorist subjunctive (potential single event). The 'unfruitful works of darkness' maps to Babylon's sins. The addition 'rather reprove them' shows separation is not passive withdrawal but active exposure.

    Second synkoinoneo occurrence — positive sharing in affliction¶

    Notwithstanding ye have well done, that ye did communicate with my affliction.

    Context: The Philippians synkoinonesantes (G4790, aorist participle) Paul's affliction — positive co-participation. This confirms the verb means ACTIVE participation, not mere proximity.

    Notes: PARSING: synkoinonesantes (G4790, V-AAP-NPM, Aor Act Ptcp) — 'having shared in.' The positive use proves the verb means real, active participation. If synkoinoneo can describe genuine sharing in Paul's suffering, then synkoinonesete in Rev 18:4 describes genuine sharing in Babylon's sins — not mere geographical proximity.

    Two-cup/two-table framework — participation requiring departure¶

    The cup of blessing which we bless, is it not the communion of the blood of Christ? The bread which we break, is it not the communion of the body of Christ? For we [being] many are one bread, [and] one body: for we are all partakers of that one bread. Behold Israel after the flesh: are not they which eat of the sacrifices partakers of the altar? What say I then? that the idol is any thing, or that which is offered in sacrifice to idols is any thing? But [I say], that the things which the Gentiles sacrifice, they sacrifice to devils, and not to God: and I would not that ye should have fellowship with devils. Ye cannot drink the cup of the Lord, and the cup of devils: ye cannot be partakers of the Lord's table, and of the table of devils.

    Context: Paul's two-table framework: participation at one table excludes participation at the other. The cup of the Lord and the cup of demons are mutually exclusive. This provides the theological framework for understanding why the come-out call requires DEPARTURE, not merely mental disagreement.

    Notes: PARSING (10:20): koinonous ton daimonion (G2844, N-APM — 'partakers of demons'). (10:21): ou dunasthe poterion Kyriou pinein kai poterion daimonion — 'you cannot drink the cup of the Lord and the cup of demons.' metechein (G3348) = partake. Established in 3am-11: this is why the remedy is 'come OUT' rather than just 'think more clearly.'

    NT composite OT catena — five antitheses and chiastic citation structure¶

    Be ye not unequally yoked together with unbelievers: for what fellowship hath righteousness with unrighteousness? and what communion hath light with darkness? And what concord hath Christ with Belial? or what part hath he that believeth with an infidel? And what agreement hath the temple of God with idols? for ye are the temple of the living God; as God hath said, I will dwell in them, and walk in [them]; and I will be their God, and they shall be my people. Wherefore come out from among them, and be ye separate, saith the Lord, and touch not the unclean [thing]; and I will receive you, And will be a Father unto you, and ye shall be my sons and daughters, saith the Lord Almighty.

    Context: Paul's most extensive 'come out' application. Heterozygeo (v.14, NT hapax from LXX Lev 19:19) frames the argument as species-mixing violation. Five antitheses (righteousness/unrighteousness, light/darkness, Christ/Belial, believer/infidel, temple/idols). Chiastic OT catena: A (v.16b) Lev 26:12/Ezek 37:27, B (v.17a) Isa 52:11, B' (v.17b) 'touch not,' A' (v.18) 2 Sam 7:14 modified.

    Notes: PARSING (6:14): heterozygountes (G2086, V-PAP-NPM, Pres Act Ptcp — NT hapax, 'being unequally yoked'), metoche (G3352, N-NSF — partnership, NT hapax), koinonia (G2842, N-NSF). (6:17): exelthate (G1831, V-2AAM-2P — SAME FORM as Rev 18:4), aphoristhete (G873, V-APM-2P Aor Pass Impv — 'be separated'), akathartou (G169 — 'unclean'). The 'Lord Almighty' (pantokrator) closing occurs only here and in Revelation in the NT. Paul modifies 2 Sam 7:14: singular 'son' → plural 'sons and daughters.'

    Original fall announcement — doubled epesen establishing fall-then-call sequence¶

    And there followed another angel, saying, Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication.

    Context: The second angel's original message. The hoti clause gives the REASON: 'because she made all nations drink.' No demon-dwelling imagery yet (contrast 18:2). The doubled epesen is prophetic aorist.

    Notes: The fall announcement (14:8) precedes the come-out call (18:4) in the narrative sequence. The hoti clause supports Bohr's claim that Babylon falls because she rejected the first angel's message — she 'made all nations drink' in response to the gospel call of 14:6-7.

    Repeated fall announcement with demon-dwelling detail added¶

    And he cried mightily with a strong voice, saying, Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird.

    Context: The expanded repetition of 14:8. New content: katoiketerion daimonion (habitation of demons), phulake pantos pneumatos akathartou (prison of every unclean spirit), phulake pantos orneou akathartou kai memisemenou (cage of every unclean and hated bird).

    Notes: Comparison with 14:8 confirms progressive deterioration. 14:8 has no demon-dwelling imagery; 18:2 adds three demonic descriptions. The egeneto (V-2ADI-3S, 'is become') marks transition — she was not always this way.

    Transfer language — delivered from darkness, translated into the kingdom¶

    Who hath delivered us from the power of darkness, and hath translated [us] into the kingdom of his dear Son: In whom we have redemption through his blood, [even] the forgiveness of sins:

    Context: NT 'transfer' language — the theological reality behind the come-out call. God delivers (errusato) from one domain and transfers (metestesen) into another. The come-out call is not just leaving but being relocated.

    Notes: The come-out tradition is not merely negative (leaving corruption) but positive (entering the kingdom). Col 1:13 provides the NT theological framework for what happens when someone responds to Rev 18:4.

    OT source for 'Babylon is fallen, is fallen'¶

    And, behold, here cometh a chariot of men, [with] a couple of horsemen. And he answered and said, Babylon is fallen, is fallen; and all the graven images of her gods he hath broken unto the ground.

    Context: Isaiah's original 'Babylon is fallen, is fallen' — the source text for Rev 14:8 and 18:2. The doubled form in Isaiah announces historical Babylon's fall to Persia.

    Notes: The LXX of Isa 21:9 uses peptoken peptoken (perfect of pipto) while Rev 14:8/18:2 use epesen epesen (aorist). The doubled form is preserved across Testaments.

    God distinguishes his people from Egypt during plagues¶

    And I will sever in that day the land of Goshen, in which my people dwell, that no swarms [of flies] shall be there; to the end thou mayest know that I [am] the LORD in the midst of the earth. And I will put a division between my people and thy people: to morrow shall this sign be.

    Context: God puts a 'division' (peduth, H6304) between his people and the Egyptians during the plagues. This is the Exodus type of the come-out separation — God's people are protected FROM the plagues.

    Notes: The pattern: God separates his people from the judged BEFORE the plagues fall. This directly parallels the second hina-me clause of Rev 18:4: 'lest you receive of her plagues.' The plagues of Rev 15-16 correspond to the Egyptian plagues in this typological framework.

    Sodom's cry reaching God — parallel to Rev 18:5¶

    And the LORD said, Because the cry of Sodom and Gomorrah is great, and because their sin is very grievous; I will go down now, and see whether they have done altogether according to the cry of it, which is come unto me; and if not, I will know.

    Context: The cry of Sodom 'reaches' God, triggering the judgment sequence. This directly parallels Rev 18:5 where Babylon's sins 'have reached unto heaven.'

    Notes: The reaching-heaven motif: Gen 18:20 (Sodom's cry reaches God → judgment), Gen 11:4 (Babel's tower reaches heaven → judgment), Ezra 9:6 (iniquities 'grown up unto the heavens'), Jer 51:9 ('her judgment reacheth unto heaven'), Rev 18:5 ('her sins have reached unto heaven'). The pattern: once sins cross this threshold, judgment is inevitable.

    First angel's message -- the gospel call Babylon rejects¶
