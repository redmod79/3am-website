    Question¶

    What does phobethete ton theon ("fear God") mean as the first imperative of the everlasting gospel? How does the OT yare/phobos tradition inform this command — is it servile terror or reverent awe? How does Deuteronomy 6:13 and 10:12-13 pair fear and love? Does "fear God" conflict with "perfect love casts out fear" (1 John 4:18)? Parse the 1 John 4:18 text carefully — what does phobos meta kolaseos ("fear with punishment") mean, and is that the same kind of fear as Rev 14:7? How does fearing God function as the foundation for refusing to fear the beast and his threats (Rev 13:15, Matt 10:28)? How does Ecclesiastes 12:13 ("Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man") provide the OT template for the first angel's message?

    Focus Areas¶

    FA-1. The Semantic Range of yare/yirah (H3372/H3374) in the OT Fear-of-God Tradition: The OT uses multiple Hebrew words for fear, and their semantic ranges are distinct. H3372 yare and its noun form H3374 yirah carry the primary meaning of reverential awe — the 'fear of the LORD' that is the beginning of wisdom (Prov 1:7), paired with love in Deut 10:12, and defined as hating evil in Prov 8:13. By contrast, H367 eimah denotes terror (Gen 15:12, Exod 15:16) and H6343 pachad denotes dread or being startled. Exodus 20:18-20 is the critical test case: at Sinai the people feared (yare) and Moses said 'fear not' (al-tir'u) — then immediately said God came 'that his fear (yirah) may be before your faces, that ye sin not.' Two different uses of the same root in one passage, distinguishing servile terror from reverent moral orientation. The LXX bridge is crucial: if phobos consistently translates yirah rather than eimah, then NT phobeo inherits the reverence tradition, not the terror tradition. Verses: Prov 1:7, Prov 8:13, Prov 9:10, Prov 14:26-27, Deut 10:12-13, Exod 20:18-20, Gen 22:12, Job 28:28, Isa 11:2-3, Eccl 12:13, Ps 111:10 Strong's: H3372, H3374, H367, H6343, H4172, G5399, G5401

    FA-2. Phobethete ton Theon — Grammar and Theology of the Imperative in Rev 14:7: Moskala claims phobethete ton theon in its specific imperative form occurs only once in the entire Bible. The verb phobeo (G5399) appears 93 times in the NT, but the imperative mood usage needs verification. If phobethete is indeed aorist passive imperative, this is significant: the aorist aspect frames the command as a decisive, whole-action response rather than an ongoing process; the passive voice suggests being moved to fear rather than manufacturing it. The three imperatives of Rev 14:7 (phobethete, dote, proskynesate) may form a climactic triad — fear leads to giving glory leads to worship. The Reading Acts article suggests a 'proleptic aorist or aorist of assurance' for the judgment announcement, which would affect how the fear command relates to the judgment hour declaration. Verses: Rev 14:7, Rev 14:6, Rev 15:4, Matt 10:28, Luke 12:5, Acts 10:2 Strong's: G5399, G5401, G1325, G4352

    FA-3. Deuteronomy's Fear-Love-Serve-Obey Pattern (Deut 6:5,13; 10:12-13): Deuteronomy presents the most developed OT theology of the fear-love relationship. In Deut 10:12-13, Moses asks 'What doth the LORD thy God require of thee?' and answers with a five-part sequence: fear the LORD → walk in all his ways → love him → serve him → keep his commandments. Fear comes FIRST — not as an alternative to love but as its foundation. Chapter 6 places the love command (v.5, 'love the LORD thy God with all thine heart') alongside the fear command (v.13, 'thou shalt fear the LORD thy God, and serve him') within the same covenantal instruction. Jesus quotes 6:13 in Matt 4:10 during the temptation, connecting it to exclusive worship. The pattern recurs in farewell addresses: Moses (Deut), Joshua (Josh 24:14), Samuel (1 Sam 12:14,24), Solomon (Eccl 12:13). Each pairs fear with obedience and love. Verses: Deut 10:12-13, Deut 6:5, Deut 6:13, Deut 5:29, Deut 13:4, Josh 24:14, 1 Sam 12:14, 1 Sam 12:24, Matt 4:10 Strong's: H3372, H157, H5647, H8104, H3212

    FA-4. Parsing 1 John 4:18 — What Kind of Fear Does Perfect Love Cast Out?: First John 4:18 says 'There is no fear in love; but perfect love casteth out fear: because fear hath punishment (he phobos kolasin echei).' The critical phrase is kolasin echei — fear 'has' or 'involves' punishment/torment. G2851 kolasis appears only twice in the NT: here and Matt 25:46 ('everlasting punishment'). Vincent distinguishes kolasis (corrective punishment) from timoria (purely retributive penalty). The fear being cast out is specifically the fear that anticipates punitive consequences — the dread of condemnation. This is NOT the reverential awe commanded in Rev 14:7. Supporting evidence: 1 John 4:17 mentions 'boldness in the day of judgment' — the context is judicial fear. Romans 8:15 distinguishes the 'spirit of bondage to fear' from the 'spirit of adoption.' First Peter 1:17 (same apostolic tradition) shows both coexisting: calling God 'Father' AND passing time 'in fear.' Bengel's progression — no love/no fear → fear without love → fear and love → love without fear — deserves testing against the biblical data. Verses: 1 John 4:18, 1 John 4:16-17, 1 John 5:3, Matt 25:46, Rom 8:15, 2 Tim 1:7, 1 Pet 1:17, Heb 12:28-29 Strong's: G5401, G2851, G5046, G26, G906, G1854, G3954

    FA-5. Fearing God as Foundation for Refusing Beast-Worship (Rev 13:15 / Matt 10:28): Revelation 13-14 presents a binary choice: worship the beast (under threat of death, Rev 13:15) or fear God (under the gospel invitation, Rev 14:6-7). The mechanisms are opposite — the beast uses terror (death threat) while God uses gospel (good news). Matt 10:28 provides the foundational principle: 'Fear not them which kill the body, but are not able to kill the soul: but rather fear him which is able to destroy both soul and body in hell.' The one who has already settled whom they ultimately fear will not be shaken by the beast's death threat. Daniel 3:16-18 provides the paradigm: Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refused the death-penalty worship command because their fear of God exceeded their fear of the furnace. The Ecclesiastes study identified the principle 'humans obey what they fear most.' This is not merely theoretical — it is the existential logic of the final crisis. Verses: Rev 13:15, Rev 13:4, Rev 14:6-7, Rev 14:9-10, Matt 10:28, Luke 12:4-5, Dan 3:16-18, Dan 6:10, Heb 11:32-37, Acts 4:19, Acts 5:29 Strong's: G5399, G5401, G4352, G615

    FA-6. Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 as OT Template for the First Angel's Message: Ecclesiastes 12:13 — 'Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man' — maps structurally onto Rev 14:7,12. The fear God command (yare, H3372) corresponds to phobethete (G5399). 'Keep his commandments' (shamar mitsvotav) corresponds to 'keep the commandments of God' (Rev 14:12). The judgment declaration (Eccl 12:14, 'God shall bring every work into judgment') corresponds to 'the hour of his judgment is come' (Rev 14:7). The prior study (3am-04) verified the LXX bridges: yare → phobeo (241 co-occurrences), mitsvah → entole (153 co-occurrences). This pattern also appears in the farewell addresses of Moses (Deut 10:12-13), Joshua (Josh 24:14), and Samuel (1 Sam 12:14,24). Ecclesiastes represents Solomon's farewell, making four OT leaders who all conclude with the same fear-obedience-judgment triad. The first angel restates this universal pattern on a global scale. Verses: Eccl 12:13, Eccl 12:14, Rev 14:7, Rev 14:12, Deut 10:12-13, Josh 24:14, 1 Sam 12:14, 1 Sam 12:24, Eccl 3:14 Strong's: H3372, H8104, H4687, H4941, G5399, G1785, G2920

    FA-7. Fear and Wisdom — The Fear of the LORD Tradition in Proverbs and Job: The wisdom tradition defines fear of the LORD as the epistemological foundation for all knowledge (Prov 1:7), the beginning of wisdom (Prov 9:10), and the source of life itself (Prov 14:27). Proverbs gives fear active moral content: 'The fear of the LORD is to hate evil' (8:13). It produces not terror but 'strong confidence' (14:26) and is 'a fountain of life' (14:27). Job 28:28 places fear as the conclusion of the divine wisdom speech: 'The fear of the Lord, that is wisdom; and to depart from evil is understanding.' Psalm 130:4 makes a remarkable claim: 'There is forgiveness with thee, that thou mayest be feared' — forgiveness produces fear, not eliminates it. This directly contradicts the assumption that grace and fear are opposed. Isaiah 11:2-3 shows that even the Messiah operates with 'the spirit of the fear of the LORD.' If Christ himself delights in the fear of God, the command to fear God cannot be sub-Christian. Verses: Prov 1:7, Prov 8:13, Prov 9:10, Prov 14:26-27, Prov 16:6, Prov 15:33, Prov 19:23, Prov 22:4, Job 28:28, Ps 111:10, Ps 130:4, Ps 34:9-11, Isa 11:2-3 Strong's: H3374, H2451, H998, H7225

    FA-8. Filial vs. Slavish Fear — Two Types Distinguished in Scripture and History: The biblical evidence supports at minimum two categories of fear. Exodus 20:18-20 is the paradigm case: Israel 'feared' (yare) at Sinai, and Moses said both 'Do not fear' (al-tir'u, same root) AND 'that his fear (yirah) may be before your faces, that ye sin not.' Moses distinguishes the terror response from the moral orientation in a single breath. Rom 8:15 contrasts the 'spirit of bondage to fear' (douleia eis phobon) with the 'spirit of adoption' (huiothesia) — slavish fear vs. family relationship. Second Timothy 1:7 denies God gives a 'spirit of fear' (deilia, G1169 — cowardice/timidity) while affirming power, love, and a sound mind. First John 4:18 specifies the fear being cast out as fear that 'has punishment' (kolasin echei) — judicial dread. Meanwhile, Heb 12:28 commands serving God 'with reverence and godly fear' (eulabeias kai deous) in the very context of receiving grace. The EGW corpus identifies 'filial fear' arising from love for God's character and 'slavish fear' as its opposite — this maps onto the biblical vocabulary but must be verified text by text rather than assumed. Verses: Exod 20:18-20, Rom 8:15, 2 Tim 1:7, 1 John 4:18, Heb 12:28-29, 1 Pet 1:17, James 2:19, Ps 2:11, Phil 2:12, 2 Cor 5:10-11, 2 Cor 7:1 Strong's: G5401, G1169, G1397, G5206, G2124, G2851, H3372, H367, H6343

    FA-9. Fear as the Relational Foundation — Fear, Worship, and Obedience Unified: Revelation 14:7 presents three imperatives: phobethete (fear), dote (give glory), proskynesate (worship). These are not three separate commands but one reality viewed from three angles. The Bohr corpus notes that Jesus interprets Isa 29:13 as connecting fear → worship → commandments: 'their fear toward me is taught by the precept of men' leads to vain worship. When fear is wrong, worship becomes wrong. The Nave's entry for FEAR lists it as 'A MOTIVE OF OBEDIENCE' with extensive scripture citations. Rev 14:12 confirms: the result of responding to the three angels is 'they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus.' The prior study (gsp-10) established that the fear word group shows worship (proskyneo) as the behavioral expression of godly fear. Cornelius (Acts 10:2) demonstrates the pattern in practice: fearing God manifested as devout living, generosity, prayer, and righteousness. Fear is not one item on a checklist but the foundation that generates all the others. Verses: Rev 14:7, Rev 14:12, Isa 29:13, Matt 15:8-9, Acts 10:2, Acts 10:35, Deut 6:13, Matt 4:10, Ps 96:4-9, Rev 15:4 Strong's: G5399, G1325, G1391, G4352, H7812, H3372

    FA-10. Psalm 130:4 — Forgiveness Produces Fear, Not Eliminates It: Psalm 130:4 is one of the most theologically dense verses in the fear-of-God tradition. The psalmist, having cried 'out of the depths' (v.1) and acknowledged that if God marks iniquities none can stand (v.3), declares: 'But there is forgiveness with thee, that thou mayest be feared.' The purpose clause is remarkable: forgiveness exists SO THAT God may be feared. This demolishes the false dichotomy between grace and fear. The experience of being forgiven — understanding the depth of sin and the magnitude of mercy — produces exactly the kind of reverent awe that constitutes the fear of God. This aligns with the EGW corpus claim that filial fear 'arises from love for God and his character' and 'is fed and kept alive by the word of God and prayer.' It also explains why the 'everlasting gospel' naturally produces the fear command: the gospel IS the grounds for fearing God properly. Verses: Ps 130:4, Ps 130:1-3, Ps 34:9-11, Mal 1:6, Mal 3:16, Neh 5:15, Luke 7:47, Luke 1:50 Strong's: H3372, H5547, G5399
