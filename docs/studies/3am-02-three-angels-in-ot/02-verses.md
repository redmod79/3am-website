# Verses

## Question
Is the three-part pattern of the three angels' messages — (1) gospel call to worship the true God, (2) exposure of a false system, (3) warning of judgment — repeated throughout the Old Testament? Examine: Noah before the flood, Abraham and Sodom, Moses and Egypt, Elijah at Carmel, Josiah's reform, Jeremiah's ministry. Do these share the same structure as Rev 14:6-12?

### First angel's message — everlasting gospel, fear God, worship the Creator

> And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

**Context:** Revelation 14 follows the beast system of chapter 13. The three angels' messages (14:6-12) are the divine counter-proclamation. The first angel establishes the baseline pattern: gospel proclamation (euangelion aionion), call to worship (proskyneo), and announcement of judgment hour (krisis).

**Notes:** Three aorist imperatives: phobethete (fear), dote (give), proskynerate (worship). The Creation formula (heaven, earth, sea, fountains of waters) echoes Exodus 20:11. This verse establishes the three-part vocabulary chain to trace through OT episodes.

### Third angel's message — judgment warning against false worship

> And the third angel followed them, saying with a loud voice, If any man worship the beast and his image, and receive his mark in his forehead, or in his hand, The same shall drink of the wine of the wrath of God, which is poured out without mixture into the cup of his indignation; and he shall be tormented with fire and brimstone in the presence of the holy angels, and in the presence of the Lamb: And the smoke of their torment ascendeth up for ever and ever: and they have no rest day nor night, who worship the beast and his image, and whosoever receiveth the mark of his name. **Context:** The third angel warns of judgment on those who choose false worship. The warning is urgent and specific. Verse 12 concludes with the character of those who heed the messages: commandment-keepers who hold the faith of Jesus.

**Notes:** Proskyneo appears twice in this passage — once for false worship (v.9) and once to describe those who persist in it (v.11). The thymos/orge wrath chain connects to the OT chemah/aph vocabulary via LXX. Fire and brimstone echo Gen 19:24 (Sodom destruction).

### Song of Moses and the Lamb — Exodus-to-Revelation bridge

> And they sing the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb, saying, Great and marvellous are thy works, Lord God Almighty; just and true are thy ways, thou King of saints. Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name? for thou only art holy: for all nations shall come and worship before thee; for thy judgments are made manifest.

**Context:** Rev 15:3-4 explicitly combines the Song of Moses (Exodus 15) with the Song of the Lamb, forming a typological bridge from the Exodus judgment-on-Egypt episode to the end-time scenario. The language echoes the first angel: fear, glorify, worship, judgments.

**Notes:** Key vocabulary overlap with Rev 14:7: phobeo (fear), doxa (glory/glorify), proskyneo (worship), krisis/krima (judgment). This confirms that Revelation itself treats the Exodus pattern as a type of the end-time three-part message.

### Noah episode: 120-year probationary period — extended gospel opportunity

> And the LORD said, My spirit shall not always strive with man, for that he also is flesh: yet his days shall be an hundred and twenty years.

**Context:** God's statement limits human probation to 120 years before the flood. This represents extended gospel opportunity — time for Noah's preaching to take effect. The striving of God's Spirit is the gospel element.

**Notes:** The 120-year period shows the patience of God before judgment — the same principle seen in all six OT episodes and culminating in the 'everlasting gospel' of Rev 14:6.

**Context:** Genesis 6:5 exposes the world's condition: total moral corruption. Every imagination (yetser) of the thoughts (machsheboth) of the heart was only evil (ra) continually. This is the exposure element — the false system is universal wickedness.

### Noah episode: earth corrupt (shachath) and filled with violence (chamas)

> The earth also was corrupt before God, and the earth was filled with violence. And God looked upon the earth, and, behold, it was corrupt; for all flesh had corrupted his way upon the earth.

**Context:** Genesis 6:11-12 uses shachath (H7843) in both Niphal ('the earth was corrupt') and Hiphil ('all flesh had corrupted his way'). The word chamas (H2555, violence) completes the exposure. These are the two key terms for the pre-flood condition.

**Context:** God reveals His judgment plan to Noah personally (Amos 3:7 principle) and commands the ark's construction. The word mashchitham (Hiphil participle of shachath + 3mp suffix) — 'I am about to destroy them' — uses the same root as the corruption in v.11-12.

### Noah episode: flood judgment announced with covenant promise

> And, behold, I, even I, do bring a flood of waters upon the earth, to destroy all flesh, wherein is the breath of life, from under heaven; and every thing that is in the earth shall die. But with thee will I establish my covenant; and thou shalt come into the ark, thou, and thy sons, and thy wife, and thy sons' wives with thee.

**Context:** The judgment announcement (v.17) is immediately followed by the covenant promise (v.18). This structure mirrors the three-part pattern: judgment is announced, but salvation is offered to those who respond in faith.

**Notes:** The contrast between v.17 ('every thing that is in the earth shall die') and v.18 ('But with thee will I establish my covenant') perfectly illustrates the gospel/judgment duality in the three-part pattern.

### Noah's faith condemned the world — exposure through righteous example

> By faith Noah, being warned of God of things not seen as yet, moved with fear, prepared an ark to the saving of his house; by the which he condemned the world, and became heir of the righteousness which is by faith.

**Context:** Hebrews explains how Noah's faith functioned as exposure: 'by the which he condemned the world.' His righteous response to God's warning implicitly exposed the world's wickedness.

**Notes:** The word 'condemned' (katekrinen) shows that Noah's faithful obedience served as judgment on the world — his righteousness exposed their wickedness. This is the exposure element operating through contrast rather than explicit accusation.

### Abraham/Sodom: God reveals judgment plan — Amos 3:7 principle

> And the LORD said, Shall I hide from Abraham that thing which I do; Seeing that Abraham shall surely become a great and mighty nation, and all the nations of the earth shall be blessed in him? For I know him, that he will command his children and his household after him, and they shall keep the way of the LORD, to do justice and judgment; that the LORD may bring upon Abraham that which he hath spoken of him. And the LORD said, Because the cry of Sodom and Gomorrah is great, and because their sin is very grievous; I will go down now, and see whether they have done altogether according to the cry of it, which is come unto me; and if not, I will know.

**Context:** God reveals His plan to Abraham before acting — the Amos 3:7 principle in action. The exposure element: 'their sin is very grievous.' Abraham's subsequent intercession (18:23-33) demonstrates the gospel element — seeking mercy and appealing to divine justice.

**Notes:** God identifies the reason for judgment: the cry of Sodom is great, their sin is very grievous. Abraham appeals to God's character: 'Shall not the Judge of all the earth do right?' (18:25). This intercession is the gospel element — seeking to save the righteous from the judgment.

**Context:** Two angels enter Sodom as divine messengers. This is the most direct structural parallel to Rev 14 — literal angels carrying God's message to a doomed city before judgment falls.

**Notes:** The Hebrew malak (H4397, angel/messenger) is the same word translated aggelos (G32) in the LXX and NT. The Sodom narrative features literal angel-messengers warning before judgment, just as Rev 14 features angel-messengers.

### Abraham/Sodom: 'come out' call before judgment — parallel to Rev 18:4

> And the men said unto Lot, Hast thou here any besides? son in law, and thy sons, and thy daughters, and whatsoever thou hast in the city, bring them out of this place: For we will destroy this place, because the cry of them is waxen great before the face of the LORD; and the LORD hath sent us to destroy it. ### Abraham/Sodom: fire judgment executed after warning

> Then the LORD rained upon Sodom and upon Gomorrah brimstone and fire from the LORD out of heaven; And he overthrew those cities, and all the plain, and all the inhabitants of the cities, and that which grew upon the ground.

**Context:** Judgment falls after warning has been given and the righteous have been extracted. The fire-and-brimstone language is echoed in Rev 14:10 (third angel's warning) and directly cited by Jesus in Luke 17:29.

**Notes:** Cross-testament parallels confirm: Luke 17:29 directly cites this verse; 2 Pet 2:6 and Jude 1:7 use Sodom as a paradigm for judgment. Rev 14:10 uses the same fire-and-brimstone imagery for the third angel's judgment warning.

### Structural principle: God reveals plans to prophets before acting

> Surely the Lord GOD will do nothing, but he revealeth his secret unto his servants the prophets.

**Context:** This verse articulates the universal principle underlying all six OT episodes: God always warns before judging. Every episode features a prophetic figure (Noah, Abraham/angels, Moses, Elijah, Huldah/Josiah, Jeremiah) who receives revelation before judgment falls.

**Notes:** This is the theological foundation for why the three-part pattern repeats: God's character requires that He reveal His plans through prophets before executing judgment. The three angels of Rev 14 are the ultimate expression of this principle.

### Moses/Egypt: first worship demand — 'Let my people go that they may hold a feast'

> And afterward Moses and Aaron went in, and told Pharaoh, Thus saith the LORD God of Israel, Let my people go, that they may hold a feast unto me in the wilderness.

**Context:** The first of the repeated worship demands. The formula 'Let my people go' uses shalach (send away) and the purpose clause 'that they may serve/worship me' uses abad (H5647). This is the gospel/worship call of the Exodus episode.

**Notes:** The first instance uses 'hold a feast' (chagag) rather than 'serve' (abad). Subsequent uses (7:16, 8:1, 9:1, 9:13, 10:3) consistently use abad — serve/worship. The core demand is for freedom to worship YHWH, paralleling the first angel's call to 'worship him that made heaven and earth.'

### Moses/Egypt: repeated worship-call formula — 'Let my people go that they may serve me'

> And thou shalt say unto him, The LORD God of the Hebrews hath sent me unto thee, saying, Let my people go, that they may serve me in the wilderness: and, behold, hitherto thou wouldest not hear.

**Context:** The standard worship-call formula using abad (H5647). This formula repeats in Exo 8:1, 8:20, 9:1, 9:13, 10:3 — each time with escalating judgment for refusal.

**Notes:** The verb abad (H5647) is the core worship verb — translated 'serve' in the Exodus formula but fundamentally a worship term (see word study H5647-abad and TR-abad-douleuo). The repeated demand to serve/worship YHWH is the gospel call of the Exodus episode.

### Moses/Egypt: confirmation — 'upon their gods also the LORD executed judgments'

> For the Egyptians buried all their firstborn, which the LORD had smitten among them: upon their gods also the LORD executed judgments.

**Context:** A retrospective confirmation of Exo 12:12 — the LORD executed judgments on Egyptian gods. This confirms the exposure-of-false-system element in the Exodus episode.

**Notes:** The identical phrase 'executed judgments' (asah shephatim) appears in both Exo 12:12 and Num 33:4, bracketing the plague narrative with the explicit statement that God judged Egypt's false worship system.

### Elijah/Carmel: worship call — 'How long halt ye between two opinions?'

> And it came to pass, when Ahab saw Elijah, that Ahab said unto him, Art thou he that troubleth Israel? And he answered, I have not troubled Israel; but thou, and thy father's house, in that ye have forsaken the commandments of the LORD, and thou hast followed Baalim. Now therefore send, and gather to me all Israel unto mount Carmel, and the prophets of Baal four hundred and fifty, and the prophets of the groves four hundred, which eat at Jezebel's table. So Ahab sent unto all the children of Israel, and gathered the prophets together unto mount Carmel. And Elijah came unto all the people, and said, How long halt ye between two opinions? if the LORD be God, follow him: but if Baal, then follow him. And the people answered him not a word.

### Sodom as example — God delivers the godly while judging the ungodly

> And turning the cities of Sodom and Gomorrha into ashes condemned them with an overthrow, making them an ensample unto those that after should live ungodly; And delivered just Lot, vexed with the filthy conversation of the wicked: (For that righteous man dwelling among them, in seeing and hearing, vexed his righteous soul from day to day with their unlawful deeds;) The Lord knoweth how to deliver the godly out of temptations, and to reserve the unjust unto the day of judgment to be punished:

**Context:** Peter uses Sodom as a paradigm for how God operates: He delivers the godly (Lot) while reserving the unjust for judgment. This dual action — salvation and judgment — is exactly the three-part pattern: gospel (deliverance), exposure (unlawful deeds), judgment (day of judgment).

**Notes:** The principle in v.9 — 'The Lord knoweth how to deliver the godly out of temptations, and to reserve the unjust unto the day of judgment' — summarizes the three-part pattern's dual outcome: salvation for those who heed, judgment for those who reject.

### Sodom as 'example, suffering the vengeance of eternal fire'

> Even as Sodom and Gomorrha, and the cities about them in like manner, giving themselves over to fornication, and going after strange flesh, are set forth for an example, suffering the vengeance of eternal fire.

**Context:** Jude uses Sodom as an 'example' (deigma) of divine judgment by fire. The fire-and-brimstone language connects directly to Rev 14:10's third angel warning.

**Notes:** The word 'example' (deigma) confirms that Sodom's judgment is paradigmatic — it establishes a pattern that repeats. The 'eternal fire' connects to Rev 14:10 (fire and brimstone in the presence of the Lamb).

### Enoch's pre-flood judgment prophecy — warning tradition before Noah

> And Enoch also, the seventh from Adam, prophesied of these, saying, Behold, the Lord cometh with ten thousands of his saints, To execute judgment upon all, and to convince all that are ungodly among them of all their ungodly deeds which they have ungodly committed, and of all their hard speeches which ungodly sinners have spoken against him.

**Context:** Jude cites Enoch's prophecy as predating Noah's ministry — establishing that the warning-before-judgment tradition extends back to 'the seventh from Adam.' This pushes the three-part pattern further back in history.

**Notes:** Enoch prophesied judgment 'upon all' for 'ungodly deeds' — the exposure and judgment elements are explicit. The fourfold repetition of 'ungodly' (asebeia/asebes) emphasizes the moral exposure. This extends the pattern before Noah's time.

### Noah's ark and God's longsuffering — gospel patience before judgment

> Which sometime were disobedient, when once the longsuffering of God waited in the days of Noah, while the ark was a preparing, wherein few, that is, eight souls were saved by water.

**Context:** Peter emphasizes God's longsuffering (makrothymia) — He waited patiently during the ark's construction. The building period was itself a gospel invitation: time to repent. Only eight souls responded.

**Notes:** The 'longsuffering of God' parallels the 120-year probationary period (Gen 6:3) and connects to 2 Pet 3:9 ('not willing that any should perish'). The few who were saved (eight) anticipates the 'patience of the saints' (Rev 14:12) — the faithful remnant who heed the warning.

### Song of Moses at Red Sea — judgment and deliverance celebrated

> Then sang Moses and the children of Israel this song unto the LORD, and spake, saying, I will sing unto the LORD, for he hath triumphed gloriously: the horse and his rider hath he thrown into the sea. The LORD is my strength and song, and he is become my salvation: he is my God, and I will prepare him an habitation; my father's God, and I will exalt him.

**Context:** The Song of Moses celebrates God's judgment on Egypt and Israel's deliverance — both elements of the three-part pattern. Rev 15:3-4 explicitly combines this song with 'the song of the Lamb,' creating the Exodus-to-Revelation typological bridge.

**Notes:** This song is the post-judgment celebration after the Exodus three-part pattern has been completed: worship call (let my people go to serve), exposure (judgment on Egyptian gods), judgment (Red Sea). Rev 15:3 explicitly references this song in the end-time setting.

### Moses/Egypt: intensified worship demand — 'How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself?'

> And Moses and Aaron came in unto Pharaoh, and said unto him, Thus saith the LORD God of the Hebrews, How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself before me? let my people go, that they may serve me.

**Context:** The most intense form of the worship-call formula. God now asks 'How long?' — the same language Elijah uses at Carmel (1 Ki 18:21 'How long halt ye?'). The demand to 'humble thyself before me' (anah, H6031) adds a personal dimension to the worship call.

**Notes:** The 'How long?' formula connects the Moses/Egypt episode to the Elijah/Carmel episode. Both prophets challenge the audience's persistent refusal to acknowledge YHWH. The verb abad (H5647, 'serve me') continues the worship-call vocabulary.

### Song of Moses references Sodom — Sodom judgment applied to Israel's future

> For their vine is of the vine of Sodom, and of the fields of Gomorrah: their grapes are grapes of gall, their clusters are bitter:

**Context:** Moses's Song (Deut 32) applies Sodom language to Israel itself — those who abandon YHWH become like Sodom. This connects the Abraham/Sodom episode to Israel's own future apostasy, showing the three-part pattern as applicable beyond pagans.

**Notes:** The Sodom typology is reapplied to God's own people — those who forsake the covenant become like Sodom. This prepares for Isaiah's use of the same typology (Isa 1:10) and shows that the three-part pattern is not merely about external enemies but about covenant unfaithfulness.

### Isaiah calls Judah's leaders 'rulers of Sodom' — Sodom typology reapplied

> Hear the word of the LORD, ye rulers of Sodom; give ear unto the law of our God, ye people of Gomorrah.

**Context:** Isaiah addresses Judah's leaders as 'rulers of Sodom' — applying the Sodom judgment type to God's own people. This is within the Isaiah 1 covenant lawsuit (rib) passage, combining the Sodom episode with the rib tradition.

**Notes:** The Sodom typology applied to Judah's leaders within a covenant lawsuit context (Isa 1:2-20). The combination of Sodom language with rib structure shows how the three-part pattern operates across multiple OT episodes simultaneously. The command to 'hear the word of the LORD' and 'give ear unto the law' is the gospel/covenant call.

### Jeremiah exposes idols as powerless — systematic exposure of false worship

> Hear ye the word which the LORD speaketh unto you, O house of Israel: Thus saith the LORD, Learn not the way of the heathen, and be not dismayed at the signs of heaven; for the heathen are dismayed at them. For the customs of the people are vain: for one cutteth a tree out of the forest, the work of the hands of the workman, with the axe. They deck it with silver and with gold; they fasten it with nails and with hammers, that it move not. They are upright as the palm tree, but speak not: they must needs be borne, because they cannot go. Be not afraid of them; for they cannot do evil, neither also is it in them to do good.

**Context:** Jeremiah systematically exposes the manufacture and impotence of idols — they are cut from trees, decorated with metal, nailed in place, cannot speak, cannot walk, cannot do good or evil. This parallels Elijah's exposure of Baal at Carmel and Isaiah 44's idol-maker satire.

**Notes:** The exposure is methodical: raw material (tree), fabrication (workman, axe), decoration (silver, gold), physical limitations (cannot speak, cannot walk, cannot act). 'Be not afraid of them' — they are powerless, unlike YHWH. This is the exposure element in Jeremiah's ministry.

### Jeremiah: 'Babylon hath been a golden cup' — direct source for Rev 14:8

> Babylon hath been a golden cup in the LORD'S hand, that made all the earth drunken: the nations have drunken of her wine; therefore the nations are mad.

**Context:** The primary OT source for Rev 14:8's imagery. Babylon as a golden cup intoxicating all nations. The second angel's message ('she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication') draws directly from this verse.

**Notes:** Lexical chain: Jer 51:7 'golden cup... made all the earth drunken... nations have drunken of her wine' → Rev 14:8 'she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication' → Rev 17:4 'golden cup in her hand full of abominations.' The cup-of-wrath tradition (chemah, H2534) links through LXX thumos (G2372) to Rev 14:10.

### Josiah reform: personal seeking of God from youth — gospel-response element

> Josiah was eight years old when he began to reign, and he reigned in Jerusalem one and thirty years. And he did that which was right in the sight of the LORD, and walked in the ways of David his father, and declined neither to the right hand, nor to the left. For in the eighth year of his reign, while he was yet young, he began to seek after the God of David his father: and in the twelfth year he began to purge Judah and Jerusalem from the high places, and the groves, and the carved images, and the molten images. And they brake down the altars of Baalim in his presence; and the images, that were on high above them, he cut down; and the groves, and the carved images, and the molten images, he brake in pieces, and made dust of them, and strowed it upon the graves of them that had sacrificed unto them.

**Context:** The Chronicles account provides the extended timeline: Josiah began seeking God at age 16 (8th year of reign) and began purging idolatry at age 20 (12th year). The gospel-response element: personal seeking of God preceded the public reform.

**Notes:** Chronicles adds detail not in Kings: the Baalim altars were broken, images cut down, carved/molten images smashed to dust and scattered on the graves of those who sacrificed to them. The dust-on-graves detail intensifies the exposure/dismantling element.

### Josiah: expanded account of law discovery and national covenant renewal

> And when they brought out the money that was brought into the house of the LORD, Hilkiah the priest found a book of the law of the LORD given by Moses. And Hilkiah answered and said to Shaphan the scribe, I have found the book of the law in the house of the LORD. And Hilkiah delivered the book to Shaphan. And Shaphan carried the book to the king, and brought the king word back again, saying, All that was committed to thy servants, they do it. And they have gathered together the money that was found in the house of the LORD, and have delivered it into the hand of the overseers, and to the hand of the workmen. Then Shaphan the scribe told the king, saying, Hilkiah the priest hath given me a book. And Shaphan read it before the king. And it came to pass, when the king had heard the words of the law, that he rent his clothes. And the king commanded Hilkiah, and Ahikam the son of Shaphan, and Abdon the son of Micah, and Shaphan the scribe, and Asaiah a servant of the king's, saying, Go, inquire of the LORD for me, and for them that are left in Israel and in Judah, concerning the words of the book that is found: for great is the wrath of the LORD that is poured out upon us, because our fathers have not kept the word of the LORD, to do after all that is written in this book. And Hilkiah, and they that the king had appointed, went to Huldah the prophetess, the wife of Shallum the son of Tikvath, the son of Hasrah, keeper of the wardrobe; (now she dwelt in Jerusalem in the college:) and they spake to her to that effect. And she answered them, Thus saith the LORD God of Israel, Tell ye the man that sent you to me, Thus saith the LORD, Behold, I will bring evil upon this place, and upon the inhabitants thereof, even all the curses that are written in the book which they have read before the king of Judah: Because they have forsaken me, and have burned incense unto other gods, that they might provoke me to anger with all the works of their hands; therefore my wrath shall be poured out upon this place, and shall not be quenched. And as for the king of Judah, who sent you to inquire of the LORD, so shall ye say unto him, Thus saith the LORD God of Israel concerning the words which thou hast heard; Because thine heart was tender, and thou didst humble thyself before God, when thou heardest his words against this place, and against the inhabitants thereof, and humbledst thyself before me, and didst rend thy clothes, and weep before me; I have even heard thee also, saith the LORD. Behold, I will gather thee to thy fathers, and thou shalt be gathered to thy grave in peace, neither shall thine eyes see all the evil that I will bring upon this place, and upon the inhabitants of the same. So they brought the king word again. Then the king sent and gathered together all the elders of Judah and Jerusalem. And the king went up into the house of the LORD, and all the men of Judah, and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, and the priests, and the Levites, and all the people, great and small: and he read in their ears all the words of the book of the covenant that was found in the house of the LORD. And the king stood in his place, and made a covenant before the LORD, to walk after the LORD, and to keep his commandments, and his testimonies, and his statutes, with all his heart, and with all his soul, to perform the words of the covenant which are written in this book. And he caused all that were present in Jerusalem and Benjamin to stand to it. And the inhabitants of Jerusalem did according to the covenant of God, the God of their fathers. And Josiah took away all the abominations out of all the countries that pertained to the children of Israel, and made all that were present in Israel to serve, even to serve the LORD their God. And all his days they departed not from following the LORD, the God of their fathers.

**Context:** The expanded Chronicles account of Josiah's reform includes Huldah's dual prophecy (judgment on the nation, personal exemption for Josiah), the covenant renewal, and the national reform. All three elements of the pattern are present: gospel/covenant call (law discovered), exposure (idolatry purged), judgment warning (Huldah's prophecy).

**Notes:** Chronicles adds that Huldah's prophecy uses 'all the curses that are written in the book' (v.24) — connecting the judgment to specific covenant curse language from Deuteronomy 28. The covenant renewal (v.31) includes 'commandments, testimonies, and statutes' — the full torah vocabulary. Despite the reform, 'my wrath shall be poured out' (v.25) remains irrevocable for the nation.

### Lamentations for Josiah — despite reform, judgment could not be averted

> And Jeremiah lamented for Josiah: and all the singing men and the singing women spake of Josiah in their lamentations to this day, and made them an ordinance in Israel: and, behold, they are written in the lamentations.

**Context:** Jeremiah lamented for Josiah — connecting the Josiah episode to the Jeremiah episode. Josiah's death in battle (despite his righteousness) confirmed that corporate judgment was not averted by individual reform.

**Notes:** The transition from Josiah's reform to Jeremiah's ministry is explicit: Jeremiah lamented for Josiah. After Josiah's death, the nation reverted to apostasy, and the judgment Huldah prophesied was fulfilled through Babylon. The three-part pattern in Josiah's case shows that even the most thoroughgoing reform cannot avert corporate judgment when the people remain uncommitted.

### Sodom's sins enumerated in full — pride, abundance, idleness, neglect of poor

> And thine elder sister is Samaria, she and her daughters that dwell at thy left hand: and thy younger sister, that dwelleth at thy right hand, is Sodom and her daughters. Yet hast thou not walked after their ways, nor done after their abominations: but, as if that were a very little thing, thou wast corrupted more than they in all thy ways. As I live, saith the Lord GOD, Sodom thy sister hath not done, she nor her daughters, as thou hast done, thou and thy daughters. Behold, this was the iniquity of thy sister Sodom, pride, fulness of bread, and abundance of idleness was in her and in her daughters, neither did she strengthen the hand of the poor and needy.

**Context:** Ezekiel provides the fullest enumeration of Sodom's sins: pride, abundance of food, idle complacency, and failure to care for the poor. This broadens the exposure element beyond the sexual sin of Gen 19:4-5. Ezekiel also declares that Jerusalem was worse than Sodom.

**Notes:** The exposure element for Sodom is comprehensive: social injustice (neglect of poor), pride, complacency, and abundance leading to moral decay. Ezekiel's comparison — Jerusalem is worse than Sodom — shows the three-part pattern applying to God's own people, not just external enemies. This is relevant to how the three angels' messages address God's professed people.

### Josiah's systematic destruction of idolatrous apparatus — detailed exposure/purging

> And the king commanded Hilkiah the high priest, and the priests of the second order, and the keepers of the door, to bring forth out of the temple of the LORD all the vessels that were made for Baal, and for the grove, and for all the host of heaven: and he burned them without Jerusalem in the fields of Kidron, and carried the ashes of them unto Bethel. And he put down the idolatrous priests, whom the kings of Judah had ordained to burn incense in the high places in the cities of Judah, and in the places round about Jerusalem; them also that burned incense unto Baal, to the sun, and to the moon, and to the planets, and to all the host of heaven. And he brought out the grove from the house of the LORD, without Jerusalem, unto the brook Kidron, and burned it at the brook Kidron, and stamped it small to powder, and cast the powder thereof upon the graves of the children of the people. And he brake down the houses of the sodomites, that were by the house of the LORD, where the women wove hangings for the grove. And he brought all the priests out of the cities of Judah, and defiled the high places where the priests had burned incense, from Geba to Beersheba, and brake down the high places of the gates that were in the entering in of the gate of Joshua the governor of the city, which were on a man's left hand at the gate of the city. Nevertheless the priests of the high places came not up to the altar of the LORD in Jerusalem, but they did eat of the unleavened bread among their brethren. And he defiled Topheth, which is in the valley of the children of Hinnom, that no man might make his son or his daughter to pass through the fire to Molech. And he took away the horses that the kings of Judah had given to the sun, at the entering in of the house of the LORD, by the chamber of Nathanmelech the chamberlain, which was in the suburbs, and burned the chariots of the sun with fire. And the altars that were on the top of the upper chamber of Ahaz, which the kings of Judah had made, and the altars which Manasseh had made in the two courts of the house of the LORD, did the king beat down, and brake them down from thence, and cast the dust of them into the brook Kidron. And the high places that were before Jerusalem, which were on the right hand of the mount of corruption, which Solomon the king of Israel had builded for Ashtoreth the abomination of the Zidonians, and for Chemosh the abomination of the Moabites, and for Milcom the abomination of the children of Ammon, did the king defile. And he brake in pieces the images, and cut down the groves, and filled their places with the bones of men. Moreover the altar that was at Bethel, and the high place which Jeroboam the son of Nebat, who made Israel to sin, had made, both that altar and the high place he brake down, and burned the high place, and stamped it small to powder, and burned the grove. And as Josiah turned himself, he spied the sepulchres that were there in the mount, and sent, and took the bones out of the sepulchres, and burned them upon the altar, and polluted it, according to the word of the LORD which the man of God proclaimed, who proclaimed these words. Then he said, What title is that that I see? And the men of the city told him, It is the sepulchre of the man of God, which came from Judah, and proclaimed these things that thou hast done against the altar of Bethel. And he said, Let him alone; let no man move his bones. So they let his bones alone, with the bones of the prophet that came out of Samaria. And all the houses also of the high places that were in the cities of Samaria, which the kings of Israel had made to provoke the LORD to anger, Josiah took away, and did to them according to all the acts that he had done in Bethel. And he slew all the priests of the high places that were there upon the altars, and burned men's bones upon them, and returned to Jerusalem.

**Context:** The most detailed account of idolatry destruction in the entire Bible. Josiah's reform systematically dismantles every form of false worship: Baal vessels, Asherah/grove, host-of-heaven worship, idolatrous priests, sodomite houses, Topheth/Molech, sun chariots, rooftop altars, high places from Solomon through Jeroboam. This is the exposure element at its most comprehensive.

**Notes:** The catalogue of false worship destroyed includes: Baal (H1168), Asherah/grove (H842), host of heaven, sun/moon/planets, Molech (child sacrifice), Ashtoreth (Zidonians), Chemosh (Moabites), Milcom (Ammonites). The exposure spans from the temple itself (v.4) to the far reaches of Samaria (v.19). Note that sun worship (v.5, 11) is present in Josiah's reform context, even if it was not the primary issue at Carmel.
