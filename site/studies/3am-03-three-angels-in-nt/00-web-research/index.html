    Web Research Findings¶

    Study Question¶

    Does the New Testament repeat the same three-part pattern (gospel call, expose false system, warn of judgment) before Revelation 14? Examine: John the Baptist's ministry, Jesus' ministry, Stephen's speech in Acts 7, Paul at Athens (Acts 17:22-31), Paul to Rome (Romans 1-3). How do these anticipate and parallel the three angels of Revelation 14:6-12?

    Sources Found¶

    C.H. Dodd -- The Apostolic Preaching and Its Developments (Harper and Row, 1964)¶

    URL: https://postbarthian.com/2012/10/15/the-apostolic-preaching-and-its-developments-by-c-h-dodd/ (review) and https://media.sabda.org/alkitab-2/Religion-Online.org%20Books/Dodd,%20C.%20H.%20-%20The%20Apostolic%20Preaching%20and%20Its%20Developments.pdf (full text) Key claims: - Dodd reconstructs the "kerygma" -- the structured core of primitive Christian preaching -- from Acts 2-5 and 1 Corinthians 15:3-8. He identifies six principal components in Peter's speeches: (1) prophecy fulfillment, (2) historical facts of Jesus' ministry/death/resurrection, (3) exaltation at God's right hand, (4) Holy Spirit as evidence of Christ's power, (5) future consummation, (6) call to repentance. - Judgment is integral to the kerygma, not an isolated element: "Judgment is for Paul a function of the universal lordship of Christ, which He attained through death and resurrection, and His second advent as Judge is a part of the kerygma -- as Judge, but also as Saviour." - Judgment is treated as a "datum of faith" -- a presupposition, not a debated point. Cited passages: Romans 2:16, 1 Corinthians 4:5, 2 Corinthians 5:10, Acts 10:42. - Paul explicitly claims continuity with the broader apostolic kerygma: "Whether I or they, it was thus that we preached, and thus that you believed" (1 Cor. 15:11). - Paul summarizes his preaching at Thessalonica: "You turned from idols to God, to serve the living and real God, and to await His Son from heaven, whom He raised from the dead -- Jesus, who saves us from the coming Retribution" (1 Thess 1:9-10). This contains: (a) gospel call (turn to God), (b) exposure (turn FROM idols), (c) judgment warning (coming Retribution). - The kerygma was evangelistic proclamation to non-believers, distinct from teaching (didache) addressed to established communities. Relevance: Dodd's kerygma analysis is highly relevant. The six-element pattern he identifies can be grouped into three macro-movements that parallel the three angels: gospel proclamation, confrontation with error, and eschatological judgment. His observation that this pattern is consistent across Peter, Paul, and other speakers supports the study's thesis of a recurring NT pattern. His 1 Thessalonians 1:9-10 summary is particularly striking as a compressed gospel-exposure-judgment sequence. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Mikeal C. Parsons (Baylor University) -- Commentary on Acts 17:22-31 (Working Preacher, Luther Seminary)¶

    URL: https://www.workingpreacher.org/commentaries/revised-common-lectionary/sixth-sunday-of-easter/commentary-on-acts-1722-31-5 Key claims: - Identifies a chiastic structure in Paul's Areopagus sermon: A (ignorance of pagan worship, vv.22-23) / B (Creator God as true object of worship, vv.24-25) / C (humanity-God relationship, vv.26-28) / B' (idols as false worship objects, v.29) / A' (ignorance ends, judgment arrives, vv.30-31). - The judgment announcement (17:30-31) functions as the sermon's "rhetorical turning point" -- God moves from "overlooking" ignorance to demanding repentance, with resurrection as divine proof. - Paul's argument is "thoroughly grounded in biblical Judaism" through allusions to Genesis, Isaiah, and Deuteronomy, despite lacking explicit Scripture citations. - Paul's critique of Athenian worship: they worship "a 'what,' not a 'whom'" -- exposing the fundamental error of idolatry. Relevance: The chiastic structure Parsons identifies maps onto the three-part pattern: the center (B/C/B') exposes the contrast between true and false worship, while the outer frame (A/A') moves from ignorance to judgment. The "rhetorical turning point" at judgment aligns with the third angel's warning function. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Stanley Toussaint / Warren Wiersbe / Derek Thomas -- Acts 7 Commentary (Precept Austin compilation)¶

    URL: https://www.preceptaustin.org/acts-7-commentary Key claims: - Toussaint identifies "three ideas run like cords through its fabric": (1) God's program shows progress and change; (2) God's blessings transcend geographical limits; (3) Israel consistently opposed God's plans and messengers. - Wiersbe outlines the speech in three sections: vv.2-8 (Patriarchs -- spiritual roots), vv.9-43 (Moses and Law), vv.44-50 (Temple). - Stephen "turns the tables" -- transforms from defendant into prosecutor, paralleling OT prophetic confrontation patterns where God's messenger indicts the people for rejecting His word. - The climactic indictment (7:51-53) employs classic prophetic language: "stiff-necked" and "resisting the Holy Spirit" echo Deuteronomic covenant language about Israel's rebellious disposition. - Derek Thomas observes Stephen demonstrates the tension between "the church's roots in the temple and its emerging independence from it." Relevance: The three-cord analysis directly supports the study question's framework. Stephen's speech recounts God's faithfulness (gospel history), exposes Israel's pattern of rejecting God's messengers (second angel parallel -- exposing the false system), and delivers judgment warning (7:51-53). The "defendant becomes prosecutor" motif is significant: the three angels' messages similarly turn from proclamation to indictment. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Zane Hodges -- "Stephen's Indictment of Israel" (Grace Evangelical Society)¶

    URL: https://faithalone.org/grace-in-focus-articles/stephens-indictment-of-israel-acts-71-29/ Key claims: - The unifying theme of Stephen's sermon is "Israel's resistance to the Holy Spirit throughout its history" (cf. Acts 7:51-53, especially v.51). - Behind this theme is a typological lesson: "the OT prefigures the rejection of Christ." - Stephen was conscious that Israel's history foreshadowed her treatment of Christ, "especially in reference to Joseph and Moses." - Greek note: "serve" (latreusousin) in v.7 is "an interpretation, not a quotation" from Genesis 15. Relevance: The typological framework is relevant -- the rejection pattern in Israel's history is not merely historical but prophetic. This supports the idea that the three-part pattern (faithfulness/exposure/judgment) recurs because it reflects a divine communication pattern, not just rhetorical convention. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    John N. Andrews -- The Three Messages of Revelation 14:6-12 (Review and Herald, 1892)¶

    URL: https://cdn.centrowhite.org.br/home/uploads/2022/12/The-Three-Messages-of-Revelation-14_6-12.pdf Key claims: - The three messages have a fourfold design: (1) give warning of coming judgments, (2) set God's people upon their watch-tower, (3) gather the scattered saints into one body, (4) restore the commandments of God and prepare for translation. - Andrews explicitly connects Paul's judgment preaching to the three angels: Paul preached before Felix "of the Judgment to come" and before the Athenians that "God hath appointed a day in the which he will judge the world" (Acts 24:25; 17:30-31). However, Andrews notes that "nowhere" in Acts does the text suggest "Christ was immediately coming to judgment" -- the apostolic preaching anticipated judgment but did not announce its arrival. - The apostolic commission extended "only to the harvest, which is the end of the world" (Matt 28:19-20; 24:14). Andrews cites Paul's warning in 2 Thessalonians 2:3 that the coming of Christ to judgment could not take place until the great apostasy and "the man of sin" were revealed. - Daniel's prophecies regarding the "time of the end" were "closed up and sealed" until that period (Dan 8:17, 26; 12:4, 9), so the specific judgment-hour proclamation of the first angel was reserved for the final generation. Relevance: Andrews draws the direct line from apostolic judgment preaching to the three angels but argues for a crucial distinction: the NT preachers anticipated judgment without announcing its arrival, while the first angel announces "the hour of his judgment IS come." This distinction (anticipation vs. announcement) is important for the study -- the NT instances are precursors/patterns, not the fulfillment itself. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Matthew 23 Structural Analysis (Wikipedia scholarly compilation)¶

    URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_23 Key claims: - Matthew 23 divides into three major sections: (1) Warning against scribes and Pharisees (vv.1-12), (2) Seven woes/denunciations (vv.13-36), (3) Jerusalem's fate and lament (vv.37-39). - The chapter features a "remarkably Jewish character" with no explanatory glosses, suggesting the author assumed audience familiarity with the practices described. - Scholars debate whether Matthew 23 constitutes part of the "fifth and final discourse" -- some treat chapters 23-25 together, linking the exposure of religious corruption directly to the judgment prophecy of the Olivet Discourse. - The conclusion (vv.37-39) moves to eschatological judgment, with Jesus' departure from the city "anticipating both that he will return, and that calamities will befall it." Relevance: The three-section structure of Matthew 23 (warning / woes / judgment-lament) maps directly onto the three-angel pattern. The scholarly debate about whether Matt 23-25 should be read as a unit is significant: if so, the entire sequence from kingdom proclamation (Sermon on the Mount) through exposure (woes) to judgment prophecy (Olivet Discourse) forms a macro-level three-part pattern across Matthew's Gospel. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Gospel Preaching Patterns in Acts (Academic thesis, Love in Truth)¶

    URL: https://loveintruth.com/amf-docs/gpia-conclusions.htm Key claims: - Identifies four consistent elements across apostolic sermons: (1) proclamation of core facts (Christ's suffering, death, resurrection), (2) exposure of error/sin -- preachers "accurately uncovered the source of their hostility to God and his Son," (3) call to response demanding faith and repentance, (4) eschatological warning referencing coming judgment (Acts 10:42; 24:25). - Peter, Stephen, and Paul followed comparable approaches despite addressing different audiences (Jews, Gentiles, civic leaders). All "unfailingly identify the main source of his hearers' opposition to Christ and then fearlessly address it." - Content remained fixed while delivery adapted to audiences. Reasoning and persuasion combined declaration with dialogue (Acts 19:8). Relevance: This four-element pattern can be grouped into the three-part framework of the study: gospel proclamation (element 1 + 3), exposure of error (element 2), judgment warning (element 4). The observation that all speakers consistently expose the specific source of opposition -- despite audience differences -- parallels how the second angel's message exposes the specific false system (Babylon). Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Douglas Campbell -- Romans 1-3 Rhetorical Analysis (BibleBridge)¶

    URL: https://www.bible-bridge.com/ancient-rhetoric-romans-1-3/ Key claims: - Campbell proposes that Romans 1:18-3:20 uses prosopopoeia (speech-in-character/parody) -- Paul presents "the view of his opponents taken to their logical and absurd conclusions" rather than his own position. - The traditional reading identifies a progressive structure: gospel power declaration (1:16-17) followed by exposure of Gentile idolatry (1:18-32), then Jewish hypocrisy (2:1-3:8), culminating in universal judgment (3:9-20). - Romans 1:16-17 functions as "the outline or roadmap for everything Paul discusses in 1:18-5:11." - The argument moves from gospel (1:16-17) to Gentile corruption exposure (1:18-32) to Jewish corruption exposure (2:1-29) to universal condemnation (3:9-20) to justification by faith (3:21-26). Relevance: Whether one follows the traditional reading or Campbell's rhetorical proposal, the three-part structure is present in the text: Paul declares the gospel's power, exposes both Gentile and Jewish corruption, and announces God's judgment. The traditional reading aligns more naturally with the three-angel parallel. Status: Lead to verify against Scripture

    Summary of Scholarly Landscape¶

    The scholarly literature confirms that a recurring preaching pattern exists across the New Testament, though scholars do not typically frame it in terms of the three angels' messages of Revelation 14. C.H. Dodd's foundational work on the kerygma establishes that apostolic preaching followed a consistent structure across different speakers and contexts, with judgment as an integral element rather than an afterthought. This has been widely accepted in NT scholarship since 1935.

    The specific three-part pattern proposed by the study question (gospel call / expose false system / warn of judgment) finds support in structural analyses of individual passages. Mikeal Parsons identifies a chiastic structure in Acts 17 that moves from ignorance through true-vs-false worship to judgment. Stanley Toussaint identifies three thematic cords in Acts 7, including Israel's consistent opposition to God's messengers -- an exposure motif. Matthew 23's three-section structure (warning / woes / lament-judgment) is widely recognized. Paul's argument in Romans 1-3 follows a gospel-exposure-judgment progression that scholars analyze under various rhetorical frameworks.

    The most direct connection to Revelation 14 comes from John N. Andrews (1892), who explicitly links apostolic judgment preaching to the three angels but draws an important distinction: the NT preachers anticipated judgment without announcing its arrival, while the first angel announces "the hour of his judgment IS come." This distinction -- pattern vs. fulfillment -- may be the key interpretive framework for the study. The NT instances established and repeated the gospel-exposure-judgment pattern; Revelation 14 represents its final, climactic iteration. No modern academic sources were found that specifically analyze the NT preaching pattern as anticipating the three angels' messages structure, making this study's contribution potentially original in connecting these threads.

    Research Directives for Downstream Agents¶

    1. Dodd's kerygma elements -- Verify whether Acts 2:14-36, Acts 3:12-26, Acts 10:34-43, and 1 Corinthians 15:3-8 all contain the three-part pattern (gospel/exposure/judgment) within the broader six-element kerygma structure.
    2. 1 Thessalonians 1:9-10 as compressed pattern -- Dodd identifies this as a summary of Paul's preaching at Thessalonica. Verify whether the three elements (turned to God / turned FROM idols / await coming Retribution) map to the three angels (everlasting gospel / Babylon exposed / judgment warning).
    3. Acts 17:30-31 judgment climax -- Verify Parsons' claim that the judgment announcement functions as the "rhetorical turning point" of Paul's Athens sermon. Check the Greek of "overlooked" (hupereidon) and "appointed a day" (estesen hemeran) for eschatological language paralleling Revelation 14.
    4. Acts 7:51-53 as prophetic indictment -- Verify Toussaint's three-cord analysis against the text. Check whether "stiff-necked" (sklerotracheloi) and "resist the Holy Spirit" (antipiptete to pneumati to hagio) echo Deuteronomic language and whether this indictment pattern parallels the second angel's exposure of Babylon.
    5. Matthew 23-25 as unified sequence -- Investigate whether the three-chapter unit (woes / Olivet Discourse judgment prophecy / parables of final judgment) forms a macro three-part pattern: exposure (ch.23) -> judgment signs (ch.24) -> judgment execution (ch.25).
    6. John the Baptist's three-part ministry -- Verify in Matthew 3:1-12 and Luke 3:1-18 whether John's preaching follows: gospel call ("repent, the kingdom of heaven is at hand") -> exposure ("generation of vipers," denouncing Herod in Luke 3:19-20) -> judgment warning ("axe laid to the root," "unquenchable fire").
    7. Romans 1-3 progressive structure -- Verify the traditional reading of Romans 1:16-3:20 as: gospel declaration (1:16-17) -> Gentile corruption exposure (1:18-32) -> Jewish corruption exposure (2:1-29) -> universal judgment (3:9-20). Check whether 2:16 ("according to my gospel") explicitly ties judgment to gospel.
    8. Andrews' anticipation vs. fulfillment distinction -- Verify Andrews' claim that NT speakers preached judgment as future/anticipated while the first angel announces it as arrived ("the hour of his judgment IS come"). Check the Greek tenses in Acts 17:31, Acts 24:25, and Revelation 14:7 for temporal differences.
    9. Pattern consistency across audiences -- The Love in Truth analysis claims all NT speakers "unfailingly identify the main source of hearers' opposition." Verify whether the exposure element adapts to audience (Pharisees for John/Jesus, Israel's history for Stephen, idolatry for Athens, Gentile/Jewish sin for Romans) while maintaining the same structural position in the pattern.
    10. Revelation 14:6-12 internal structure -- Verify that the three angels themselves follow the pattern: first angel = gospel call ("everlasting gospel," "fear God and give glory"), second angel = exposure of false system ("Babylon is fallen"), third angel = judgment warning ("wrath of God," "tormented with fire and brimstone"). Map each angel to its NT precursor instances.
