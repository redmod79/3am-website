# Verses

## Question
How do the four elements of the first angel's message (euangelion aionion + phobethete + dote doxan + hoti elthen he hora tes kriseos + proskynesate to poiesanti) function as one integrated gospel proclamation? How does Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 demonstrate this is the same message? How does Paul at Athens (Acts 17:22-31) serve as apostolic precedent — element by element? How does Psalm 96 contain every element in OT worship form? Capstone synthesizing studies 04-08.

### The first angel's message in full — the text being synthesized

> And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

**Context:** Revelation 14:1-5 introduces the Lamb and 144,000 on Mount Zion singing a new song. Verses 6-7 are the first of three angelic proclamations positioned between the secured victory of 14:1-5 and the dual harvest of 14:14-20. The angel flies in midheaven with universal scope.

**Notes:** Three aorist imperatives (phobethete, dote, proskynesate) form a climactic triad: internal orientation (fear), relational acknowledgment (give glory), behavioral expression (worship). The legon participle structurally embeds v.7 as the CONTENT of the euangelion in v.6. The hoti clause grounds all three imperatives. The creation formula uses poieo (not ktizo), mapping to Exo 20:11 LXX. The anarthrous euangelion aionion is qualitative. The cognate accusative euangelion euangelisai intensifies the gospel-ness.

### Commandments + faith of Jesus — gospel result completing the inclusio

> Here is the patience of the saints: here [are] they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus.

**Context:** Follows the third angel's warning (14:9-11). Rev 14:12 is the conclusion of the three angels' messages, describing the character of those who respond rightly. Forms an inclusio with Rev 12:17.

**Notes:** The articular present participle hoi terountes (V-PAP-NPM) is substantival — it describes who the saints ARE, not what they must DO to be saved. Descriptive, not prescriptive. The pistin Iesou genitive can be objective (faith IN Jesus) or subjective (faithfulness OF Jesus). The commandment inclusio: Rev 12:17 (entolas tou theou) and Rev 14:12 (entolas tou theou) bracket the three angels' messages.

### Opening bracket of the commandment inclusio

> And the dragon was wroth with the woman, and went to make war with the remnant of her seed, which keep the commandments of God, and have the testimony of Jesus Christ.

**Context:** Concludes Revelation 12, the great cosmic conflict chapter. The dragon targets those who keep God's commandments and have the testimony of Jesus — the same two markers that reappear in 14:12.

**Notes:** Establishes the commandment-keeping + testimony/faith of Jesus profile that brackets the three angels' messages. The war against commandment-keepers in 12:17 is answered by the gospel call of 14:6-7 and the character description of 14:12.

### OT structural twin — fear God + commandments + judgment = 'the whole of man'

> Let us hear the conclusion of the whole matter: Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this [is] the whole [duty] of man. For God shall bring every work into judgment, with every secret thing, whether [it be] good, or whether [it be] evil.

**Context:** The conclusion of the book of Ecclesiastes. After Solomon's exploration of meaning and vanity, this is his final verdict on the purpose of human existence. The Hebrew kol-ha-adam has three interpretive options: 'all mankind,' 'whole duty of man,' or 'man's all' (entire purpose/being).

**Notes:** Element mapping to Rev 14:7,12: (1) 'Fear God' (yare et ha-elohim) = 'Fear God' (phobethete ton theon), (2) 'keep his commandments' (mitsvotav shamar) = 'commandments of God' (entolas tou theou, Rev 14:12), (3) 'God shall bring every work into judgment' (mishpat) = 'the hour of his judgment is come' (kriseos). LXX bridges verified: yare maps to phobeo (241 co-occurrences), mitsvah maps to entole (153 co-occurrences), mishpat maps to krisis. The kol-ha-adam being 'man's all' means Eccl 12:13 defines the totality of human existence — paralleling the 'everlasting gospel' as the comprehensive message for 'every nation.' Fifth iteration of the farewell-address pattern (Moses, Joshua, Samuel, Solomon, first angel).

### Apostolic precedent — Paul's Athens sermon mapping element by element to Rev 14:7

> Then Paul stood in the midst of Mars' hill, and said, [Ye] men of Athens, I perceive that in all things ye are too superstitious. For as I passed by, and beheld your devotions, I found an altar with this inscription, TO THE UNKNOWN GOD. Whom therefore ye ignorantly worship, him declare I unto you. God that made the world and all things therein, seeing that he is Lord of heaven and earth, dwelleth not in temples made with hands; Neither is worshipped with men's hands, as though he needed any thing, seeing he giveth to all life, and breath, and all things; And hath made of one blood all nations of men for to dwell on all the face of the earth, and hath determined the times before appointed, and the bounds of their habitation; That they should seek the Lord, if haply they might feel after him, and find him, though he be not far from every one of us: For in him we live, and move, and have our being; as certain also of your own poets have said, For we are also his offspring. Forasmuch then as we are the offspring of God, we ought not to think that the Godhead is like unto gold, or silver, or stone, graven by art and man's device. And the times of this ignorance God winked at; but now commandeth all men every where to repent: Because he hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world in righteousness by [that] man whom he hath ordained; [whereof] he hath given assurance unto all [men], in that he hath raised him from the dead.

**Context:** Paul at the Areopagus, addressing Athenian philosophers. This is his gospel for pagans — people without OT background. Parsons identifies a chiastic structure: A (ignorant worship, 22-23) / B (true worship of Creator, 24-25) / C (humanity-God relationship, 26-28) / B' (critique of idol worship, 29) / A' (end of ignorance via judgment, 30-31).

**Notes:** Element-by-element mapping to Rev 14:7: (a) 'God who made (poiesas, G4160) the world' (17:24) = 'worship him that made (poiesanti, G4160)' (14:7) — SAME VERB poieo in both; (b) 'commands all people everywhere to repent (metanoein)' (17:30) = 'fear God and give him glory' (14:7) — repentance and fear as moral reorientation; (c) 'appointed a day (hemeran) to judge (krinein) the world' (17:31) = 'the hour (hora) of his judgment (kriseos) is come' (14:7); (d) 'does not dwell in temples made with hands (cheiropoietois)' (17:24) = worship correction away from false worship toward the Creator. Temporal shift: Acts 17:31 uses mellei (about to), Rev 14:7 uses elthen (IS come) — same message, different temporal phase. Carson: this framework is 'the necessary framework without which his proclamation of Jesus makes no sense.' Paul delays mentioning Christ until v.31, just as Rev 14:7 delays explicit christological naming until 14:12.

### OT worship form containing every element of Rev 14:6-7

> O sing unto the LORD a new song: sing unto the LORD, all the earth. Sing unto the LORD, bless his name; shew forth his salvation from day to day. Declare his glory among the heathen, his wonders among all people. For the LORD [is] great, and greatly to be praised: he [is] to be feared above all gods. For all the gods of the nations [are] idols: but the LORD made the heavens. Honour and majesty [are] before him: strength and beauty [are] in his sanctuary. Give unto the LORD, O ye kindreds of the people, give unto the LORD glory and strength. Give unto the LORD the glory [due unto] his name: bring an offering, and come into his courts. O worship the LORD in the beauty of holiness: fear before him, all the earth. Say among the heathen [that] the LORD reigneth: the world also shall be established that it shall not be moved: he shall judge the people righteously. Let the heavens rejoice, and let the earth be glad; let the sea roar, and the fulness thereof. Let the field be joyful, and all that [is] therein: then shall all the trees of the wood rejoice Before the LORD: for he cometh, for he cometh to judge the earth: he shall judge the world with righteousness, and the people with his truth.

**Context:** An enthronement psalm celebrating YHWH's kingship over all nations. One of the 'new song' psalms. Contains every element of the first angel's message in OT worship form.

**Notes:** Element mapping: (1) 'Sing a new song' (v.1, shiyr chadash) connects to Rev 14:3's new song via LXX ode kaine; (2) 'shew forth his salvation' (v.2, basseru yeshuato — Piel of basar, 'proclaim good news of his salvation') = 'everlasting gospel' — basar is the Hebrew root behind euangelizo; (3) 'Declare his glory among the heathen' (v.3) = 'every nation and kindred' (Rev 14:6) — universal scope; (4) 'the LORD made (asah) the heavens' (v.5) = 'worship him that made (poieo = asah in LXX)' (Rev 14:7); (5) 'Give unto the LORD glory (kabod)' (vv.7-8) = 'give him glory (doxa)' (Rev 14:7); (6) 'worship the LORD' (v.9) = 'worship him that made' (Rev 14:7); (7) 'he cometh to judge (shaphat) the earth' (v.13) = 'the hour of his judgment is come' (Rev 14:7). Critical: v.5 in the Hebrew reads kol-elohe ha-ammim elilim va-YHWH shamayim asah — 'all the gods of the nations are worthless things, but the LORD made the heavens.' This is the creation-vs-idolatry antithesis that both Acts 17 and Rev 14 present.

### Moses' farewell — fear + commandments + obedience pattern

> And now, Israel, what doth the LORD thy God require of thee, but to fear the LORD thy God, to walk in all his ways, and to love him, and to serve the LORD thy God with all thy heart and with all thy soul, To keep the commandments of the LORD, and his statutes, which I command thee this day for thy good?

**Context:** Moses' farewell address to Israel before they enter the Promised Land. The first iteration of the farewell-address fear-commandments pattern.

**Notes:** Pattern: fear (yirah, H3372 Qal InfCon) + walk + love + serve (avod, H5647) + keep commandments (lishmor mitsvot, H8104 + H4687). First iteration of the pattern that will recur in Joshua, Samuel, Solomon, and the first angel.

### Joshua's farewell — fear + serve + remove idols

> Now therefore fear the LORD, and serve him in sincerity and in truth: and put away the gods which your fathers served on the other side of the flood, and in Egypt; and serve ye the LORD.

**Context:** Joshua's farewell address at Shechem. Second iteration of the farewell-address pattern, adding the idol-removal element.

**Notes:** Pattern: fear (yeru, H3372 Qal Impv) + serve (ivdu, H5647 Qal Impv) in sincerity/truth + put away false gods (hasiru, H5493 Hiphil Impv). Adds the idol-removal dimension that appears in both Acts 17:29 and the creation-vs-idolatry contrast of Psa 96:5.

### Samuel's farewell — fear + serve + remember God's acts

> Only fear the LORD, and serve him in truth with all your heart: for consider how great [things] he hath done for you.

**Context:** Samuel's farewell address after the appointment of Saul as king. Third iteration of the farewell-address pattern.

**Notes:** Pattern: fear (yeru, H3372 Qal Impv) + serve (avadtem, H5647) in truth + consider/remember (reu, H7200 Qal Impv) God's great acts. Samuel adds the 'remember what God has done' element — connecting to creation memory in Rev 14:7.

### Paul labels judgment as 'according to my gospel'

> In the day when God shall judge the secrets of men by Jesus Christ according to my gospel.

**Context:** Romans 2:1-16 discusses God's righteous judgment. Paul identifies judgment day as part of his gospel proclamation — kata to euangelion mou ('according to MY gospel').

**Notes:** One of three independent NT texts labeling judgment as gospel. The articular to euangelion mou ('MY gospel') is Paul's own designation — he considers judgment content to be part of the gospel he preaches. The judgment is 'through Jesus Christ' (dia Christou Iesou), making it explicitly christological.

### John's judgment preaching explicitly labeled euangelizo

> Whose fan [is] in his hand, and he will throughly purge his floor, and will gather the wheat into his garner; but the chaff he will burn with fire unquenchable. And many other things in his exhortation preached he unto the people.

**Context:** Luke 3:7-17 records John the Baptist's judgment preaching — fire, axe at root, winnowing fork. Then v.18 labels this preaching 'euangelizeto' — 'he preached the gospel.'

**Notes:** Second independent text labeling judgment as gospel. Luke uses euangelizeto (imperfect middle of euangelizo) to label the entirety of John's preaching — including the judgment fire of v.17 — as gospel. If judgment preaching IS gospel when John does it, the same content in Rev 14:7 cannot be non-gospel.

### Christ as Creator — christological content implicit in 'worship him that made'

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. The same was in the beginning with God. All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made.

**Context:** The prologue of John's Gospel identifying the Word (Logos) as the agent of creation. 'All things were made by him' establishes Christ as Creator.

**Notes:** When Rev 14:7 says 'worship him that made,' the 'him that made' includes Christ. The 'natural theology' objection ('no Christ, therefore no gospel') fails because Christ IS the Creator being invoked. The absence of explicit christological naming in Rev 14:7 is not the absence of Christ — it is the invocation of Christ under his Creator title.

### Christ as Creator — 'by him were all things created'

> Who is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature: For by him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible, whether [they be] thrones, or dominions, or principalities, or powers: all things were created by him, and for him: And he is before all things, and by him all things consist.

**Context:** The Colossian Christ-hymn establishing Christ's role in creation. Paul uses ktizo (created) here, but the theological point applies to Rev 14:7's poieo as well.

**Notes:** Confirms Christ as Creator. 'By him were all things created' and 'by him all things consist.' Rev 14:7's 'worship him that made' implicitly invokes this christological reality.

### Fourth commandment — the creation formula's primary allusion target

> Remember the sabbath day, to keep it holy. Six days shalt thou labour, and do all thy work: But the seventh day [is] the sabbath of the LORD thy God: [in it] thou shalt not do any work, thou, nor thy son, nor thy daughter, thy manservant, nor thy maidservant, nor thy cattle, nor thy stranger that [is] within thy gates: For [in] six days the LORD made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that in them [is], and rested the seventh day: wherefore the LORD blessed the sabbath day, and hallowed it.

**Context:** The fourth of the Ten Commandments. Exo 20:11 provides the creation formula that Rev 14:7 alludes to: 'made heaven and earth, the sea.'

**Notes:** Four terms match Rev 14:7 in the same sequence: made (asah/poieo), heaven (shamayim/ouranon), earth (erets/gen), sea (yam/thalassan). Rev 14:7 diverges with 'fountains of waters' instead of 'all that in them is.' The verb asah (H6213) maps to poieo (G4160) in the LXX — the same verb Rev 14:7 uses instead of ktizo. Paulien: 'There is no direct allusion to the Old Testament in Revelation that is more certain than the allusion to the fourth commandment in Rev 14:7.' The Sabbath is the only commandment containing the three-element seal: name (YHWH), title (Creator), territory (heaven, earth, sea).

### Heavenly worship of Creator using ktizo — contrast with Rev 14:7's poieo

> Thou art worthy, O Lord, to receive glory and honour and power: for thou hast created all things, and for thy pleasure they are and were created.

**Context:** The throne-room worship scene in Revelation 4. The twenty-four elders worship the Creator using ktizo (ektisas, ektisthesan).

**Notes:** Uses ektisas (G2936, ktizo) — NOT poieo. Revelation uses ktizo for generic creation praise in 4:11 and 10:6. Only 14:7 uses poieo — deliberately chosen to alluse to Exo 20:11 LXX.

### Angel's oath by Creator using ktizo — Rev 14:7 deliberately shifts to poieo

> And sware by him that liveth for ever and ever, who created heaven, and the things that therein are, and the earth, and the things that therein are, and the sea, and the things which are therein, that there should be time no longer:

**Context:** The mighty angel's oath formula. Lists the same creation domains (heaven, earth, sea) but uses ktizo (ektisen) and includes 'the things therein.'

**Notes:** Uses ektisen (G2936, ktizo). The creation domain list is nearly identical to Rev 14:7 (heaven, earth, sea) but uses the generic creation verb. Rev 14:7's switch to poieo is deliberate — it invokes the specific vocabulary of Exo 20:11 LXX.

### The glory-exchange failure — exact antiparallel to Rev 14:7

> For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who hold the truth in unrighteousness; Because that which may be known of God is manifest in them; for God hath shewed [it] unto them. For the invisible things of him from the creation of the world are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made, [even] his eternal power and Godhead; so that they are without excuse: Because that, when they knew God, they glorified [him] not as God, neither were thankful; but became vain in their imaginations, and their foolish heart was darkened. Professing themselves to be wise, they became fools, And changed the glory of the uncorruptible God into an image made like to corruptible man, and to birds, and fourfooted beasts, and creeping things.

**Context:** Romans 1:18-23 describes humanity's failure to glorify God despite knowing Him through creation. This is the precise antiparallel to the first angel's command.

**Notes:** Rom 1:21 — edoxasan (G1392, aorist active ind) — 'they glorified [him] not as God.' The first angel's 'give him glory' (dote auto doxan) is the exact reversal. The glory-exchange of Rom 1:23 (using eikon, G1504 — the same root word as Rev 13's 'image of the beast') shows the first angel reversing the primal human failure: failure to glorify leads to idolatry, which the first angel corrects by calling people to worship the Creator.

### Abraham's faith IS glory-giving — same construction as Rev 14:7

> He staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief; but was strong in faith, giving glory to God;

**Context:** Paul's exposition of Abraham's faith in Romans 4. Abraham's faith is expressed as glory-giving.

**Notes:** dous doxan to Theo (giving glory to God) — aorist active participle of didomi (G1325) + doxa (G1391) + dative. Same construction as dote auto doxan in Rev 14:7 (didomi + doxa + dative). Abraham's faith IS his glory-giving — not works-righteousness but faith expressed as recognition of God's reality and power.

### Verbal echo of Rev 14:7 — same three lexemes, but refusal

> And men were scorched with great heat, and blasphemed the name of God, which hath power over these plagues: and they repented not to give him glory.

**Context:** The fourth bowl plague. Those under judgment refuse to repent and give God glory — the exact opposite of what the first angel commanded.

**Notes:** Same three Greek lexemes as Rev 14:7: didomi + autos + doxa. In 14:7 it is a command (dote auto doxan, 'give him glory'). In 16:9 it is a refusal (ou metenoesan dounai auto doxan, 'they repented not to give him glory'). The gospel call rejected becomes the standard of judgment.

### 'Give glory to God' as confession/truth-telling before judgment

> And Joshua said unto Achan, My son, give, I pray thee, glory to the LORD God of Israel, and make confession unto him; and tell me now what thou hast done; hide [it] not from me.

**Context:** After Achan's theft of devoted things at Jericho. Joshua calls Achan to 'give glory to the LORD' — meaning confession and truth-telling before God's judgment seat.

**Notes:** The Hebraic expression 'give glory to God' means 'tell the truth before God's judgment seat.' This connects the glory imperative (Rev 14:7) directly to the judgment announcement — fear God (internal orientation), give glory (confess truth/acknowledge God's character), because judgment is coming.

### Throne-room judgment scene — the event the first angel announces

> I beheld till the thrones were cast down, and the Ancient of days did sit, whose garment [was] white as snow, and the hair of his head like the pure wool: his throne [was like] the fiery flame, [and] his wheels [as] burning fire. A fiery stream issued and came forth from before him: thousand thousands ministered unto him, and ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him: the judgment was set, and the books were opened.

**Context:** Daniel's throne-room vision of the pre-advent judgment. This is the event the first angel announces has come.

**Notes:** The Aramaic dina (judgment) connects via LXX kriterion/krima to NT krisis. The books being opened establishes the investigative/evaluative nature of this judgment — krisis (process), not katakrima (condemnation).

---

### Judgment given FOR the saints — benefactive le preposition

> Until the Ancient of days came, and judgment was given to the saints of the most High; and the time came that the saints possessed the kingdom.

**Context:** The outcome of the throne-room judgment: it results in the saints receiving the kingdom. Judgment is FOR, not AGAINST, God's people.

**Notes:** Aramaic: dina yehib leqaddishe elyonin — 'judgment was given FOR the saints.' The preposition le is benefactive. The judgment reverses persecution, not creates it. This is why judgment IS gospel — it vindicates the faithful.

### Compressed gospel summary containing all first angel elements

> For they themselves shew of us what manner of entering in we had unto you, and how ye turned to God from idols to serve the living and true God; And to wait for his Son from heaven, whom he raised from the dead, [even] Jesus, which delivered us from the wrath to come.

**Context:** Paul's summary of the Thessalonians' conversion. The most compressed parallel to the first angel's message in one sentence.

**Notes:** Element mapping: (1) 'turned from idols' = worship correction (Rev 14:7 worship the Creator, not the beast); (2) 'serve the living and true God' = fear God and give glory (Rev 14:7); (3) 'wait for his Son from heaven' = christological content; (4) 'delivered from the wrath to come' = judgment as gospel assurance. All four elements of the first angel's message compressed into one sentence.

### First table of Decalogue — salvation, judgment, and creation themes

> I [am] the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage. Thou shalt have no other gods before me. Thou shalt not make unto thee any graven image, or any likeness [of any thing] that [is] in heaven above, or that [is] in the earth beneath, or that [is] in the water under the earth: Thou shalt not bow down thyself to them, nor serve them: for I the LORD thy God [am] a jealous God, visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children unto the third and fourth [generation] of them that hate me; And shewing mercy unto thousands of them that love me, and keep my commandments.

**Context:** The preamble and first two commandments of the Decalogue. Paulien/Baldwin identify the first angel's message as echoing the First Table: salvation (v.2), judgment (vv.5-6), creation (v.11).

**Notes:** Rev 14:6-7 echoes the First Table: (1) euangelion aionion (salvation, cf. Exo 20:2 'brought thee out'); (2) 'the hour of his judgment' (cf. Exo 20:5-6 visiting iniquity/showing mercy); (3) 'him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea' (cf. Exo 20:11). Paulien: 'a triple collection of thematic references' virtually confirming the fourth commandment allusion.

### Fear God not man — the fear that grounds resistance to the beast

> And fear not them which kill the body, but are not able to kill the soul: but rather fear him which is able to destroy both soul and body in hell.

**Context:** Jesus instructs disciples on persecution. The one who fears God cannot be moved by human threats.

**Notes:** The person who has settled whom they ultimately fear (phobeo, same verb as Rev 14:7) cannot be moved by the beast's death threat (Rev 13:15). Fear of God is the foundation for resistance to all counterfeit worship.

### Paul condemns substitution for grace, NOT inclusion of judgment/creation in gospel

> I marvel that ye are so soon removed from him that called you into the grace of Christ unto another gospel: Which is not another; but there be some that trouble you, and would pervert the gospel of Christ. But though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed. As we said before, so say I now again, If any [man] preach any other gospel unto you than that ye have received, let him be accursed.

**Context:** Galatians 1 opening. Paul condemns the Judaizers who added circumcision as a salvation condition.

**Notes:** Paul uses heteron (G2087, 'another of different kind') in v.6 and allo (G243, 'another of same kind') in v.7 — 'a different gospel which is not really another.' Paul condemns adding salvation CONDITIONS (circumcision), not adding gospel CONTENT (judgment, creation, commandments) that he himself preached (Rom 2:16, Acts 17:24, 1 Cor 7:19). The Answering Adventism critique misapplies this passage.

### Christ's model of glory-giving — completing assigned work

> I have glorified thee on the earth: I have finished the work which thou gavest me to do.

**Context:** Jesus' high priestly prayer. He defines his own glory-giving as faithfully completing his assigned work.

**Notes:** Christ's model makes 'give him glory' concrete: glory-giving = faithfully completing assigned work. This is the pattern the saints in Rev 14:12 follow — keeping the commandments and maintaining the faith of Jesus under eschatological pressure.

---

### Sabbatismos — Sabbath-keeping as creation-worship response

> There remaineth therefore a rest to the people of God.

**Context:** Hebrews 4:1-11 discusses the 'rest' that remains for God's people. The word sabbatismos (G4520, hapax legomenon) specifically means Sabbath-keeping.

**Notes:** Sabbatismos (hapax) means Sabbath-keeping in every extant Greek source. Lincoln (anti-Sabbatarian) concedes the lexical point. Rev 14:7's creation formula invokes the commandment that memorializes creation — the Sabbath. Hebrews 4:9 confirms Sabbath-keeping remains for God's people.

### Two fears distinguished in a single verse

> And Moses said unto the people, Fear not: for God is come to prove you, and that his fear may be before your faces, that ye sin not.

**Context:** Immediately after the Sinai theophany. Moses distinguishes terror-fear ('Fear not' — do not be terrified) from reverential fear ('that his fear may be before your faces').

**Notes:** Two different aspects of fear in one verse: (1) 'Fear not' = do not be terrified (al-tiru, negated imperative); (2) 'that his fear (yirato, H3374) may be before your faces, that ye sin not' = reverential awe as moral orientation. Rev 14:7's phobethete calls for the second kind — the fear that prevents sin, not the fear that produces dread.

---

### Perfect love casts out kolasis-fear, not reverential awe

> There is no fear in love; but perfect love casteth out fear: because fear hath torment. He that feareth is not made perfect in love.

**Context:** John's discussion of love and fear. The fear love casts out is specifically kolasis-fear — dread of divine punishment.

**Notes:** The fear (phobos) love casts out is specified by the word kolasis (G2851, punishment/torment — only 2 NT occurrences). This is servile terror, not reverential awe. 1 John 4:18 does NOT contradict Rev 14:7. The first angel calls for reverential fear (the yirah tradition), not the kolasis-fear that love casts out.

### New song + creation by word + fear of the LORD — convergence of elements

> Rejoice in the LORD, O ye righteous: [for] praise is comely for the upright. Praise the LORD with harp: sing unto him with the psaltery [and] an instrument of ten strings. Sing unto him a new song; play skilfully with a loud noise. For the word of the LORD [is] right; and all his works [are done] in truth. ### The Lamb's secured victory — immediate literary context preceding the messages

### Dual harvest — literary context following the messages

> And I looked, and behold a white cloud, and upon the cloud [one] sat like unto the Son of man, having on his head a golden crown, and in his hand a sharp sickle. And the winepress was trodden without the city, and blood came out of the winepress, even unto the horse bridles, by the space of a thousand [and] six hundred furlongs.

**Context:** The dual harvest immediately following the three angels' messages. Two harvests: grain (positive) and grapes (judgment). The messages prepare for this separation.

**Notes:** The three angels' messages are positioned between secured victory (14:1-5) and final harvest (14:14-20). The messages are the call that determines which harvest one belongs to.

### Creation vs. idolatry — the LORD made the heavens

> For all the gods of the nations [are] idols: but the LORD made the heavens.

**Context:** Psalm 96:5 contrasts the worthless idols of the nations with YHWH who made the heavens. The LXX reads: pantes hoi theoi ton ethnon eidola, ho de kyrios tous ouranous epoiesen — using poieo (same verb as Rev 14:7).

**Notes:** LXX uses epoiesen (poieo) for asah — the same verb Rev 14:7 uses (poiesanti). The creation-vs-idolatry antithesis here is the same dynamic as Acts 17:24-29 and Rev 14:7.

**Context:** Paul and Barnabas at Lystra, redirecting worship from themselves to the Creator God. Uses the same creation formula as Rev 14:7.

### Ark of the covenant opened — activating the covenant-law theme

**Context:** The ark of the covenant is revealed in heaven's temple, activating the covenant-law theme that runs through Rev 12:17 to 14:12.

### Sabbath as sign (owth) — first of four sign references

> Speak thou also unto the children of Israel, saying, Verily my sabbaths ye shall keep: for it [is] a sign between me and you throughout your generations; that [ye] may know that I [am] the LORD that doth sanctify you.

**Context:** God designates the Sabbath as a sign (owth) between Himself and Israel. First of four owth references for the Sabbath.

**Notes:** Sabbath as owth (sign, H226) — the only commandment explicitly called a sign. Four times: Exo 31:13, 31:17, Eze 20:12, 20:20.

### proeuangelizomai — God preached the gospel beforehand to Abraham

**Context:** Paul coins proeuangelizomai (hapax) to describe God preaching the gospel to Abraham before the NT era.

### Death threat from the beast — fear of man vs. fear of God

### Heavens declare the glory of God — creation-revelation precedent

> To the chief Musician, A Psalm of David. The heavens declare the glory of God; and the firmament sheweth his handywork. Day unto day uttereth speech, and night unto night sheweth knowledge. [There is] no speech nor language, [where] their voice is not heard. Their line is gone out through all the earth, and their words to the end of the world. In them hath he set a tabernacle for the sun, Which [is] as a bridegroom coming out of his chamber, [and] rejoiceth as a strong man to run a race. His going forth [is] from the end of the heaven, and his circuit unto the ends of it: and there is nothing hid from the heat thereof.

**Context:** David declares that creation itself proclaims God's glory. The heavens are a universal witness requiring no human language.

**Notes:** Creation as glory-proclamation. The heavens declare kabod (H3519) — the same glory the first angel commands humanity to give. Creation's witness is universal (v.4 'through all the earth'), paralleling Rev 14:6's universal scope.

### Climax of Athens sermon — appointed day, judge the world, proof by resurrection

> Because he hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world in righteousness by [that] man whom he hath ordained; [whereof] he hath given assurance unto all [men], in that he hath raised him from the dead.

**Context:** The climax of Paul's Athens sermon. He moves from creation (v.24) through worship correction (vv.25-29) to judgment (v.31), finally introducing Christ through the resurrection.

**Notes:** Temporal shift: mellei krinein ('is about to judge', present of mello + infinitive) vs. Rev 14:7's elthen ('IS come', prophetic aorist). Same message, different temporal phase. Paul delays mentioning Christ until this climax — the resurrection as proof (pistin paraschon). If Paul's speech is gospel with delayed christological naming, Rev 14:7 follows the same pattern.

### God proclaiming His own glory/character — definition of kabod-doxa content

> And the LORD passed by before him, and proclaimed, The LORD, The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth, Keeping mercy for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear [the guilty]; visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children, and upon the children's children, unto the third and to the fourth [generation].

**Context:** God's self-proclamation of His character in response to Moses' request to see His glory (Exo 33:18). This defines what kabod/doxa actually IS.

**Notes:** When Moses asked to see God's glory (kabod, 33:18), God proclaimed His CHARACTER — merciful, gracious, longsuffering, forgiving, yet just. Glory-giving (Rev 14:7 dote doxan) means recognizing THIS character, not merely visible radiance.

### God as judge — he cometh and shall not keep silence

> Our God shall come, and shall not keep silence: a fire shall devour before him, and it shall be very tempestuous round about him. And the heavens shall declare his righteousness: for God [is] judge himself. Selah.

**Context:** A judgment theophany psalm. God comes to judge His own covenant people — not outsiders but those who made covenant with Him.
