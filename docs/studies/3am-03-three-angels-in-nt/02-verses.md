# Verses

## Question
Does the New Testament repeat the same three-part pattern (gospel call, expose false system, warn of judgment) before Revelation 14? Examine: John the Baptist's ministry, Jesus' ministry, Stephen's speech in Acts 7, Paul at Athens (Acts 17:22-31), Paul to Rome (Rom 1-3).

### First angel's message — everlasting gospel, fear God, judgment hour, worship Creator

> And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

**Context:** Revelation 14:1-5 introduces the 144,000 with the Lamb on Mount Sion. The three angels' messages follow (vv.6-12), then the harvest imagery (vv.14-20). The first angel announces the everlasting gospel with universal scope.

**Notes:** Greek parsing shows phobethete (aorist passive imperative), elthen (aorist — judgment IS come, not will come), proskynesate (aorist imperative). The aorist of elthen is critical for the anticipation-vs-announcement distinction.

---

### Second angel's message — Babylon is fallen

> And there followed another angel, saying, Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication.

**Context:** The second angel follows immediately after the first, announcing the fall of the false system. The double 'is fallen, is fallen' echoes Isaiah 21:9.

**Notes:** The second angel exposes the false system — Babylon. This is the structural parallel for the exposure element in each NT episode.

---

### Third angel's message — judgment warning against beast worship

> And the third angel followed them, saying with a loud voice, If any man worship the beast and his image, and receive his mark in his forehead, or in his hand, The same shall drink of the wine of the wrath of God, which is poured out without mixture into the cup of his indignation; and he shall be tormented with fire and brimstone in the presence of the holy angels, and in the presence of the Lamb: And the smoke of their torment ascendeth up for ever and ever: and they have no rest day nor night, who worship the beast and his image, and whosoever receiveth the mark of his name.

**Context:** The third angel delivers the most severe warning in Scripture — the consequences of false worship. Uses thumos (G2372) for divine fury.

**Notes:** thumos tou theou (fury of God) connects to the OT chemah-thumos bridge documented in 3am-02.

---

### Result clause — patience of saints, commandments, faith of Jesus

> Here is the patience of the saints: here are they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus.

**Context:** The result of heeding the three messages — the saints are characterized by commandment-keeping and faith. Structural parallel to Eccl 12:13-14.

**Notes:** This verse functions as the response clause — those who heed the three messages are described by their faithfulness, not just their escape from judgment.

### John the Baptist's gospel call — repent, kingdom at hand, forerunner prophecy

> In those days came John the Baptist, preaching in the wilderness of Judaea, And saying, Repent ye: for the kingdom of heaven is at hand. For this is he that was spoken of by the prophet Esaias, saying, The voice of one crying in the wilderness, Prepare ye the way of the Lord, make his paths straight.

**Context:** Matthew 3 opens John's ministry with his gospel proclamation. The verb kerysso (G2784) describes his activity. Metanoeite is present active imperative — an ongoing command to repent.

**Notes:** Matt 3:2 parsing: metanoeite = present active imperative (ongoing command), eggiken = perfect active indicative of eggizo (G1448) — the kingdom has drawn near and remains near. This is the gospel-call element of John's three-part ministry.

---

### John exposes Pharisees/Sadducees — generation of vipers, axe at root

> But when he saw many of the Pharisees and Sadducees come to his baptism, he said unto them, O generation of vipers, who hath warned you to flee from the wrath to come? Bring forth therefore fruits meet for repentance: And think not to say within yourselves, We have Abraham to our father: for I say unto you, that God is able of these stones to raise up children unto Abraham. And now also the axe is laid unto the root of the trees: therefore every tree which bringeth not forth good fruit is hewn down, and cast into the fire.

**Context:** John's exposure element. He specifically targets the Pharisees' and Sadducees' false security in Abrahamic descent. The phrase 'generation of vipers' (gennemata echidnon) is the exposure formula. Matt 3:10 transitions to judgment.

**Notes:** Matt 3:10 parsing: ede (already) + keitai (present indicative — is laid) shows judgment is imminent, not distant. The axe IS at the root — present tense. ekkoptetai and balletai are also present passive indicatives — fruitless trees ARE being cut down and thrown into fire. This bridges exposure and judgment.

---

### John's judgment warning — baptism with fire, unquenchable fire

> I indeed baptize you with water unto repentance: but he that cometh after me is mightier than I, whose shoes I am not worthy to bear: he shall baptize you with the Holy Ghost, and with fire: Whose fan is in his hand, and he will throughly purge his floor, and gather his wheat into the garner; but he will burn up the chaff with unquenchable fire.

**Context:** John's explicit judgment warning element. The Coming One brings both Spirit and fire — salvation and judgment. The winnowing imagery separates wheat from chaff.

**Notes:** pyr asbestos (unquenchable fire, G4442 + G762) parallels the judgment fire language throughout the OT prophets and Rev 14:10-11. The winnowing fork (ptuon, G4425) is a judgment-separation image.

### Luke's expanded account — exposure and ethical demands

> Then said he to the multitude that came forth to be baptized of him, O generation of vipers, who hath warned you to flee from the wrath to come? Bring forth therefore fruits worthy of repentance, and begin not to say within yourselves, We have Abraham to our father: for I say unto you, That God is able of these stones to raise up children unto Abraham. And now also the axe is laid unto the root of the trees: every tree therefore which bringeth not forth good fruit is hewn down, and cast into the fire. And the people asked him, saying, What shall we do then? He answereth and saith unto them, He that hath two coats, let him impart to him that hath none; and he that hath meat, let him do likewise. Then came also publicans to be baptized, and said unto him, Master, what shall we do? And he said unto them, Exact no more than that which is appointed you. And the soldiers likewise demanded of him, saying, And what shall we do? And he said unto them, Do violence to no man, neither accuse any falsely; and be content with your wages.

**Context:** Luke's version addresses the multitude (not just Pharisees/Sadducees as in Matthew). Luke adds the ethical demands (vv.10-14) — practical outworking of repentance for three groups: people, publicans, soldiers.

**Notes:** Luke broadens the exposure beyond the religious leaders to the entire crowd. The ethical instructions show that John's exposure was not merely theological but practical — exposing specific sins in each group's occupation.

---

### Luke's summary — John's entire message labeled euangelizo

> And as the people were in expectation, and all men mused in their hearts of John, whether he were the Christ, or not; John answered, saying unto them all, I indeed baptize you with water; but one mightier than I cometh, the latchet of whose shoes I am not worthy to unloose: he shall baptize you with the Holy Ghost and with fire: Whose fan is in his hand, and he will throughly purge his floor, and will gather the wheat into his garner; but the chaff he will burn with fire unquenchable. And many other things in his exhortation preached he unto the people.

**Context:** This is the critical summary verse. Luke 3:18 uses euengelizeto (imperfect middle of euangelizo, G2097) to describe John's ENTIRE message — including the judgment warnings. Luke labels all of it gospel.

**Notes:** Luke 3:18 parsing: euengelizeto = imperfect middle indicative of euangelizo (G2097). The imperfect tense indicates continuous/repeated action — John WAS CONTINUALLY preaching good news. The critical point is that Luke labels the totality of John's message (repentance call + exposure + judgment warning) as euangelizo. This parallels Rev 14:6 where the judgment announcement IS the everlasting gospel.

### Jesus warns about scribes and Pharisees — opening exposure

> Then spake Jesus to the multitude, and to his disciples, Saying, The scribes and the Pharisees sit in Moses' seat: All therefore whatsoever they bid you observe, that observe and do; but do not ye after their works: for they say, and do not. For they bind heavy burdens and grievous to be borne, and lay them on men's shoulders; but they themselves will not move them with one of their fingers. But all their works they do for to be seen of men: they make broad their phylacteries, and enlarge the borders of their garments, And love the uppermost rooms at feasts, and the chief seats in the synagogues, And greetings in the markets, and to be called of men, Rabbi, Rabbi. But be not ye called Rabbi: for one is your Master, even Christ; and all ye are brethren. And call no man your father upon the earth: for one is your Father, which is in heaven. Neither be ye called masters: for one is your Master, even Christ. But he that is greatest among you shall be your servant. And whosoever shall exalt himself shall be abased; and he that shall humble himself shall be exalted.

**Context:** Matthew 23 is Jesus' final public discourse in the temple. He addresses the multitude AND his disciples (v.1), warning about the scribes and Pharisees. This section sets up the systematic exposure.

**Notes:** Jesus' opening functions like the initial warning — it describes the false system before the formal denunciation begins. The audience parallel: Jesus speaks to the people about the false system, not to the system's leaders themselves.

### Seven woes — systematic exposure of Pharisaic hypocrisy

> But woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! for ye shut up the kingdom of heaven against men: for ye neither go in yourselves, neither suffer ye them that are entering to go in. Woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! for ye devour widows' houses, and for a pretence make long prayer: therefore ye shall receive the greater damnation. Woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! for ye compass sea and land to make one proselyte, and when he is made, ye make him twofold more the child of hell than yourselves. Woe unto you, ye blind guides, which say, Whosoever shall swear by the temple, it is nothing; but whosoever shall swear by the gold of the temple, he is a debtor! Ye fools and blind: for whether is greater, the gold, or the temple that sanctifieth the gold? And, Whosoever shall swear by the altar, it is nothing; but whosoever sweareth by the gift that is upon it, he is guilty. Ye fools and blind: for whether is greater, the gift, or the altar that sanctifieth the gift? Whoso therefore shall swear by the altar, sweareth by it, and by all things thereon. And whoso shall swear by the temple, sweareth by it, and by him that dwelleth therein. And he that shall swear by heaven, sweareth by the throne of God, and by him that sitteth thereon. Woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! for ye pay tithe of mint and anise and cummin, and have omitted the weightier matters of the law, judgment, mercy, and faith: these ought ye to have done, and not to leave the other undone. Ye blind guides, which strain at a gnat, and swallow a camel. Woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! for ye make clean the outside of the cup and of the platter, but within they are full of extortion and excess. Thou blind Pharisee, cleanse first that which is within the cup and platter, that the outside of them may be clean also. Woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! for ye are like unto whited sepulchres, which indeed appear beautiful outward, but are within full of dead men's bones, and of all uncleanness. Even so ye also outwardly appear righteous unto men, but within ye are full of hypocrisy and iniquity. Woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! because ye build the tombs of the prophets, and garnish the sepulchres of the righteous, And say, If we had been in the days of our fathers, we would not have been partakers with them in the blood of the prophets. Wherefore ye be witnesses unto yourselves, that ye are the children of them which killed the prophets. Fill ye up then the measure of your fathers. Ye serpents, ye generation of vipers, how can ye escape the damnation of hell?

**Context:** The seven woes form the systematic exposure of the false religious system. Each woe targets a specific dimension of hypocrisy. ouai (G3759) appears 7+ times. The section climaxes with 'how can ye escape the damnation (krisis) of hell (geenna)?'

**Notes:** ouai (G3759, 47 occurrences total) is the prophetic denunciation formula. It collocates with hupokrites (G5273, 20 occurrences) 7 times in Matt 23. The woe oracle is an OT prophetic genre (cf. Isa 5 and Hab 2). v.33 uses krisis (judgment/damnation) + geenna — transitioning from exposure to judgment. v.23 explicitly mentions 'the weightier matters of the law, judgment (krisis), mercy, and faith.'

### Judgment warning — damnation of gehenna, blood guilt

> Ye serpents, ye generation of vipers, how can ye escape the damnation of hell? Wherefore, behold, I send unto you prophets, and wise men, and scribes: and some of them ye shall kill and crucify; and some of them shall ye scourge in your synagogues, and persecute them from city to city: That upon you may come all the righteous blood shed upon the earth, from the blood of righteous Abel unto the blood of Zacharias son of Barachias, whom ye slew between the temple and the altar. Verily I say unto you, All these things shall come upon this generation.

**Context:** The transition from exposure to judgment warning. v.33 asks how they can escape the krisis of geenna. vv.34-36 announce that accumulated blood-guilt will come upon this generation.

**Notes:** krisis (G2920) of geenna (G1067) — the judgment vocabulary. 'This generation' (he genea haute) marks the judgment as both corporate and temporally specific.

### Judgment lament — Jerusalem, your house left desolate

> O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, thou that killest the prophets, and stonest them which are sent unto thee, how often would I have gathered thy children together, even as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings, and ye would not! Behold, your house is left unto you desolate. For I say unto you, Ye shall not see me henceforth, till ye shall say, Blessed is he that cometh in the name of the Lord.

**Context:** The lament over Jerusalem. Jesus' emotional cry shows the same tension between warning and compassion found in the OT prophets. 'Your house is left desolate' (eremos, G2048) announces the judgment.

**Notes:** eremos (G2048, desolate) connects to the OT desolation language. 'Your house' may refer to the temple or the nation. The lament parallels Jeremiah's weeping over Jerusalem (Jer 9:1). Cross-testament parallels for Matt 23:37 found connections to Jer 4:11, 2 Chr 21:13 (OT) and Luke 13:34 (synoptic parallel).

### Stephen's gospel history — Abraham called, covenant promise

> And he said, Men, brethren, and fathers, hearken; The God of glory appeared unto our father Abraham, when he was in Mesopotamia, before he dwelt in Charran, And said unto him, Get thee out of thy country, and from thy kindred, and come into the land which I shall shew thee. Then came he out of the land of the Chaldaeans, and dwelt in Charran: and from thence, when his father was dead, he removed him into this land, wherein ye now dwell. And he gave him none inheritance in it, no, not so much as to set his foot on: yet he promised that he would give it to him for a possession, and to his seed after him, when as yet he had no child. And God spake on this wise, That his seed should sojourn in a strange land; and that they should bring them into bondage, and entreat them evil four hundred years. And the nation to whom they shall be in bondage will I judge, said God: and after that shall they come forth, and serve me in this place. And he gave him the covenant of circumcision: and so Abraham begat Isaac, and circumcised him the eighth day; and Isaac begat Jacob; and Jacob begat the twelve patriarchs.

**Context:** Stephen begins his defense by recounting God's initiative with Abraham — the gospel history. God appears, calls, promises. This is the gospel element of Stephen's speech: God's faithful covenant initiative.

**Notes:** Stephen's opening emphasizes God's initiative and faithfulness, not Israel's merit. v.7 already hints at judgment: 'the nation to whom they shall be in bondage will I judge.' The gospel history establishes the standard against which Israel's rejection will be measured.

### Stephen — Joseph rejected then exalted, pattern of rejected savior

> And the patriarchs, moved with envy, sold Joseph into Egypt: but God was with him, And delivered him out of all his afflictions, and gave him favour and wisdom in the sight of Pharaoh king of Egypt; and he made him governor over Egypt and all his house. Now there came a dearth over all the land of Egypt and Chanaan, and great affliction: and our fathers found no sustenance. But when Jacob heard that there was corn in Egypt, he sent out our fathers first. And at the second time Joseph was made known to his brethren; and Joseph's kindred was made known unto Pharaoh. Then sent Joseph, and called his father Jacob to him, and all his kindred, threescore and fifteen souls. So Jacob went down into Egypt, and died, he, and our fathers, And were carried over into Sychem, and laid in the sepulchre that Abraham bought for a sum of money of the sons of Emmor the father of Sychem.

**Context:** The Joseph narrative establishes the rejection-then-exaltation typology. The patriarchs sold Joseph out of envy (v.9), but God was with him and elevated him to save the very brothers who rejected him.

**Notes:** The rejected-then-exalted pattern (Joseph sold, then became governor) is typological of Christ. Stephen is building a case: Israel's pattern is to reject God's chosen deliverers first, then be saved through them.

### Stephen — Moses rejected by Israel then sent as deliverer

> In which time Moses was born, and was exceeding fair, and nourished up in his father's house three months: And when he was cast out, Pharaoh's daughter took him up, and nourished him for her own son. And Moses was learned in all the wisdom of the Egyptians, and was mighty in words and in deeds. And when he was full forty years old, it came into his heart to visit his brethren the children of Israel. And seeing one of them suffer wrong, he defended him, and avenged him that was oppressed, and smote the Egyptian: For he supposed his brethren would have understood how that God by his hand would deliver them: but they understood not. And the next day he shewed himself unto them as they strove, and would have set them at one again, saying, Sirs, ye are brethren; why do ye wrong one to another? But he that did his neighbour wrong thrust him away, saying, Who made thee a ruler and a judge over us? Wilt thou kill me, as thou diddest the Egyptian yesterday? Then fled Moses at this saying, and was a stranger in the land of Madian, where he begat two sons. And when forty years were expired, there appeared to him in the wilderness of mount Sina an angel of the Lord in a flame of fire in a bush. When Moses saw it, he wondered at the sight: and as he drew near to behold it, the voice of the Lord came unto him, Saying, I am the God of thy fathers, the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob. Then Moses trembled, and durst not behold. Then said the Lord to him, Put off thy shoes from thy feet: for the place where thou standest is holy ground. I have seen, I have seen the affliction of my people which is in Egypt, and I have heard their groaning, and am come down to deliver them. And now come, I will send thee into Egypt. This Moses whom they refused, saying, Who made thee a ruler and a judge? the same did God send to be a ruler and a deliverer by the hand of the angel which appeared to him in the bush. He brought them out, after that he had shewed wonders and signs in the land of Egypt, and in the Red sea, and in the wilderness forty years.

**Context:** The Moses cycle repeats the Joseph pattern more explicitly: Israel refuses Moses (v.27, 35), but God sends this same Moses as ruler and deliverer. v.35 emphasizes: 'This Moses whom they refused... the same did God send.'

**Notes:** The repetition of the rejection-exaltation pattern strengthens Stephen's argument: Israel consistently rejected God's chosen deliverers. This builds the exposure element — Israel's pattern of opposing God's messengers.

### Stephen — Israel turned to idolatry despite Moses' ministry

> This is that Moses, which said unto the children of Israel, A prophet shall the Lord your God raise up unto you of your brethren, like unto me; him shall ye hear. This is he, that was in the church in the wilderness with the angel which spake to him in the mount Sina, and with our fathers: who received the lively oracles to give unto us: To whom our fathers would not obey, but thrust him from them, and in their hearts turned back again into Egypt, Saying unto Aaron, Make us gods to go before us: for as for this Moses, which brought us out of the land of Egypt, we wot not what is become of him. And they made a calf in those days, and offered sacrifice unto the idol, and rejoiced in the works of their own hands. Then God turned, and gave them up to worship the host of heaven; as it is written in the book of the prophets, O ye house of Israel, have ye offered to me slain beasts and sacrifices by the space of forty years in the wilderness? Yea, ye took up the tabernacle of Moloch, and the star of your god Remphan, figures which ye made to worship them: and I will carry you away beyond Babylon.

**Context:** Stephen exposes Israel's idolatry despite receiving the living oracles. The golden calf (v.41), worship of host of heaven (v.42), Moloch and Remphan (v.43). The phrase 'God gave them up' (v.42) parallels Romans 1:24,26,28.

**Notes:** eidolon (G1497) in v.41 — 'offered sacrifice unto the idol.' The parallel with Romans 1:24-28 is significant: both Stephen and Paul describe God 'giving up' those who persist in idolatry. v.43 ends with 'beyond Babylon' — the judgment consequence.

### Stephen — temple theology exposed, God not confined to buildings

> Our fathers had the tabernacle of witness in the wilderness, as he had appointed, speaking unto Moses, that he should make it according to the fashion that he had seen. Which also our fathers that came after brought in with Jesus into the possession of the Gentiles, whom God drave out before the face of our fathers, unto the days of David; Who found favour before God, and desired to find a tabernacle for the God of Jacob. But Solomon built him an house. Howbeit the most High dwelleth not in temples made with hands; as saith the prophet, Heaven is my throne, and earth is my footstool: what house will ye build me? saith the Lord: or what is the place of my rest? Hath not my hand made all these things?

**Context:** Stephen's theological exposure: the Most High does not dwell in temples made with hands (cheiropoietos, G5499). This directly challenges the Sanhedrin's temple theology — the very charge against him (Acts 6:13-14).

**Notes:** cheiropoietos (G5499, made with hands) — used in Acts 7:48 and paralleled in Acts 17:24 where Paul says the same about Athenian temples. Stephen and Paul independently expose the same error: God cannot be contained in human structures. This is the specific false-system exposure for Stephen's audience.

### Paul at Athens — Creator God who made the world, does not dwell in temples

> Then Paul stood in the midst of Mars' hill, and said, Ye men of Athens, I perceive that in all things ye are too superstitious. For as I passed by, and beheld your devotions, I found an altar with this inscription, TO THE UNKNOWN GOD. Whom therefore ye ignorantly worship, him declare I unto you. God that made the world and all things therein, seeing that he is Lord of heaven and earth, dwelleth not in temples made with hands; Neither is worshipped with men's hands, as though he needed any thing, seeing he giveth to all life, and breath, and all things;

**Context:** Paul's gospel proclamation at Athens. He begins with creation theology — God made the world (kosmos, G2889) and all things therein. The Creator-worship language parallels Rev 14:7 ('worship him that made heaven, and earth').

**Notes:** Acts 17:24 parallels Rev 14:7 in Creator worship. 'Dwelleth not in temples made with hands' directly echoes Stephen (Acts 7:48). The Parsons chiasm places this as the B element: Creator God as true object of worship.

### Paul at Athens — God's universal sovereignty over nations

> And hath made of one blood all nations of men for to dwell on all the face of the earth, and hath determined the times before appointed, and the bounds of their habitation; That they should seek the Lord, if haply they might feel after him, and find him, though he be not far from every one of us: For in him we live, and move, and have our being; as certain also of your own poets have said, For we are also his offspring.

**Context:** The center of Parsons' chiasm (C element): the relationship between humanity and God. Universal scope — 'all nations' parallels Rev 14:6 'every nation.'

**Notes:** panta ethne (all nations) — ethnos (G1484, 162 occurrences). The universal scope parallels Rev 14:6 'panti ethnei' (every nation). Paul uses the Athenians' own poets to establish common ground before the turn to judgment.

### Paul at Athens — idolatry exposed, judgment announced, repentance commanded

> Forasmuch then as we are the offspring of God, we ought not to think that the Godhead is like unto gold, or silver, or stone, graven by art and man's device. And the times of this ignorance God winked at; but now commandeth all men every where to repent: Because he hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world in righteousness by that man whom he hath ordained; whereof he hath given assurance unto all men, in that he hath raised him from the dead.

**Context:** The climax of Paul's Areopagus sermon. Three movements compressed: (1) idolatry exposure (v.29), (2) repentance command (v.30), (3) judgment announcement (v.31). This is the three-part pattern in three verses.

**Notes:** Critical parsing: hupereidon (G5237, aorist participle — God having overlooked) vs. apangellei (present indicative — now commands) vs. estesen (G2476, aorist indicative — appointed) hemeran (day) vs. mellei (G3195, present — is about to) krinein (judge). The temporal shift: God previously overlooked ignorance (aorist), NOW commands repentance (present), BECAUSE he appointed (aorist) a day of judgment. The krino (G2919) + oikoumene (the inhabited world) parallels the universal scope. estesen hemeran (appointed a day) — aorist: the day is already set, judgment is certain.

### Gospel declaration — power of God unto salvation, righteousness revealed

> For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ: for it is the power of God unto salvation to every one that believeth; to the Jew first, and also to the Greek. For therein is the righteousness of God revealed from faith to faith: as it is written, The just shall live by faith.

**Context:** Romans 1:16-17 is Paul's thesis statement for the entire epistle. euangelion (G2098) is the power (dynamis, G1411) of God unto salvation. This is the gospel-declaration element.

**Notes:** euangelion (G2098, 77 occurrences) + dynamis (G1411, 118 occurrences) — the gospel as God's saving power. Universal scope: 'to every one that believeth; to the Jew first, and also to the Greek' parallels Rev 14:6 'every nation.'

### Gentile corruption exposed — knew God but glorified him not, idolatry

> For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who hold the truth in unrighteousness; Because that which may be known of God is manifest in them; for God hath shewed it unto them. For the invisible things of him from the creation of the world are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made, even his eternal power and Godhead; so that they are without excuse: Because that, when they knew God, they glorified him not as God, neither were thankful; but became vain in their imaginations, and their foolish heart was darkened. Professing themselves to be wise, they became fools, And changed the glory of the uncorruptible God into an image made like to corruptible man, and to birds, and fourfooted beasts, and creeping things.
