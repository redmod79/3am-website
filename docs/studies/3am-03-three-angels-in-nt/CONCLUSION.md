# The Three Angels' Pattern in the New Testament

## Question
Does the New Testament repeat the same three-part pattern (gospel call → expose false system → warn of judgment) before Revelation 14? Examine: John the Baptist's ministry, Jesus' ministry, Stephen's speech in Acts 7, Paul at Athens (Acts 17:22-31), Paul to Rome (Rom 1-3).

## Summary Answer
The three-part pattern of Rev 14:6-12 — gospel call, exposure of a false system, judgment warning — appears in every major NT preaching episode before Revelation. John the Baptist, Jesus, Stephen, and Paul each deliver this same sequence, adapting the exposure element to their specific audience while preserving the structural order. Three independent NT texts (Luke 3:18, Rom 2:16, Rev 14:6-7) explicitly identify the combined message — including the judgment warning — as "gospel." The NT preachers delivered the same pattern as the three angels but in anticipatory mode: judgment was certain and coming. The first angel announces that the anticipated hour has arrived.

## Key Verses

> **John's Entire Message Labeled Gospel (Luke 3:18)** And many other things in his exhortation preached he unto the people.

> **Judgment According to My Gospel (Romans 2:16)** In the day when God shall judge the secrets of men by Jesus Christ according to my gospel.

> **The Everlasting Gospel and Judgment Hour (Revelation 14:6-7)** And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel to preach unto them that dwell on the earth, and to every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people, Saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come: and worship him that made heaven, and earth, and the sea, and the fountains of waters.

> **Compressed Three-Part Pattern (1 Thessalonians 1:9-10)** And how ye turned to God from idols to serve the living and true God; And to wait for his Son from heaven, whom he raised from the dead, even Jesus, which delivered us from the wrath to come.

> **Paul's Judgment Announcement at Athens (Acts 17:30-31)** And the times of this ignorance God winked at; but now commandeth all men every where to repent: Because he hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world in righteousness by that man whom he hath ordained; whereof he hath given assurance unto all men, in that he hath raised him from the dead.

> **Stephen's Climactic Indictment (Acts 7:51-52)** Ye stiffnecked and uncircumcised in heart and ears, ye do always resist the Holy Ghost: as your fathers did, so do ye. Which of the prophets have not your fathers persecuted? and they have slain them which shewed before of the coming of the Just One; of whom ye have been now the betrayers and murderers.

> **Jesus' Judgment Lament (Matthew 23:37-38)** O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, thou that killest the prophets, and stonest them which are sent unto thee, how often would I have gathered thy children together, even as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings, and ye would not! Behold, your house is left unto you desolate.

> **John's Exposure and Judgment Warning (Matthew 3:10, 12)** And now also the axe is laid unto the root of the trees: therefore every tree which bringeth not forth good fruit is hewn down, and cast into the fire... he will burn up the chaff with unquenchable fire.

> **Solomon's Structural Template (Ecclesiastes 12:13-14)** Let us hear the conclusion of the whole matter: Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man. For God shall bring every work into judgment, with every secret thing, whether it be good, or whether it be evil.

## Analysis

### The Baseline: What the Three Angels Actually Say

Before tracing the pattern through NT preaching, the standard must be defined from Revelation 14:6-12 itself. The first angel (vv.6-7) carries the "everlasting gospel" (euangelion aionion) and issues three aorist imperatives: fear God (phobethete, G5399), give glory (dote doxan, G1391), and worship the Creator (proskynesate, G4352). The reason clause — "for the hour of his judgment (krisis, G2920) is come (elthen, aorist)" — ties the gospel proclamation to an arrived judgment. The creation formula echoes Exodus 20:11, grounding the worship call in God's identity as Creator.

The second angel (v.8) announces the fall of the false system: "Babylon is fallen, is fallen." The third angel (vv.9-11) warns of the consequences for choosing the false system over true worship — the undiluted fury (thumos, G2372) of God. The passage concludes with the faithful remnant: "Here is the patience of the saints: here are they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus" (v.12).

The three-part structure is: (1) gospel call to worship the true God, (2) exposure/fall of the false system, (3) judgment warning for those who persist in the false system. The question is whether this structure appears only in Revelation or runs through NT preaching before Revelation was written.

### Episode 1: John the Baptist — The First NT Instance

John's ministry divides naturally into three movements. The gospel call opens with kerysso (G2784, herald/proclaim) and the command metanoeite (G3340, present active imperative — repent, as an ongoing posture): "Repent ye: for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (Matt 3:2). The perfect tense of eggiken (G1448 — has drawn near and remains near) signals that something new has happened in God's activity — the kingdom has arrived and demands response.

The exposure element follows immediately. When Pharisees and Sadducees come to his baptism, John addresses them directly: "O generation of vipers, who hath warned you to flee from the wrath to come?" (Matt 3:7). Then the specific false system is named: "think not to say within yourselves, We have Abraham to our father" (Matt 3:9). The "Babylon" for John's audience was inherited covenant privilege — the assumption that Abrahamic descent guarantees safety before God. John dismantles this by pointing to God's sovereignty: "God is able of these stones to raise up children unto Abraham." The false security is exposed as empty.

The judgment warning completes the pattern: "And now also the axe is laid unto the root of the trees" (Matt 3:10). The Greek parsing reveals the urgency: ede (already) + keitai (present indicative — IS laid) + ekkoptetai (present passive — IS being cut down) + balletai (present passive — IS being thrown into fire). The present tenses show imminent, ongoing judgment, not a distant threat. The unquenchable fire of Matt 3:12 (pyr asbestos) parallels the third angel's language of inescapable judgment.

The critical verse is Luke 3:18, where Luke provides the interpretive label for the entire message. After recording the exposure ("generation of vipers"), the ethical demands (Luke 3:10-14), and the judgment warnings (unquenchable fire, winnowing fork), Luke summarizes: "and many other things in his exhortation preached he (euengelizeto) unto the people." The verb is the imperfect middle of euangelizo (G2097) — John WAS CONTINUALLY preaching good news. Luke labels the totality of John's message — exposure and judgment included — as gospel. This parallels Rev 14:6 where the judgment announcement IS the everlasting gospel. The NT itself categorizes the three-part message as a single gospel proclamation.

Luke's account also extends the exposure beyond religious leaders. John confronts Herod about Herodias and "all the evils which Herod had done" (Luke 3:19). The exposure element reaches political corruption as well as religious presumption — the false system is not merely theological but institutional.

### Episode 2: Jesus in Matthew 23 — The Seven Woes

Jesus' final public discourse in the temple follows the same three-part sequence at an expanded scale. The opening section (Matt 23:1-12) addresses the multitude and disciples about the scribes and Pharisees. Jesus describes the false system — heavy burdens on others, works done for human approval, love of titles and prominence — and presents the kingdom alternative: "he that is greatest among you shall be your servant" (v.11). The gospel element is the kingdom vision of service-based rather than hierarchy-based community.

The exposure element is the most detailed in any single NT passage. The seven woes (ouai, G3759) systematically dismantle the Pharisaic system. Each woe targets a specific dimension of hypocrisy (hupokrites, G5273 — an actor playing a role). The false system is theatrical religion: shutting up the kingdom while claiming to open it (v.13), making converts worse than the converters (v.15), manipulating oath casuistry (vv.16-22), tithing herbs while neglecting "the weightier matters of the law, judgment (krisis), mercy, and faith" (v.23), cleaning the outside of the cup while leaving the inside full of corruption (vv.25-26), appearing righteous while full of hypocrisy (vv.27-28), building prophets' tombs while sharing the nature of those who killed the prophets (vv.29-31).

The climax transitions from exposure to judgment: "Ye serpents, ye generation of vipers, how can ye escape the damnation (krisis, G2920) of hell (geenna, G1067)?" (v.33). The judgment warning continues with accumulated blood guilt (vv.34-36) and the lament over Jerusalem: "Behold, your house is left unto you desolate" (v.38, eremos, G2048). The lament reveals the emotional dimension of the three-part pattern: God warns, exposes, announces judgment — and mourns when the warning is refused. Jesus' cry — "how often would I have gathered thy children together... and ye would not!" — parallels Jeremiah's weeping over the same city (Jer 9:1).

### Episode 3: Stephen's Speech — Covenant Lawsuit in Narrative Form

Stephen's speech in Acts 7 is the longest sermon recorded in Acts and follows the three-part pattern through historical narrative rather than direct proclamation.

The gospel element spans vv.2-43 — not as explicit preaching but as gospel history. Stephen recounts God's faithful initiative: calling Abraham (vv.2-8), preserving Joseph (vv.9-16), raising up Moses (vv.20-36), giving the "lively oracles" (vv.37-38). The rejected-deliverer typology runs through this section: the patriarchs sold Joseph out of envy, but God elevated him to save the very brothers who rejected him (v.9). Israel refused Moses — "Who made thee a ruler and a judge over us?" (v.27) — but God sent this same Moses as ruler and deliverer (v.35). The repeated pattern (rejection → God's intervention → eventual acceptance) is the gospel in narrative form: God saves through the very messengers His people reject.

The exposure element becomes explicit in vv.44-50. Stephen targets the specific false system of his accusers — temple theology. He quotes Isaiah 66:1-2: "the most High dwelleth not in temples made with hands (cheiropoietos, G5499)" (v.48). This directly answers the charge against him (Acts 6:13-14) by showing the temple was never meant to contain God. The exposure is devastating not because Stephen rejects the temple but because he shows the Sanhedrin has made the temple into something it was never designed to be — a system of false security replacing genuine relationship with God.

The verbal link between Acts 7:48 and Acts 17:24, where Paul makes the identical argument using the identical word (cheiropoietos) to a completely different audience (Athenian philosophers), demonstrates that the exposure element targets a universal human tendency. Two speakers, two continents, two audiences, one word, one argument: God cannot be contained in human structures.

The judgment element climaxes in vv.51-53. Stephen uses sklerotrochelos (G4644, stiff-necked) — a hapax in the NT but used 5 times in the LXX for Israel's collective resistance (Exo 33:3, 33:5, 34:9; Deut 9:6, 9:13). By choosing this Deuteronomic covenant term, Stephen invokes the formal covenant lawsuit (riyb) tradition documented in 3am-02. antipipto (G496, "ye do always resist") is present active indicative — the resistance is continuous, ongoing, a permanent posture. The indictment is precise: stiff-necked, uncircumcised in heart and ears, always resisting the Holy Spirit, persecuting every prophet, betraying and murdering the Just One, receiving the law but not keeping it.

### Episode 4: Paul at Athens — The Most Compressed Instance

Paul's Areopagus sermon (Acts 17:22-31) contains the tightest correspondence with Rev 14:6-7 of any NT passage. Parsons identifies a chiastic structure: A (ignorance, vv.22-23) / B (Creator, vv.24-25) / C (humanity-God, vv.26-28) / B' (idols exposed, v.29) / A' (judgment, vv.30-31). The chiasm places Creator theology at its center — matching the first angel's central concern.

The gospel element is Creator worship: "God that made the world and all things therein, seeing that he is Lord of heaven and earth" (v.24). This parallels Rev 14:7 verbally: both use poieo (G4160) for God's creative act, both identify God as Lord of heaven and earth, both present Creator identity as the ground for worship.

The universal scope appears in v.26 — "hath made of one blood all nations (panta ethne, G1484)" — matching Rev 14:6's "every nation" (panti ethnei). Paul establishes that God's sovereignty reaches every people, which grounds the universal judgment announcement.

The exposure in v.29 is brief but precise: "we ought not to think that the Godhead is like unto gold, or silver, or stone, graven by art and man's device." The false system is philosophical idolatry — worshipping a crafted image rather than the living Creator. Parsons describes this as worshipping a "what" instead of a "whom."

The judgment announcement in vv.30-31 shows a critical temporal shift. hupereidon (G5237, aorist participle — "having overlooked") marks the past era of divine patience. apangellei (G518, present active indicative — "now commands") marks the present demand. metanoein (G3340, present infinitive — "to repent") is commanded of all people everywhere. Then the judgment: estesen (G2476, aorist — he appointed) hemeran (G2250, a day) in which he mellei (G3195, present — is about to) krinein (G2919, judge) the oikoumene (inhabited world) in dikaiosyne (righteousness). The day is already appointed (aorist), the judgment is imminent (present).

This passage is the bridge between NT preaching and the first angel. The elements are identical; only the temporal frame differs. Paul says God "appointed a day" (future reference); the first angel says "the hour IS come" (arrived). The pattern is the same; the moment of fulfillment is different.

### Episode 5: Paul in Romans — The Systematic Argument

Romans 1-3 presents the three-part pattern in its most systematic form, developed as a theological argument rather than a sermon.

The gospel declaration (Rom 1:16-17) is Paul's thesis: "the gospel of Christ... is the power (dynamis, G1411) of God unto salvation to every one that believeth; to the Jew first, and also to the Greek." Universal scope, divine power, faith as the response. The righteousness of God is apokalyptetai (present passive — is being revealed) — ongoing revelation.

The exposure of Gentile corruption (1:18-32) follows immediately. The same verb apokalyptetai describes both the revelation of God's righteousness (v.17) and the revelation of God's wrath (v.18) — they share a single disclosure. The degenerative chain traces the fall: knew God → glorified him not → became fools → changed the glory (doxa, G1391) of the incorruptible God into images → God gave them up (paredoken, G3860). The glory exchange in v.23 is the exact antiparallel of Rev 14:7: where the first angel commands "give glory (doxa) to him," Rom 1:23 describes exchanging God's glory for corruptible images.

The triple "God gave them up" (vv.24, 26, 28) parallels Acts 7:42 where Stephen uses the same verb for God giving Israel up to worship the host of heaven. Two independent NT speakers — Stephen and Paul — describe the same pattern using the same word.

The exposure of Jewish corruption (Rom 2:1-11) turns the same lens on the religious person: "thou that judgest doest the same things" (v.1). Paul names the specific false security: "despisest thou the riches of his goodness... not knowing that the goodness of God leadeth thee to repentance (metanoia, G3341)?" (v.4). Then the judgment: "after thy hardness and impenitent heart treasurest up unto thyself wrath (orge, G3709) against the day of wrath (hemera orges)" (v.5). The same pattern as John confronting the Pharisees and Jesus in the seven woes: religious people who condemn others while failing the same standard.

The capstone verse is Rom 2:16: "in the day when God shall judge (krinei, G2919) the secrets of men by Jesus Christ according to my gospel (kata to euangelion mou)." Judgment happens "according to" Paul's gospel. This is not judgment appended to the gospel or judgment separate from it — judgment IS the gospel's judicial function. Together with Luke 3:18 (John's judgment preaching labeled euangelizo) and Rev 14:6-7 (judgment announcement IS the everlasting gospel), Rom 2:16 forms a triple witness that judgment is integral to the gospel.

The universal verdict (Rom 3:9-20) completes the pattern: "we have before proved both Jews and Gentiles, that they are all under sin" (v.9). The catena of OT quotations (vv.10-18, drawing from Psalms, Isaiah, and Proverbs) functions like a covenant lawsuit evidence presentation. The verdict: "every mouth stopped, and all the world... guilty before God" (v.19). v.18 — "there is no fear of God before their eyes" — inverts the first angel's opening command: "Fear God." The universal human condition is the absence of what the gospel commands.

### The Dodd Kerygma and the Three-Part Structure

C.H. Dodd identified six elements in the apostolic kerygma (core preaching pattern) from Peter's speeches in Acts: (1) prophecy fulfillment, (2) Jesus' ministry, death, and resurrection, (3) exaltation, (4) Holy Spirit evidence, (5) future consummation and judgment, (6) call to repentance. These six elements can be grouped as three: Gospel (elements 1-4 — the good news of what God has done), Exposure (implicit — the contrast between God's saving acts and the hearers' rejection), and Judgment (elements 5-6 — consummation and repentance because judgment comes).

Peter's Pentecost sermon (Acts 2:14-40) confirms this grouping: gospel proclamation (vv.14-36 — prophecy fulfilled, Jesus approved, crucified, risen, exalted), exposure ("ye have taken, and by wicked hands have crucified," v.23; "whom ye have crucified," v.36), and judgment/repentance call ("Repent," v.38; "Save yourselves from this untoward generation," v.40). Peter's temple sermon (Acts 3:12-26) follows the same pattern: gospel (God glorified Jesus, raised him), exposure ("ye delivered up and denied the Holy One," vv.13-15), and judgment ("every soul which will not hear that prophet shall be destroyed," v.23). Paul at Antioch (Acts 13:16-41) repeats it again: gospel history (vv.16-25), exposure of ignorant condemnation (vv.27-29), judgment warning from Habakkuk 1:5 ("Beware, ye despisers... perish," vv.40-41).

Paul claims the kerygma is consistent: "Whether it were I or they, so we preach (keryssomen, G2784), and so ye believed" (1 Cor 15:11). The three-part pattern is not Paul's innovation or Peter's style — it is the shared apostolic message.

### The Anticipation-vs-Announcement Distinction

The temporal framework from Andrews resolves the relationship between the NT instances and the three angels' messages. The NT preachers delivered the same three-part pattern, but in anticipatory mode. The Greek evidence is precise:

- **Acts 17:31:** estesen hemeran... mellei krinein — "he appointed a day... he is about to judge." The day is set (aorist — already appointed) but the judgment is still approaching (mellei, present — about to).
- **Acts 24:25:** krima to mellon — "the judgment to come." Future participle. Judgment is coming but has not arrived.
- **Rev 14:7:** elthen he hora tes kriseos autou — "the hour of his judgment IS come." Aorist — the hour has arrived.

The shift from mellei/mellon (about to, coming) to elthen (IS come) marks the difference between anticipation and announcement. The NT preachers knew judgment was certain and appointed; they preached it as future. The first angel announces that the appointed hour has arrived.

Paul himself was aware of this temporal gap. In 2 Thess 2:3-4, he explicitly warns that the day of Christ cannot come until "a falling away first, and that man of sin be revealed." The apostles knew the final judgment was reserved for a future period. Daniel's prophecies — sealed "until the time of the end" (Dan 8:17, 12:4) — explain why: the specific judgment-hour announcement could not be made until the sealed prophecies were opened. The NT instances are the pattern; Rev 14:6-12 is the fulfillment.

### The Elijah Chain: Three Messengers, One Pattern

The Elijah chain provides the typological backbone connecting these instances across time. Malachi prophesied: "I will send you Elijah the prophet before the coming of the great and dreadful day of the LORD" (Mal 4:5). Jesus identified John as the fulfillment: "this is Elias, which was for to come" (Matt 11:14). Gabriel announced John's mission in the language of Malachi: "he shall go before him in the spirit and power (en pneumati kai dynamei, G4151 + G1411) of Elias, to turn the hearts of the fathers to the children" (Luke 1:17).

The pattern runs: historical Elijah at Carmel (1 Ki 18) delivered the three-part message — worship call ("if the LORD be God, follow him," 18:21), exposure (Baal's failure, 18:26-29), and judgment (fire from heaven, execution of false prophets, 18:38-40). John the Baptist repeated it — gospel call ("Repent, kingdom at hand," Matt 3:2), exposure ("generation of vipers," false Abrahamic security, 3:7-9), and judgment (axe at root, unquenchable fire, 3:10-12). The three angels complete the chain — everlasting gospel (14:6-7), Babylon fallen (14:8), wrath of God (14:9-11).

The chain is not merely structural but vocational. EGW draws the explicit connection: "As John the Baptist, in preparing a people for Christ's first advent, called their attention to the Ten Commandments, so we are to give, with no uncertain sound, the message: 'Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come'" (Mar 22.6). This claim can be tested against the biblical text. Does John explicitly reference commandments? Luke 3:10-14 shows John issuing specific ethical directives tailored to each group — practical commandment applications. The parallel holds in substance if not in exact vocabulary.

### The Exposure Element as Audience-Adapted Second Angel

The most consistent feature of the pattern across all five episodes is the exposure element's adaptation to the audience's specific false system:

- **John** exposes Pharisaic presumption — the false security of inherited covenant membership (Matt 3:9, "think not to say, We have Abraham to our father")
- **Jesus** exposes scribal hypocrisy — outward religion concealing inward corruption (Matt 23:27, "whited sepulchres... full of dead men's bones")
- **Stephen** exposes temple theology — the belief that God is contained in human structures (Acts 7:48, "the most High dwelleth not in temples made with hands") and the pattern of rejecting God's messengers (7:51-52)
- **Paul at Athens** exposes philosophical idolatry — representing the divine as crafted material (Acts 17:29, "the Godhead is not like unto gold, or silver, or stone")
- **Paul in Romans** exposes both Gentile idolatry (1:23, "changed the glory of the incorruptible God into an image") and Jewish hypocrisy (2:1, "thou that judgest doest the same things")

The common thread is false security. Each audience trusts a system — covenant membership, religious performance, temple presence, philosophical sophistication, moral superiority — instead of God. The second angel announces "Babylon is fallen" — the collapse of the false system. In every NT episode, the preacher names the specific "Babylon" relevant to the hearers. The exposure adapts; the structure persists.

### Synthesis with the OT Study

This study completes the argument begun in 3am-02. The OT study proved the three-part pattern runs through six episodes: Noah (warning before the flood), Sodom (angels warning Lot), Egypt (plagues exposing false gods), Carmel (Elijah confronting Baal worship), Josiah (reform exposing institutional corruption), and Jeremiah (calling Judah out of Babylon). The vocabulary chains — riyb→krisis, shachah→proskyneo, yatsa→exerchomai, chemah→thumos — trace the OT terms directly into the Greek of Revelation through the LXX.

The NT study adds five more episodes (John, Jesus, Stephen, Paul at Athens, Paul in Romans) plus three additional instances (Peter at Pentecost, Paul at Antioch, Paul before Felix) and one compressed summary (1 Thess 1:9-10). The vocabulary chains continue into the NT: euangelizo bridges basar (OT good tidings), krisis bridges riyb (covenant lawsuit), proskyneo bridges shachah (worship), and Stephen's sklerotrochelos (Deut 9:6 LXX) explicitly invokes the covenant lawsuit tradition in the NT.

The combined evidence from both testaments establishes that the three-part pattern is not an invention of Revelation but the standard mode of divine communication before judgment throughout Scripture. The "everlasting gospel" (euangelion aionion) of Rev 14:6 is genuinely everlasting — it is the same message God has always sent. What changes across the biblical timeline is not the pattern but the scope: from localized episodes (Noah's generation, Sodom) through national crises (Egypt, Carmel, Josiah, Jeremiah) to universal apostolic preaching (John, Jesus, the apostles) to the final universal announcement (the three angels).

## Word Studies

**euangelizo (G2097, 61 occurrences)** — The verb connecting all instances. Luke 3:18 uses the imperfect middle to label John's entire three-part message as gospel. Rev 14:6 uses the infinitive to describe the first angel's mission. The basar→euangelizo bridge through the LXX means the gospel vocabulary traces to Isaiah's herald of good tidings in the Babylon-deliverance context (Isa 52:7, quoted by Paul in Rom 10:15).

**krisis (G2920, 47 occurrences)** — The judgment term appearing in Rev 14:7 (hora tes kriseos), Matt 23:33 (krisis of geenna), and throughout the NT judgment passages. The riyb→krisis bridge means this term carries the weight of the entire OT covenant lawsuit tradition.

**ouai (G3759, 47 occurrences)** — The prophetic denunciation formula. Jesus uses it 7 times in Matt 23 against the scribes and Pharisees. The OT woe-oracle genre (Isa 5, Hab 2) provides the precedent. It collocates with hupokrites (G5273, 20 occurrences) — the theatrical metaphor for performed religion.

**sklerotrochelos (G4644, 1 occurrence)** — Stephen's hapax. A compound meaning "stiff-necked," used 5 times in the LXX for Israel's collective resistance (Deut 9:6, etc.). Stephen's choice of this Deuteronomic term invokes the formal covenant lawsuit tradition.

**hora (G5610, 108 occurrences)** — The temporal term in Rev 14:7 (hora tes kriseos — hour of his judgment). The distinction between hemera (day, G2250, used in Acts 17:31) and hora (hour, used in Rev 14:7) encodes the anticipation-vs-announcement framework: the NT preachers announced a coming day; the first angel announces an arrived hour.

## Difficult Passages

**The naturalism objection — is the three-part division imposed?** Any speech can be divided into three parts after the fact. But the pattern survives multiple independent tests: it appears across uncoordinated speakers, it survives compression (1 Thess 1:9-10), Luke explicitly labels all three elements together as gospel (Luke 3:18), and the OT precedent (3am-02) establishes it independently. Imposed patterns do not produce this kind of cross-author, cross-testament consistency.

**Campbell's prosopopoeia theory (Rom 1:18-32).** If Paul is presenting an opponent's views rather than his own, the exposure section loses authorial weight. However, the three-part structural movement remains regardless of rhetorical strategy. Paul chose to organize his argument as gospel → exposure → judgment. The pattern is in the architecture even if the exposure section is speech-in-character.

**Stephen's speech lacks an explicit gospel proclamation.** The gospel element in Acts 7 is in narrative form — God's faithful initiative with Abraham, Joseph, and Moses — rather than direct declaration. The rejected-deliverer typology (Joseph sold then exalted, Moses refused then sent) IS the gospel in narrative mode: God saves through the very messengers His people reject. The gospel is implicit but structurally foundational — without it, the indictment of vv.51-53 lacks its moral ground.

**The anticipation-vs-announcement distinction.** The NT preachers used future-oriented language (mellei, mellon), while Rev 14:7 uses the aorist elthen. But Acts 17:31 also uses the aorist estesen (appointed) — the day is already set. The temporal distinction is real but graduated: the NT preachers knew the judgment was certain and appointed, they simply could not name the hour. Daniel's sealed prophecies (Dan 8:17, 12:4) explain the gap.

## Conclusion

The three-part pattern — gospel call, exposure of false system, judgment warning — runs through every major NT preaching episode. John the Baptist, Jesus, Stephen, and Paul each deliver this sequence independently, adapting the exposure element to their specific audience while preserving the structural order. The pattern appears in full-length sermons (Acts 7, Acts 17, Romans 1-3), in concentrated discourses (Matt 23), and in compressed summaries (1 Thess 1:9-10, Acts 24:25). Three independent texts — Luke 3:18, Rom 2:16, and Rev 14:6-7 — explicitly identify the combined message, judgment included, as gospel.

The Dodd kerygma analysis confirms that the pattern was not one preacher's style but the standard apostolic message. Paul claims continuity: "Whether it were I or they, so we preach" (1 Cor 15:11). The exposure element adapts to each audience (Pharisaic presumption for John, scribal hypocrisy for Jesus, temple theology for Stephen, philosophical idolatry for Athens, Gentile and Jewish corruption for Romans) while maintaining the same structural position between gospel and judgment. The common target is false security — the system each audience trusts instead of God.

The anticipation-vs-announcement distinction from Andrews provides the interpretive framework. The Greek tenses support it: Acts 17:31 uses mellei krinein (about to judge, future), while Rev 14:7 uses elthen (IS come, arrived). Daniel's sealed prophecies (Dan 8:17, 12:4) explain why the specific announcement was reserved: the hour could not be declared until the sealed time was reached. The NT instances are the pattern; the three angels' messages are the fulfillment.

Combined with 3am-02 (the OT study), the evidence now spans both testaments. The vocabulary chains (riyb→krisis, shachah→proskyneo, basar→euangelizo, chemah→thumos, yatsa→exerchomai) trace through the LXX from the OT prophets to the NT preachers to the Apocalypse. The "everlasting gospel" of Rev 14:6 is not a new message but the message God has always delivered before acting in judgment — the same pattern Noah preached, Moses enacted, Elijah confronted, Jeremiah proclaimed, John heralded, Jesus declared, Stephen testified, and Paul reasoned. What Revelation adds is not the pattern but the announcement: the hour of its final, universal fulfillment has come.
